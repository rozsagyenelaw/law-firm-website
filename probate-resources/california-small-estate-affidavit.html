California Small Estate Affidavit: Skip Probate Guide

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 10 min read

If your loved one passed away with a small estate in California, you may be able to avoid the lengthy and expensive probate process entirely. California's Small Estate Affidavit allows you to collect assets without going to court—saving thousands of dollars and months of time.

In California, estates worth less than $184,500 (as of 2024) can be transferred using a simple affidavit instead of formal probate. This streamlined process takes as little as 40 days and costs only a few hundred dollars instead of several thousand.

This complete guide explains how California's small estate procedures work, whether you qualify, and exactly how to use an affidavit to collect estate assets.

What Is a Small Estate Affidavit in California?

A Small Estate Affidavit (officially called an "Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property" or "Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value") is a sworn legal document that allows heirs to collect a deceased person's assets without opening a formal probate case.

California Probate Code Section 13100-13116 provides this simplified procedure for estates that meet specific value and timing requirements. Instead of appointing an executor and going through court proceedings, successors simply present the affidavit to banks, financial institutions, or other asset holders to claim the property.

Small Estate Value Limits in California (2025)

The small estate threshold in California depends on the type of property:

Personal Property (Bank Accounts, Vehicles, etc.): $184,500

If the total value of personal property (excluding real estate and assets that pass outside probate) is $184,500 or less, you can use Form DE-305 (Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property).

Real Property (Real Estate): $61,500

California real estate valued at $61,500 or less can be transferred using Form DE-305 (Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value). Note: This limit is much lower than for personal property.

Important: These thresholds are adjusted periodically for inflation. Always check the current limits when filing.

What Counts Toward the $184,500 Limit?

Only certain assets count toward the small estate limit. Here's what's included and excluded:

Assets That Count Toward the Limit:

  • Bank accounts and credit union accounts
  • Investment accounts (stocks, bonds, mutual funds)
  • Vehicles, boats, and RVs
  • Personal property (jewelry, furniture, collectibles)
  • Wages or salary owed to the deceased
  • Business interests (partnerships, LLC interests)

Assets That DON'T Count (Pass Outside Probate):

  • Property held in a living trust
  • Jointly owned property with right of survivorship
  • Accounts with payable-on-death (POD) beneficiaries
  • Life insurance proceeds with named beneficiaries
  • Retirement accounts (IRA, 401k) with beneficiaries
  • Transfer-on-death (TOD) securities
  • Community property going to surviving spouse

Key Point: Many families discover that even though the deceased's total assets were substantial, most passed outside probate through beneficiary designations, leaving the probate estate well under $184,500.

Eligibility Requirements for California Small Estate Affidavit

To use a Small Estate Affidavit, you must meet ALL of these requirements:

1. Asset Value Under $184,500

The gross value of personal property subject to probate must be $184,500 or less.

2. 40-Day Waiting Period

You must wait at least 40 days after the person's death before filing the affidavit. This gives creditors time to come forward and ensures no one else has filed for probate.

3. No Probate Case Filed

No petition for probate can have been filed or granted. If someone has already opened a probate case, you cannot use the small estate procedure.

4. You Must Be Entitled to the Property

You must be a legal heir or beneficiary entitled to receive the property under the will or California intestacy law.

Step-by-Step: How to File a Small Estate Affidavit

Step 1: Wait 40 Days After Death

California law requires a mandatory 40-day waiting period after the date of death. Use this time to:

  • Obtain multiple certified copies of the death certificate (usually 6-10)
  • Identify all estate assets and their values
  • Locate the will (if one exists)
  • Identify all legal heirs and beneficiaries

Step 2: Determine Total Estate Value

Calculate the total value of assets subject to probate. Remember to exclude anything that passes outside probate (joint tenancy property, beneficiary accounts, trust property, etc.).

If the total exceeds $184,500, you'll need to pursue formal probate instead.

Step 3: Complete the Appropriate Affidavit Form

California has different forms for different assets:

Form DE-305: Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property

Use this for bank accounts, vehicles, securities, and other personal property under $184,500.

Form DE-310: Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value

Use this for real estate valued at $61,500 or less.

These forms are available free from California courts or online at www.courts.ca.gov.

Step 4: Fill Out the Affidavit

The affidavit requires specific information:

  • Deceased person's full name and date of death
  • Deceased's county of residence
  • Description of the property you're claiming
  • Current value of the property
  • Your relationship to the deceased
  • Statement that you're entitled to the property under the will or by law
  • List of all other heirs and their addresses
  • Statement that at least 40 days have passed since death
  • Statement that no probate proceeding is pending
  • List of all estate debts you're aware of

Step 5: Sign Before a Notary

The affidavit must be signed under penalty of perjury and notarized. Bring valid photo ID to the notary appointment.

Step 6: Present the Affidavit to Asset Holders

Take the notarized affidavit and a certified copy of the death certificate to each institution holding estate assets:

  • Banks and credit unions
  • Brokerage firms
  • The California DMV (for vehicles)
  • Employers (for final wages)
  • Other asset holders

The institution must release the property to you within a reasonable time—typically 10-30 days.

Step 7: Pay Estate Debts

You're legally obligated to use estate assets to pay the deceased's debts before keeping any inheritance. Priority debts include:

  1. Funeral and burial expenses
  2. Medical expenses from final illness
  3. Secured debts (mortgages, car loans)
  4. Taxes
  5. Other unsecured debts

Step 8: Distribute Remaining Assets

After paying debts, distribute remaining assets according to the will or California intestacy law.

Real Estate and Small Estates

California has a separate, lower threshold for real estate: $61,500. If the deceased owned real property valued at $61,500 or less, you can use Form DE-310 to transfer title.

The process is similar:

  • Wait 40 days after death
  • Complete Form DE-310 (Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value)
  • Get the form notarized
  • Record the affidavit with the county recorder where the property is located
  • Attach a certified copy of the death certificate

Once recorded, legal title to the property transfers to the heirs.

Important: Most California real estate exceeds $61,500, making this option unavailable for many families. If the property is worth more, you'll need formal probate or another transfer method.

Transferring Vehicles with Small Estate Affidavit

California has a specific process for transferring vehicles from a deceased owner:

For vehicles worth under $184,500:

  • Wait 40 days after death
  • Complete Form DE-305 (Small Estate Affidavit)
  • Take the form, death certificate, and vehicle title to the DMV
  • Complete Form REG 5 (Affidavit for Transfer Without Probate)
  • Pay transfer fees

The DMV will issue a new title in the heir's name.

What If Someone Refuses to Accept the Affidavit?

California law requires asset holders to accept a properly completed affidavit and release the property. However, institutions sometimes refuse due to:

  • Internal policies requiring court orders
  • Concerns about liability
  • Disputes among heirs
  • Unclear property ownership

If an institution refuses your affidavit, you have several options:

  1. Ask to speak with a manager or legal department
  2. Provide additional documentation (will, proof of heirship)
  3. Have an attorney send a demand letter
  4. File a formal probate case as a last resort

Small Estate Affidavit vs. Spousal Property Petition

California surviving spouses have a third option: the Spousal Property Petition (Form DE-221). This allows a spouse to transfer all community property and some separate property without full probate, regardless of value.

Spouses should consider this option instead of the small estate affidavit if:

  • The estate exceeds $184,500
  • Real estate is involved
  • They want clear legal documentation of the transfer

Pros and Cons of Small Estate Affidavit

Advantages:

  • Fast: Can be completed in 40-60 days vs. 9-18 months for probate
  • Inexpensive: Costs $50-300 vs. $5,000-15,000+ for probate
  • Simple: No court appearances or attorney required
  • Private: Doesn't become public record like probate
  • Efficient: Each heir can file separately for their share

Disadvantages:

  • Limited protection: No court oversight means higher risk of disputes
  • Personal liability: You're personally responsible for paying debts
  • Value cap: Doesn't work for estates over $184,500
  • No Letters: You don't receive Letters Testamentary proving authority
  • Some institutions hesitate: Not all asset holders readily accept affidavits

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Filing Before 40 Days

The law requires a 40-day waiting period. An affidavit filed too early is invalid.

2. Miscalculating Estate Value

Carefully exclude assets that pass outside probate. Many people incorrectly include trust property or beneficiary accounts.

3. Forgetting to Pay Debts

You're legally required to use estate funds to pay the deceased's debts. Keeping assets without paying debts can result in personal liability.

4. Not Checking for Pending Probate

Before filing an affidavit, verify no one else has filed for probate. Check with the superior court in the deceased's county of residence.

5. Incomplete or Inaccurate Information

Asset holders can reject affidavits with errors, omissions, or inconsistencies. Double-check all information before notarizing.

What If the Estate Is Slightly Over $184,500?

If the estate value is close to the limit, consider:

  • Pay debts first: Funeral expenses, final medical bills, and other debts reduce the estate value. After paying these, you might fall under $184,500.
  • Check valuations: Have assets professionally appraised. You might discover values are lower than estimated.
  • Split assets: Some assets might pass outside probate with beneficiary designations, lowering the probate estate value.

If you're still over the limit, you'll need formal probate—but consider whether Independent Administration (IAEA) can streamline the process.

Do You Need an Attorney?

The small estate affidavit process is designed for people to complete without an attorney. However, consider hiring legal help if:

  • You're unsure whether the estate qualifies
  • There are disputes among heirs
  • Asset holders refuse to accept the affidavit
  • The deceased had significant debts
  • There's no will and intestacy law is unclear
  • Real estate is involved

An experienced probate attorney can review your situation for a few hundred dollars and advise whether the small estate procedure is appropriate.
