How Much Does Probate Cost in California?

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 10 min read

California has some of the highest probate costs in the nation. If you're facing probate, understanding these fees upfront helps you plan accordingly and explore alternatives where possible.

Here's a complete breakdown of what probate costs in California and who pays for it.

California Statutory Probate Fees

California is one of the few states that sets attorney and executor fees by statute. These fees are calculated as a percentage of the estate's gross value—not the net value after debts.

Statutory Fee Schedule (Probate Code §10810):

Estate Value Attorney Fee Executor Fee
First $100,000 4% ($4,000) 4% ($4,000)
Next $100,000 3% ($3,000) 3% ($3,000)
Next $800,000 2% ($16,000) 2% ($16,000)
Next $9,000,000 1% 1%
Above $10,000,000 0.5% 0.5%

Example: $500,000 Estate

  • Attorney fee: $13,000
  • Executor fee: $13,000
  • Combined statutory fees: $26,000

Example: $1,000,000 Estate

  • Attorney fee: $23,000
  • Executor fee: $23,000
  • Combined statutory fees: $46,000

These fees apply to the gross estate value. If someone owns a home worth $800,000 with a $400,000 mortgage, the fee is calculated on the full $800,000—not the $400,000 equity.

Additional Probate Costs

Beyond statutory fees, expect these additional expenses:

  • Court Filing Fees: $435-$550 for the initial petition in Los Angeles County, plus fees for other filings throughout the case.
  • Probate Referee Fees: California requires a court-appointed referee to appraise certain assets. The fee is typically 0.1% of the assets appraised (minimum $150).
  • Publication Costs: Notice to creditors must be published in a local newspaper, typically $200-$400.
  • Bond Premiums: If the court requires the executor to post a bond, expect annual premiums of 0.5-1% of the estate value.
  • Extraordinary Fees: Complex situations (litigation, tax issues, real estate sales, business interests) may justify additional attorney fees beyond the statutory amount, subject to court approval.
  • Accounting Fees: If the estate requires professional accounting or tax preparation, costs vary based on complexity.
  • Real Estate Costs: Selling property may involve agent commissions, repairs, and other expenses.

These estimates include statutory fees, court costs, and typical administrative expenses. Contested cases or complex estates can cost significantly more.

Who Pays Probate Costs?

Probate costs are paid from the estate's assets before distribution to beneficiaries. The executor uses estate funds to pay:

  • Attorney fees
  • Executor compensation
  • Court costs
  • Creditor claims
  • Taxes
  • Administrative expenses

Beneficiaries receive what remains after all debts and expenses are paid.

Ways to Reduce or Avoid Probate Costs

  • Living Trust: Assets in a properly funded living trust pass outside probate entirely. Trust administration typically costs a fraction of probate—often 1-2% of assets versus 4-7% for probate.
  • Small Estate Affidavit: Estates under $184,500 (as of 2024) may qualify for simplified transfer procedures, avoiding formal probate.
  • Joint Ownership: Property held in joint tenancy passes automatically to the surviving owner.
  • Beneficiary Designations: Life insurance, retirement accounts, and TOD/POD accounts pass directly to named beneficiaries.
  • Spousal Property Petition: Surviving spouses may transfer community property through a simplified court procedure.

Is Probate Worth Avoiding?

For most California families, the answer is yes. A living trust package typically costs $2,000-$5,000 to create, compared to tens of thousands in probate fees.

Beyond cost savings, avoiding probate also means:

  • Faster distribution (weeks vs. 12-18 months)
  • Complete privacy (probate is public record)
  • Continuity if you become incapacitated
