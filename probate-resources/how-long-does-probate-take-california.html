How Long Does Probate Take in California?

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 8 min read

If you've recently lost a loved one, you're probably wondering how long the probate process will take before the estate can be settled. The short answer: California probate typically takes 9 to 18 months, but it can extend to 2 years or more for complex estates.

Here's what affects the timeline and what you can expect at each stage.

Average Probate Timeline in California

  • Simple estates: 9-12 months
  • Moderate estates: 12-18 months
  • Complex or contested estates: 18-36+ months

Los Angeles County, including Glendale, Burbank, and Pasadena, often runs on the longer end due to heavy court caseloads.

The California Probate Process Step-by-Step

1. Filing the Petition (Weeks 1-2)

The process begins when someone files a Petition for Probate with the Superior Court. This names the proposed executor or administrator and submits the will (if one exists).

2. Waiting Period and Hearing (4-6 weeks)

California law requires at least 15 days notice before the hearing. The court typically schedules hearings 30-45 days after filing. At the hearing, the judge officially appoints the personal representative.

3. Letters Testamentary Issued (1-2 weeks after hearing)

Once appointed, the executor receives "Letters Testamentary" giving them legal authority to act on behalf of the estate.

4. Creditor Notification Period (4 months minimum)

The executor must notify known creditors and publish a notice in a local newspaper. Creditors have 4 months from the notice date to file claims against the estate. This waiting period cannot be shortened.

5. Inventory and Appraisal (4 months)

The executor must file an inventory of all estate assets within 4 months of appointment. A probate referee appraises certain assets.

6. Estate Administration (varies)

This phase involves paying debts, filing tax returns, managing property, and preparing for distribution. Duration depends on estate complexity.

7. Petition for Final Distribution (2-4 months)

After debts are paid and the creditor period closes, the executor petitions the court to distribute assets to beneficiaries.

8. Final Accounting and Discharge

The executor files a final accounting, the court approves distribution, and the estate closes.

What Can Delay California Probate?

Several factors can extend the timeline significantly:

  • Will contests: If someone challenges the validity of the will, litigation can add 6 months to several years.
  • Missing heirs: Locating beneficiaries who have moved or are difficult to find takes time.
  • Real estate sales: Selling property through probate requires court approval, adding 2-4 months.
  • Tax issues: Complex tax situations, especially with federal estate taxes, can cause delays.
  • Family disputes: Disagreements among beneficiaries often slow the process.
  • Unusual assets: Business interests, intellectual property, or out-of-state property require extra handling.

Can You Speed Up California Probate?

While you can't skip required waiting periods, you can avoid unnecessary delays:

  • Hire an experienced probate attorney who knows the local court procedures and can anticipate issues before they arise.
  • Stay organized with paperwork and respond promptly to requests from the court or attorney.
  • Consider Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, which allows the executor to take many actions without court approval, significantly speeding up administration.
  • Address disputes early through mediation rather than letting conflicts escalate.

Avoiding Probate Altogether

The best way to avoid a lengthy probate process is proper estate planning before death. Living trusts, joint ownership, beneficiary designations, and transfer-on-death deeds can pass assets outside of probate entirely.
