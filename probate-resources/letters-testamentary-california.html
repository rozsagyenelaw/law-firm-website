Letters Testamentary in California: Complete Guide

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 10 min read

If you've been named as an executor in California, you'll need Letters Testamentary before you can access bank accounts, sell property, or conduct any official estate business. These court-issued documents are your proof of authority—and without them, financial institutions and government agencies won't let you act on behalf of the estate.

Letters Testamentary are official documents issued by a California probate court that authorize an executor to manage and distribute a deceased person's estate. They're obtained after the probate hearing, typically 6-8 weeks after filing the initial petition, and grant legal authority to access assets, pay debts, and handle all estate matters.

This complete guide explains what Letters Testamentary are, how to obtain them in Los Angeles County and throughout California, and exactly how to use them.

What Are Letters Testamentary?

Letters Testamentary (California Judicial Council Form DE-150) are court-issued documents that prove you have legal authority to act as executor of an estate. They're sometimes called "Letters" or "probate letters."

Think of them as your official credentials—like a driver's license proves you can legally drive, Letters Testamentary prove you can legally manage the deceased's estate.

Letters Testamentary vs. Letters of Administration

California uses two types of Letters:

Letters Testamentary: Issued when the deceased left a will and named an executor

Letters of Administration: Issued when the deceased died without a will (intestate) and the court appoints an administrator

Functionally, both documents grant the same authority. The only difference is the terminology based on whether a will exists.

What Authority Do Letters Testamentary Grant?

Once you have Letters Testamentary, you can legally:

  • Access bank accounts and investment accounts in the deceased's name
  • Open an estate bank account to manage estate funds
  • Sell or transfer real estate owned by the deceased
  • Sell or transfer vehicles and other titled assets
  • Access safe deposit boxes
  • Collect debts owed to the deceased
  • Pay estate debts and expenses
  • File tax returns on behalf of the estate
  • Represent the estate in legal matters
  • Distribute assets to beneficiaries (when authorized)

Without Letters Testamentary, you cannot do any of these things—even if you're named as executor in the will.

Full Authority vs. Limited Authority

California Letters Testamentary come in two varieties:

Full Authority (IAEA)

Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority allows you to take most actions without court approval, including:

  • Selling personal property
  • Selling real estate (with some notice requirements)
  • Paying debts
  • Distributing assets
  • Making most estate decisions independently

IAEA authority dramatically speeds up probate by eliminating the need for multiple court hearings.

Limited Authority

Without IAEA authority, you must petition the court and obtain approval before taking significant actions like selling property or making distributions. This adds months to the probate process.

Best Practice: Always request full authority under IAEA when filing your Petition for Probate (Form DE-111). Most courts grant it unless beneficiaries object.

How to Obtain Letters Testamentary in California

Letters Testamentary are issued after the probate hearing. Here's the complete process:

Step 1: File Petition for Probate

Submit Form DE-111 (Petition for Probate) to the Superior Court in the county where the deceased resided. In Los Angeles County, file at:

Stanley Mosk Courthouse
111 N. Hill Street
Los Angeles, CA 90012
Department 5 (Probate Division)

Your petition must include:

  • The original will
  • Death certificate
  • Information about all heirs and beneficiaries
  • Estimated value of estate assets
  • Request for IAEA authority (check the appropriate box)

Step 2: Provide Notice

After filing, you must:

  • Publish notice in a local newspaper for three consecutive weeks
  • Mail notice to all heirs and beneficiaries at least 15 days before the hearing

Step 3: Attend the Probate Hearing

The court schedules a hearing approximately 30-45 days after filing. At the hearing:

  • The judge reviews your petition
  • The judge verifies notice was properly given
  • The judge validates the will (if applicable)
  • If no one objects, the judge signs an Order for Probate appointing you as executor

Step 4: Request Letters Testamentary

After the judge signs the Order for Probate, request certified copies of Letters Testamentary from the court clerk.

Cost in Los Angeles County (2025):

  • First certified copy: $30
  • Additional copies: $5 each

How many copies should you get? Order at least 6-10 certified copies. You'll need them for:

  • Each bank or financial institution (2-4 copies)
  • Real estate transactions (1-2 copies)
  • Vehicle transfers (1 copy)
  • IRS and tax authorities (1 copy)
  • Your records (1 copy)

Getting extras upfront is cheaper than ordering more later.

Step 5: Receive Letters (1-2 weeks)

The court typically issues Letters Testamentary 1-2 weeks after the hearing. In Los Angeles County, you can usually pick them up in person or request them by mail.

What Information Is on Letters Testamentary?

California Letters Testamentary include:

  • Court name and case number
  • Deceased person's name and date of death
  • Your name as appointed executor
  • Date of appointment
  • Statement that you're authorized to act
  • Whether you have full or limited authority
  • Court clerk's signature and official seal
  • Expiration date (typically none—they're valid indefinitely during probate)

The document is printed on security paper with the court's raised seal—institutions check for these features to verify authenticity.

How to Use Letters Testamentary

At Banks and Financial Institutions

Present your Letters along with a certified death certificate to:

  • Close individual accounts and transfer funds to the estate account
  • Access account information
  • Liquidate investments
  • Access safe deposit boxes

Pro Tip: Call ahead to ask about specific requirements. Some institutions have their own internal forms to complete.

For Real Estate Transactions

To sell estate real property:

  • Provide copies to the title company
  • Record copies with the county recorder (some transactions)
  • Show to potential buyers and real estate agents as proof of authority

If you have IAEA authority, you can sell real estate with just 15 days' notice to beneficiaries. Without IAEA, you need court confirmation—adding 2-3 months to the process.

With Government Agencies

Use Letters Testamentary when dealing with:

  • IRS: Filing tax returns and handling estate tax issues
  • Social Security Administration: Reporting death and stopping benefits
  • DMV: Transferring or selling vehicles
  • County Recorder: Transferring real property
  • Pension administrators: Claiming benefits

For Legal and Business Matters

Present Letters when:

  • Opening the estate bank account
  • Collecting debts owed to the deceased
  • Defending or pursuing lawsuits on behalf of the estate
  • Dealing with business partners or corporate interests
  • Closing utilities and canceling services

When Do Letters Testamentary Expire?

California Letters Testamentary remain valid throughout the probate process and don't have an expiration date. However, your authority ends when:

  • The court closes the estate
  • The court removes you as executor
  • You resign as executor
  • Final distribution is complete

After probate closes, Letters are no longer valid because you no longer have an estate to administer.

Can Letters Testamentary Be Revoked?

Yes. The court can revoke Letters and remove an executor for:

  • Breach of fiduciary duty: Mismanaging assets or acting dishonestly
  • Incompetence: Failing to perform duties properly
  • Self-dealing: Using estate assets for personal benefit
  • Failure to account: Not providing required financial reports
  • Conflicts of interest: Having interests adverse to the estate
  • Insolvency or incapacity: Being unable to serve

Beneficiaries can petition to remove an executor. If the court agrees, it revokes Letters and appoints a replacement.

What If You Lose Your Letters Testamentary?

If you lose a copy or need more, return to the court and request additional certified copies. Bring:

  • Photo ID
  • The case number
  • Payment ($5 per copy after the first)

The court can issue new certified copies at any time during probate.

Do Out-of-State Institutions Accept California Letters?

Generally, yes. California Letters Testamentary are recognized throughout the United States under reciprocity laws. However:

  • Some institutions may require additional documentation
  • You may need to open ancillary probate for out-of-state real estate
  • Foreign countries may not recognize California Letters

If the deceased owned property in another state, consult an attorney about ancillary probate requirements.

Letters Testamentary with Bond vs. Without Bond

Sometimes the court requires executors to post a bond—like insurance that protects beneficiaries from executor misconduct.

When Is Bond Required?

Bond is typically required unless:

  • The will waives bond
  • All beneficiaries waive bond in writing
  • The executor is the sole beneficiary

Bond Amounts

Bond amount equals the estate's value plus expected annual income. For example:

  • $500,000 estate = $500,000 bond
  • Cost: Approximately $500-1,500 annually (0.5-1% of bond amount)

Bond costs are paid from estate funds, not personally.

How Bond Affects Letters

If bond is required, Letters won't be issued until you post bond. This can delay your appointment by 1-2 weeks while arranging bonding.

Best Practice: If you drafted the will, include language waiving bond. If you're handling an estate where bond is required, work with a surety company experienced in probate bonds.

Common Problems with Letters Testamentary

Problem 1: Institution Refuses to Accept Letters

Solution: Letters Testamentary are legal authority under California law. If an institution refuses valid Letters, ask to speak with their legal department or a manager. If they still refuse, your attorney can send a demand letter.

Problem 2: Letters Show Limited Authority

Solution: If you forgot to request IAEA authority, you can petition the court to amend your Letters. File Form DE-147 requesting full authority. Most courts grant this unless beneficiaries object.

Problem 3: Wrong Name or Information on Letters

Solution: If there's an error, petition the court to correct it. File a motion explaining the mistake and requesting correction. The court will issue amended Letters.

Problem 4: Letters Lost or Destroyed

Solution: Return to the court and purchase new certified copies. The court maintains records throughout probate.

Temporary Letters vs. General Letters

California also issues Special Letters of Administration in emergency situations before a full hearing can be held. These allow immediate action to:

  • Prevent estate waste or loss
  • Pay urgent expenses
  • Secure property

Temporary Letters expire quickly (usually 30-60 days) and are replaced by general Letters after the full hearing.

Checklist: Using Your Letters Testamentary

Once you receive Letters, take these actions:

  1. Make copies: Scan and save digital copies
  2. Open estate bank account: Take Letters to a bank to open an account in the estate's name
  3. Notify financial institutions: Contact all banks, brokerages, and investment accounts
  4. Access safe deposit box: If applicable
  5. Obtain estate EIN: Apply for an Employer Identification Number from the IRS
  6. Transfer titled assets: Work with DMV for vehicles, county recorder for real estate
  7. Notify creditors: Begin the formal notice process
  8. Begin inventory: You have 4 months to complete the estate inventory
