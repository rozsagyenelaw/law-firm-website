Selling a House in Probate California: Executor's Guide

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 14 min read

Selling a house during probate in California involves unique procedures, court requirements, and timelines that differ significantly from a standard real estate transaction. As an executor, you need to understand whether you need court approval, how the overbid process works, and what steps to take to complete the sale legally.

In California, selling probate real estate requires either Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority—allowing sale with 15 days' notice to beneficiaries—or court confirmation through a public hearing where others can submit higher bids. The process takes 2-4 months and must meet strict legal requirements including proper pricing and disclosure.

This comprehensive guide explains exactly how to sell a house in California probate, whether you're in Los Angeles County, Glendale, Burbank, Pasadena, or anywhere in the state.

Two Ways to Sell Real Estate in California Probate

California law provides two methods for selling probate property:

Method 1: IAEA Sale (Independent Administration)

If you have full authority under the Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA), you can sell real estate with just 15 days' written notice to beneficiaries. No court hearing is required.

Advantages:

  • Much faster (saves 2-3 months)
  • No court confirmation hearing
  • No overbid process
  • More attractive to buyers (no uncertainty)
  • Lower costs

Method 2: Court Confirmation Sale

Without IAEA authority, you must petition the court to approve the sale. This involves a public hearing where others can submit competing bids.

Disadvantages:

  • Adds 2-3 months to the process
  • Public overbid process (original buyer can be outbid)
  • More expensive (additional court filings and hearings)
  • Buyers often hesitant due to uncertainty

Bottom Line: Always request IAEA authority when filing your Petition for Probate. It dramatically simplifies real estate sales.

Step-by-Step: Selling a House with IAEA Authority

If you have IAEA authority, here's the streamlined process:

Step 1: Obtain Letters Testamentary

You need Letters Testamentary (Form DE-150) showing you have authority to sell. These are issued after your probate hearing, typically 6-8 weeks after filing the petition.

Step 2: Get a Professional Appraisal (Optional but Recommended)

While not required for IAEA sales, getting an appraisal protects you from claims you sold property below fair market value. Cost: $400-800.

Step 3: Hire a Real Estate Agent

Choose an agent experienced with probate sales. They'll understand California probate disclosure requirements and procedures.

Commission: Typically 5-6% of sale price, paid from estate funds at closing.

Step 4: Prepare the Property

As executor, you can use estate funds to:

  • Clean and remove personal property
  • Make minor repairs
  • Stage the property (if beneficial)
  • Maintain landscaping

Important: Keep receipts. All expenses must be documented in your final accounting.

Step 5: Disclose Probate Status

California requires specific disclosures for probate sales:

  • Notice of Proposed Action (Form DE-165): Must be given to beneficiaries 15 days before completing the sale
  • Probate status disclosure: Inform buyers the sale is subject to probate
  • Standard disclosures: Property condition, natural hazards, etc.

Step 6: Market and Show the Property

List the property like any other sale, but include "probate sale" in marketing materials. Some buyers specifically seek probate properties.

Step 7: Accept an Offer

Review offers with your real estate agent. You must accept a price that's fair market value—significantly underpricing property can result in personal liability.

The purchase agreement should include:

  • Contingency language acknowledging probate status
  • Extended escrow period (60-90 days typical)
  • AS-IS clause (executors typically sell without warranties)

Step 8: Provide Notice to Beneficiaries

Send Notice of Proposed Action (Form DE-165) to all beneficiaries at least 15 days before close of escrow. This notice must include:

  • Description of the property
  • Proposed sale price and terms
  • Buyer's name
  • Estimated closing date
  • Real estate commission

Beneficiaries have 15 days to object. If they do, you may need court approval.

Step 9: Open Escrow

Work with a title/escrow company experienced in probate transactions. They'll handle:

  • Title search and title insurance
  • Coordination of documents
  • Prorated property taxes
  • Recording transfer of ownership
  • Disbursement of funds

Step 10: Complete Required Inspections and Reports

The buyer typically conducts:

  • Home inspection
  • Termite inspection
  • Title review
  • Appraisal (if financing)

As executor, you're selling AS-IS, but you must disclose known material defects.

Step 11: Sign Closing Documents

Sign all required documents as "Executor of the Estate of [Deceased's Name]." Bring your Letters Testamentary to escrow signing.

Step 12: Close Escrow

After all conditions are met, escrow closes and funds are wired to the estate bank account (never your personal account).

Step 13: Report the Sale to the Court (If Required)

Depending on local rules, you may need to file a report or include the sale in your final accounting.

IAEA Timeline: 60-90 days from accepting offer to closing

Step-by-Step: Selling a House with Court Confirmation

If you don't have IAEA authority, the process is more complex:

Step 1-7: Same as Above

Follow steps 1-7 from the IAEA process to get an offer.

Step 8: Meet Minimum Price Requirements

California Probate Code requires court confirmation sales meet minimum price thresholds:

  • 90% of appraised value: The offer must be at least 90% of the probate referee's appraisal
  • Appraisal required: The probate referee (court-appointed appraiser) must value the property

Example: If the property is appraised at $500,000, the minimum acceptable offer is $450,000.

Step 9: File Petition for Confirmation of Sale

Submit these forms to the court:

  • Form DE-260: Petition for Confirmation of Sale of Real Property
  • Form DE-265: Order Confirming Sale
  • Copy of the purchase agreement
  • Probate referee's appraisal
  • Real estate broker's commission agreement

Step 10: Publish Notice

Publish notice of the hearing in a local newspaper at least 20 days before the hearing. This alerts potential buyers who may want to submit competing bids.

Step 11: Mail Notice

Mail notice to all beneficiaries at least 15 days before the hearing.

Step 12: Attend the Confirmation Hearing

At the hearing, the judge reviews the sale. If the price meets requirements and no one objects, the judge confirms the sale.

However: This is where the overbid process comes into play.

Understanding California's Overbid Process

At the confirmation hearing, other buyers can submit higher bids. This creates an auction-like environment in the courtroom.

How Overbids Work

Minimum Overbid Formula:

  • First $10,000 of original bid: Increase by 10%
  • Amount over $10,000: Increase by 5%

Example Calculation:

Original offer: $500,000

  • 10% of first $10,000 = $1,000
  • 5% of remaining $490,000 = $24,500
  • Minimum overbid = $500,000 + $1,000 + $24,500 = $525,500

Subsequent Bids: After the first overbid, subsequent increases must be at least $5,000.

Original Buyer's Dilemma

The original buyer can match overbids, but they risk losing the property even after spending money on inspections and appraisals. This is why many buyers avoid court confirmation sales.

Who Gets the Property?

The highest bidder at the hearing wins. The judge confirms the sale to that buyer.

Important: Overbidders must bring a cashier's check for 10% of their bid to the hearing as a deposit.

Real Estate Commission Adjustments

When an overbid occurs, the original broker's commission is calculated on the higher sale price—but the court may reduce the percentage if the increase is substantial.

Pricing Strategy for Probate Sales

For IAEA Sales

  • Price at fair market value
  • Consider comparative market analysis from your agent
  • Don't underprice—beneficiaries can object and force court confirmation
  • Don't overprice—extends time on market, increasing estate costs

For Court Confirmation Sales

  • Price at or above 90% of appraised value
  • Consider pricing slightly below market to attract overbids (maximizes estate value)
  • Be transparent with buyers about the overbid process

Timeline: How Long Does It Take to Sell Probate Property?

IAEA Sale Timeline

  • Listing to offer: 30-60 days (market dependent)
  • 15-day notice period: 15 days
  • Escrow: 30-45 days
  • Total: 75-120 days (2.5-4 months)

Court Confirmation Timeline

  • Listing to offer: 30-60 days
  • File petition and schedule hearing: 30-45 days
  • Confirmation hearing: 1 day
  • Escrow after confirmation: 30-45 days
  • Total: 90-150 days (3-5 months)

Special Considerations for Probate Real Estate Sales

Multiple Properties

If the estate owns multiple properties, you can sell them individually or together depending on what maximizes estate value.

Out-of-State Property

Property located outside California requires ancillary probate in that state. Consult an attorney in the state where the property is located.

Co-Owned Property

If the deceased owned property as a tenant in common (not joint tenancy), only their share goes through probate. Selling may require cooperation from other co-owners.

Underwater Mortgages

If the property is worth less than the mortgage balance, you may need to pursue a short sale with lender approval—a complex process requiring specialized expertise.

Beneficiary Objections

If beneficiaries object to the sale, the court will hold a hearing to determine whether the sale should proceed. Common objections include:

  • Price too low
  • Beneficiary wants to purchase the property
  • Beneficiary wants to keep the property
  • Conflicts of interest

Tax Implications of Selling Probate Real Estate

Capital Gains Tax

When you sell probate property, capital gains tax is calculated based on the difference between:

  • Sale price
  • Stepped-up basis (fair market value at date of death)

Example:

  • Deceased bought house for $200,000 in 1990
  • House worth $600,000 at date of death (stepped-up basis)
  • House sells for $620,000
  • Capital gain: $620,000 - $600,000 = $20,000

The estate or beneficiaries pay tax only on the $20,000 gain, not the full $420,000 increase since original purchase.

Estate Income Tax

Any capital gains from the sale are reported on the estate's income tax return (Form 1041). Gains may be passed through to beneficiaries depending on timing of distribution.

Property Tax Reassessment

California property taxes don't increase upon inheritance if certain conditions are met (parent-child exclusion, etc.). Consult a tax professional about available exclusions.

Common Mistakes When Selling Probate Property

1. Selling Before Appointment

You cannot sell estate property until you receive Letters Testamentary. Any attempts to do so are void.

2. Underpricing Property

Selling significantly below market value can result in personal liability or beneficiary objections.

3. Not Maintaining Property

Failing to maintain insurance, pay property taxes, or secure the property can result in liability for losses.

4. Commingling Funds

All sale proceeds must go into the estate bank account, never your personal account.

5. Missing Notice Requirements

Failing to provide proper notice to beneficiaries can invalidate the sale.

6. Not Disclosing Defects

Even though sales are typically AS-IS, you must disclose known material defects.

Working with Real Estate Professionals

Choosing the Right Agent

Look for agents with:

  • Probate sale experience
  • Knowledge of local court procedures
  • Certified Probate Real Estate Specialist (CPRES) designation
  • Track record of closed probate sales

Commission Agreements

Real estate commissions in probate sales are typically:

  • 5-6% of sale price
  • Paid from estate funds at closing
  • Subject to court approval (in confirmation sales)

The listing agreement should specify the commission is contingent on court confirmation if applicable.

Do You Need an Attorney to Sell Probate Property?

While not always required, hiring a probate attorney is highly recommended when selling real estate because:

  • Real estate transactions are complex
  • Mistakes can result in personal liability
  • Beneficiaries may object
  • Court procedures are technical
  • Tax implications need professional guidance

Attorney fees are paid from estate funds and are based on California's statutory fee schedule.
