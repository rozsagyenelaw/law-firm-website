Spousal Property Petition California: Skip Full Probate

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 12 min read

If your spouse recently passed away in California, you may be able to transfer their estate assets directly to yourself without going through full probate. The Spousal Property Petition (also called a "Spousal Property Order" or "spousal set aside") is a streamlined court procedure that saves time, money, and stress for surviving spouses.

A California Spousal Property Petition (Form DE-221) allows surviving spouses and domestic partners to transfer all community property and certain separate property to themselves without full probate administration. This simplified procedure takes 3-6 months instead of 12-18 months and costs significantly less than formal probate.

This comprehensive guide explains how the Spousal Property Petition works, who qualifies, what property can be transferred, and the complete filing process in Los Angeles County and throughout California.

What Is a Spousal Property Petition?

A Spousal Property Petition is a court proceeding under California Probate Code Sections 13500-13660 that allows surviving spouses (and registered domestic partners) to obtain a court order transferring the deceased spouse's property directly to them.

Key advantages:

  • No executor or administrator appointment needed
  • No creditor publication requirements
  • No inventory and appraisal required
  • No formal estate administration
  • Much faster than full probate (3-6 months vs. 12-18 months)
  • Significantly cheaper (often $3,000-7,000 vs. $15,000-30,000+)
  • Works regardless of estate size

Who Can File a Spousal Property Petition?

Eligible Filers

  • Surviving spouse legally married to the deceased at time of death
  • Registered domestic partner (same rights as spouses in California)

Who Cannot Use This Procedure

  • Unmarried partners (even long-term relationships)
  • Ex-spouses (divorced before death)
  • Separated spouses who weren't legally married at death
  • Children, parents, or other relatives

Important: If you were separated but not yet divorced, you're still considered legally married and can use this procedure.

What Property Can Be Transferred?

Community Property (Always Transferrable)

All community property passes to the surviving spouse:

  • Real estate acquired during marriage with marital funds
  • Bank accounts funded with marital earnings
  • Investment accounts opened during marriage
  • Retirement accounts funded during marriage
  • Vehicles purchased during marriage
  • Business interests created during marriage
  • Personal property acquired during marriage

Remember: In California, all property acquired during marriage using either spouse's earnings is presumed to be community property.

Separate Property (Conditionally Transferrable)

Separate property can pass to the surviving spouse through a Spousal Property Petition IF:

1. The Will Leaves It to the Spouse

If the deceased's will explicitly leaves separate property to you, it can be transferred through the petition.

2. No Will Exists and You're the Sole Heir

If there's no will and you're the only person who would inherit under intestacy law (no children, parents, siblings, etc.), all separate property passes to you.

3. The Will Leaves Everything to the Spouse

If the will says "I leave everything to my spouse," both community and separate property pass through the petition.

Property That Cannot Be Transferred via Spousal Petition

The Spousal Property Petition cannot transfer separate property if:

  • The will leaves separate property to someone other than the spouse
  • There's no will and other heirs exist (children, parents, etc.)
  • Someone contests the will

In these situations, you'll need full probate for separate property (but can still use the spousal petition for community property).

Step-by-Step: How to File a Spousal Property Petition

Step 1: Determine if You Qualify

Before starting, confirm:

  • You were legally married or in a registered domestic partnership at time of death
  • You know which property is community vs. separate
  • You understand what property will transfer
  • No creditor issues complicate the estate

Step 2: Gather Required Documents

Collect:

  • Certified death certificate (at least 5-10 copies)
  • Original will (if one exists)
  • Marriage certificate or domestic partnership registration
  • Property deeds for all real estate
  • Bank and investment account statements
  • Vehicle titles
  • List of all assets with values

Step 3: Complete Required Forms

Form DE-221: Spousal or Domestic Partner Property Petition

This 3-page form is the heart of your petition. It requires:

  • Deceased's name, date of death, and county of residence
  • Your name and relationship
  • Whether a will exists
  • Description of all property you're asking the court to transfer
  • Whether property is community or separate
  • Estimated values of property

Form DE-111: Petition for Probate (if no probate case exists)

If no one has filed a probate petition, you may need to file this concurrently.

Form DE-226: Spousal or Domestic Partner Property Order

This is the proposed order you're asking the judge to sign.

Form DE-120: Notice of Hearing

This notice will be mailed to interested parties.

Step 4: File with the Court

Submit your completed forms to the probate court in the county where the deceased lived.

Los Angeles County Filing Location:

Stanley Mosk Courthouse
111 N. Hill Street
Los Angeles, CA 90012
Department 5 (Probate Division)

Filing Fee: $465 (as of 2025)

The clerk will:

  • Stamp your documents
  • Assign a case number
  • Schedule a hearing (typically 30-45 days out)

Step 5: Provide Notice to Interested Parties

At least 15 days before the hearing, mail notice to:

  • All heirs who would inherit if there were no will
  • All beneficiaries named in the will
  • Anyone named as executor in the will
  • The Attorney General (if charitable gifts involved)

Complete Form DE-120 (Proof of Service by Mail) documenting who received notice.

Note: Unlike full probate, you don't need to publish notice in a newspaper.

Step 6: Attend the Court Hearing

Arrive at the courthouse 30 minutes early. Bring:

  • Photo ID
  • Filed copies of all documents
  • Proof of service showing notice was mailed
  • Original will (if applicable)

At the hearing:

  1. Check in with the courtroom clerk
  2. Wait for your case to be called
  3. The judge reviews your petition
  4. The judge may ask questions about property characterization
  5. If everything is in order and no one objects, the judge signs the order

Most hearings last 5-10 minutes for uncontested cases.

Step 7: Obtain Certified Copies of the Order

After the hearing, request certified copies of the signed Spousal Property Order.

Cost: $30 for the first copy, $5 for each additional

How many to order: At least 5-10 copies for use with banks, title companies, etc.

Step 8: Transfer Assets

Use the court order to transfer property:

Real Estate:

  • Record the order with the county recorder where the property is located
  • Attach a certified death certificate
  • Update title to your name

Bank Accounts:

  • Present the order and death certificate to each financial institution
  • Close accounts in deceased's name
  • Transfer funds to your accounts

Vehicles:

  • Take the order to the DMV
  • Complete transfer forms
  • Obtain new title in your name

Investment Accounts:

  • Submit the order to brokerage firms
  • Transfer securities to your accounts

Timeline: How Long Does It Take?

  • Week 1-2: Gather documents and prepare forms
  • Week 2-3: File petition with court
  • Week 3-6: Wait for hearing date (30-45 days out)
  • Week 4-5: Mail notice to interested parties (at least 15 days before hearing)
  • Week 6-8: Attend hearing and receive order
  • Week 8-12: Transfer assets using the order

Total Time: 3-6 months (compared to 12-18 months for full probate)

Community Property vs. Separate Property: Why It Matters

Understanding the difference is critical for spousal petitions:

Community Property

Property acquired during marriage through either spouse's efforts:

  • Wages and salary earned during marriage
  • Real estate purchased with marital funds
  • Businesses started during marriage
  • Retirement accounts funded during marriage

The surviving spouse always gets 100% of community property, regardless of the will.

Separate Property

Property that belongs to one spouse alone:

  • Property owned before marriage
  • Gifts and inheritances received by one spouse
  • Property acquired after permanent separation
  • Property designated separate in a prenuptial agreement

Separate property only passes to the surviving spouse if the will says so or if the spouse is the sole heir.

Commingled Property (The Gray Area)

Sometimes separate and community property mix:

  • Separate property house where mortgage is paid with marital earnings
  • Inheritance deposited into joint bank account
  • Separate property business that spouse works in during marriage

When You Cannot Use Spousal Property Petition

The spousal petition won't work if:

1. Separate Property Goes to Others

If the will leaves separate property to children, relatives, or friends, you need full probate for that property (though you can still use the spousal petition for community property).

2. Will Contest Exists

If someone contests the will's validity, the court won't grant a spousal petition until the contest is resolved.

3. Significant Creditor Issues

If the deceased had substantial debts that exceed estate assets, full probate provides better creditor procedures.

4. Complex Estate Issues

  • Business interests requiring ongoing management
  • Litigation involving the estate
  • Tax complexities requiring extended administration
  • Multiple properties requiring sales

Combining Spousal Petition with Other Procedures

Spousal Petition + Small Estate Affidavit

You can use a spousal petition for community property and a small estate affidavit for separate property under $184,500 going to others.

Spousal Petition + Partial Probate

Transfer community property via spousal petition while running full probate only for separate property going to others.

Spousal Petition + Living Trust

Assets in a living trust pass outside probate. Use the spousal petition only for assets not titled in the trust.

Tax Considerations

Property Tax Reassessment

Good news: Property transferred to a surviving spouse is exempt from property tax reassessment under California Proposition 19. Your property taxes won't increase just because title transfers.

Income Tax

The surviving spouse generally doesn't pay income tax on inherited property. You receive a "stepped-up basis" to fair market value at date of death.

Estate Tax

California has no state estate tax. Federal estate tax only applies to estates over $13.61 million (2024), and the unlimited marital deduction means no federal estate tax is due when property passes to a spouse.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Assuming All Property Is Community Property

Don't assume. Trace property origins carefully, especially for real estate and investments.

2. Not Providing Proper Notice

Failing to notify all interested parties can result in the petition being denied or challenged later.

3. Transferring Assets Before Getting the Order

Wait for the court order. Transferring assets prematurely can create legal problems.

4. Ignoring Creditors

While you don't have formal creditor publication requirements, you're still responsible for paying valid debts.

5. Not Updating Estate Planning

After receiving property, update your own estate plan to reflect new ownership.

Spousal Property Petition After Full Probate Started

What if someone already filed for full probate?

You can still file a Spousal Property Petition even if full probate proceedings have begun. The court will:

  • Set aside community property to you immediately
  • Allow full probate to continue only for separate property going to others

This still saves time and money by removing community property from probate administration.
