Executor Duties in California: Complete Guide

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 12 min read

Being named as an executor (also called a personal representative) in California comes with significant legal responsibilities. If you've been appointed or nominated as an executor, you're probably wondering what exactly you need to do and what legal obligations you must fulfill.

As a California executor, you're legally required to manage the deceased person's estate, pay debts and taxes, and distribute assets to beneficiaries—all while following strict court procedures and maintaining fiduciary standards. The role typically takes 9-18 months to complete and involves more than a dozen specific duties.

This comprehensive guide walks you through every executor responsibility in California, from filing the initial petition through final distribution.

What Is an Executor in California?

An executor (or personal representative) is the person named in a will to manage a deceased person's estate through probate court. If there's no will, the court appoints an "administrator" who performs the same duties.

In California, the executor acts as a fiduciary—meaning you're legally obligated to act in the best interests of the estate and its beneficiaries, not yourself. You must be honest, transparent, and diligent in performing your duties.

Who Can Serve as Executor in California?

To be eligible, you must be:

  • At least 18 years old
  • Of sound mind
  • Not a convicted felon (unless the court grants special permission)
  • Preferably a California resident (non-residents may need to post additional bond)

Complete Checklist of Executor Duties in California

Phase 1: Initiating Probate (Weeks 1-6)

1. Locate the Original Will and Death Certificate

Obtain multiple certified copies of the death certificate (usually 10-15 copies). Find the original will—California courts require the original document, not a copy.

2. File the Petition for Probate

Within 30 days of death, file a Petition for Probate (Form DE-111) with the Superior Court in the county where the deceased lived. You'll need:

  • The original will
  • Death certificate
  • List of all heirs and beneficiaries
  • Estimated value of estate assets

3. Notify All Interested Parties

You must provide legal notice to:

  • All beneficiaries named in the will
  • All legal heirs (even if not in the will)
  • Anyone named as executor in the will

4. Publish Notice to Creditors

California law requires publishing a "Notice of Petition to Administer Estate" in a local newspaper for three consecutive weeks. This alerts potential creditors and gives them a deadline to file claims.

5. Attend the Probate Hearing

Approximately 30-45 days after filing, attend the court hearing where the judge officially appoints you as executor. If no one objects, you'll receive Letters Testamentary—your legal authority to act.

Phase 2: Estate Administration (Months 2-12)

6. Obtain Letters Testamentary

These court-issued documents prove your authority. Request multiple certified copies—you'll need them to access bank accounts, sell property, and conduct estate business.

7. Open an Estate Bank Account

Open a checking account in the estate's name (e.g., "Estate of John Doe"). All estate funds must flow through this account for proper accounting. Never mix estate money with personal funds.

8. Identify and Secure All Estate Assets

Locate everything the deceased owned:

  • Real estate (homes, rental properties, land)
  • Bank accounts and investments
  • Vehicles, boats, and recreational vehicles
  • Personal property (jewelry, art, collectibles)
  • Business interests
  • Life insurance proceeds payable to the estate
  • Digital assets and cryptocurrency

Take immediate steps to secure property: change locks, maintain insurance, collect rent from rental properties, and protect valuables.

9. File the Inventory and Appraisal

Within 4 months of appointment, file Form DE-160 listing all estate assets and their values. The court will appoint a probate referee to appraise certain assets. This is a critical deadline—missing it can result in penalties.

10. Notify Known Creditors

Send formal written notice (Form DE-157) to all known creditors within 4 months of your appointment. They then have 60 days (or 4 months from the newspaper publication date, whichever is later) to file claims.

11. Manage Estate Property

You're responsible for maintaining and protecting estate assets during probate:

  • Pay mortgages, property taxes, and insurance
  • Maintain and repair property
  • Collect rent and manage tenants
  • Make prudent investment decisions
  • Secure and store personal property

12. Pay Valid Debts and Claims

Review all creditor claims. Pay legitimate debts from estate funds in this order of priority:

  1. Funeral and burial expenses
  2. Estate administration costs (court fees, attorney fees, executor fees)
  3. Secured debts (mortgages, car loans)
  4. Medical expenses from final illness
  5. Tax debts
  6. Other unsecured debts

You can reject claims you believe are invalid, but the creditor may petition the court to review your decision.

13. File Tax Returns

You're responsible for filing multiple tax returns:

  • Final personal income tax return: The deceased's final Form 1040
  • Estate income tax returns: Form 1041 for any income the estate earns during probate
  • Federal estate tax return: Form 706 if the estate exceeds $13.61 million (2024)
  • California estate tax: California has no state estate tax, but may have other filing requirements

Hire a CPA experienced in estate taxation—mistakes can result in personal liability.

14. Sell Estate Assets (If Necessary)

If the estate needs liquidity to pay debts or if the will directs it, you may need to sell assets. In California probate, selling real estate requires either:

  • Court confirmation at a noticed hearing (traditional process)
  • Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, which allows sale without court confirmation

Real estate sales in probate can take 2-4 additional months.

Phase 3: Final Distribution (Months 12-18)

15. Prepare and File the Final Accounting

After the 4-month creditor period closes and all debts are paid, prepare a detailed accounting of every dollar that entered and left the estate. This includes:

  • All assets received
  • All income earned
  • Every expense and debt paid
  • Current balance of assets remaining

File Form DE-160 (Inventory and Appraisal) if not already filed, and provide a complete accounting to all beneficiaries.

16. File Petition for Final Distribution

File Form DE-295 requesting the court's approval to distribute assets to beneficiaries according to the will or California intestacy law.

17. Distribute Assets to Beneficiaries

After court approval, transfer assets to beneficiaries. Obtain signed receipts from each beneficiary acknowledging receipt of their inheritance.

18. Close the Estate

File the final documents with the court, including proof that all assets have been distributed. Once approved, the judge will discharge you from your duties and close the estate.

Executor Compensation in California

California law entitles executors to "reasonable compensation" based on a statutory fee schedule calculated on the gross estate value:

  • 4% of the first $100,000
  • 3% of the next $100,000
  • 2% of the next $800,000
  • 1% of the next $9 million
  • 0.5% of the next $15 million
  • Reasonable amount for estates over $25 million

For example, an estate worth $500,000 would entitle the executor to $13,000 in fees ($4,000 + $3,000 + $6,000).

Executors can also receive additional compensation for extraordinary services like managing a business, handling litigation, or selling real estate.

Executor Fiduciary Duties in California

Beyond the mechanical tasks, California law imposes strict fiduciary duties on executors:

Duty of Loyalty: You must act solely in the interest of the estate and beneficiaries. Don't engage in self-dealing or conflicts of interest.

Duty of Impartiality: Treat all beneficiaries fairly. Don't favor one over another.

Duty of Prudence: Act with reasonable care, skill, and caution. Make decisions a prudent person would make.

Duty to Account: Keep detailed records and provide accountings to beneficiaries and the court.

Duty to Inform: Keep beneficiaries reasonably informed about estate administration.

Common Executor Mistakes to Avoid

1. Missing Court Deadlines

California probate has strict deadlines. Missing the 4-month inventory deadline or creditor notice requirements can result in penalties or personal liability.

2. Mixing Personal and Estate Funds

Never deposit estate money into your personal account or pay estate expenses from personal funds. Always use the estate bank account.

3. Distributing Assets Too Early

Wait until the creditor period closes and all debts are paid. If you distribute assets prematurely and a creditor makes a claim, you could be personally liable.

4. Failing to Communicate with Beneficiaries

Keep beneficiaries informed. Lack of communication is the number one source of conflict and can lead to litigation.

5. Not Hiring Professional Help

California probate is complex. Most executors benefit from hiring an experienced probate attorney and accountant.

6. Ignoring Tax Obligations

File all required tax returns on time. The IRS can hold you personally responsible for estate taxes.

7. Making Emotional Decisions

Don't let family pressure influence your duties. You're required to follow the law and the will, even if family members disagree.

When Should You Hire a Probate Attorney?

While California law doesn't require executors to hire an attorney, most do. Consider hiring a probate attorney if:

  • The estate is worth more than $500,000
  • There's real estate to sell
  • There are disputes among beneficiaries
  • The deceased owned a business
  • There are complex tax issues
  • You're unfamiliar with California probate law
  • You live out of state
  • There are potential creditor issues

Attorney fees are paid from the estate (not your personal funds) and are based on the same statutory schedule as executor compensation.

Can You Decline to Serve as Executor?

Yes. Being named as executor in a will doesn't obligate you to serve. If you don't want the responsibility, you can decline by filing a form with the court. The court will then appoint someone else.

Common reasons to decline include:

  • Living far from California
  • Poor health or advanced age
  • Lack of time or financial expertise
  • Conflicts with beneficiaries
  • Emotional difficulty handling a loved one's estate

Timeline for Completing Executor Duties

Here's a realistic timeline for California executor duties:

  • Week 1-2: Locate will, gather documents, secure property
  • Week 2-4: File petition for probate, publish notice
  • Week 6-8: Attend probate hearing, receive Letters Testamentary
  • Month 2-3: Open estate account, notify creditors, begin inventory
  • Month 4: File inventory and appraisal
  • Month 4-8: Wait for creditor period to expire, pay debts
  • Month 9-12: File tax returns, resolve any claims
  • Month 12-15: Prepare final accounting
  • Month 15-18: Petition for distribution, distribute assets, close estate

Complex estates can take 24-36 months or longer.
