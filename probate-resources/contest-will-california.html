How to Contest a Will in California

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 15 min read

Contesting a will in California is a serious legal matter that should only be pursued when you have valid grounds and substantial evidence. If you believe a loved one's will is invalid due to undue influence, lack of capacity, fraud, or improper execution, California law provides a process to challenge it—but the bar is high and the timeline is strict.

To contest a will in California, you must have legal standing (be an interested party), valid grounds for contest (undue influence, lack of capacity, fraud, duress, or improper execution), and file your contest within 120 days of the will being admitted to probate. Will contests are complex litigation that typically takes 1-3 years and requires substantial evidence.

This comprehensive guide explains the legal grounds for contesting a will, who can contest, the filing process, evidence requirements, and what to expect during California will contest proceedings.

Who Can Contest a Will in California?

Not everyone can challenge a will. California law requires "standing"—meaning you must be an "interested person" who would be affected by the will's validity.

People with Standing to Contest

  • Beneficiaries named in the will (if they received less than they believe they should)
  • Beneficiaries named in a previous will (if a newer will changed their inheritance)
  • Legal heirs who would inherit under California intestacy law if no will existed
  • Creditors of the estate (in limited circumstances)
  • Anyone with a property right or claim against the estate

People Without Standing

  • Friends or acquaintances not mentioned in the will
  • Distant relatives with no inheritance rights
  • People disinherited in all versions of the will
  • Anyone who wouldn't benefit from invalidating the will

Important: The court will dismiss your contest if you lack standing, regardless of your grounds.

Valid Grounds to Contest a Will in California

California Probate Code recognizes five primary grounds for will contests. You must prove at least one:

1. Lack of Testamentary Capacity

The testator (person making the will) must have been mentally competent when signing the will. California requires the testator to:

  • Understand they're creating a will
  • Know the nature and extent of their property
  • Understand who their natural heirs are (spouse, children, etc.)
  • Understand how the will distributes their property

Common scenarios:

  • Advanced dementia or Alzheimer's disease
  • Mental illness affecting judgment
  • Severe medication side effects
  • Being under anesthesia or heavily medicated

Evidence needed:

  • Medical records documenting cognitive impairment
  • Doctor testimony about mental state
  • Witness testimony about confusion or incompetence
  • Timeline showing progressive deterioration

2. Undue Influence

Undue influence occurs when someone exerts excessive pressure on the testator, overpowering their free will and causing them to make a will they wouldn't have otherwise made.

Factors California courts consider:

  • The beneficiary had a confidential relationship with the testator (caregiver, advisor, etc.)
  • The beneficiary actively participated in creating the will
  • The beneficiary received a disproportionate benefit
  • The testator was vulnerable due to age, illness, or dependency
  • The testator's wishes changed suddenly and dramatically
  • The beneficiary isolated the testator from family

Common scenarios:

  • Caregiver who isolated elderly person and pressured them to change will
  • New romantic partner who convinced testator to disinherit family
  • Adult child who moved in and manipulated dependent parent
  • Financial advisor who gained inappropriate influence

Evidence needed:

  • Testimony about suspicious circumstances
  • Evidence of isolation from family
  • Timeline showing sudden relationship and will change
  • Financial records showing control or transfers
  • Medical evidence of vulnerability

3. Fraud

Fraud occurs when someone intentionally deceives the testator about material facts, causing them to execute a will they wouldn't have otherwise made.

Types of fraud:

  • Fraud in the execution: Testator doesn't know they're signing a will (thinks it's another document)
  • Fraud in the inducement: Someone lies to testator about facts that influence the will's provisions

Examples:

  • Telling the testator one child is dead when they're alive
  • Forging signatures or altering the will
  • Lying about a beneficiary's actions to cause disinheritance
  • Tricking testator into signing will by claiming it's a different document

Evidence needed:

  • Proof of the false statements
  • Evidence testator relied on false information
  • Testimony from witnesses who heard the lies
  • Documentary evidence of deception

4. Duress

Duress involves threats or coercion that force the testator to sign a will against their true wishes.

Examples:

  • Threatening physical harm to testator or family members
  • Threatening to withhold care or medication
  • Threatening to abandon elderly testator
  • Using fear or intimidation to force signature

Evidence needed:

  • Testimony about threats or coercion
  • Evidence of testator's fear
  • Pattern of abuse or control
  • Contemporaneous complaints to others

5. Improper Execution (Technical Defects)

California has strict requirements for valid will execution. A will is invalid if it doesn't meet these formalities:

California Will Requirements:

  • Testator must be at least 18 years old
  • Will must be in writing
  • Testator must sign the will (or direct someone to sign on their behalf)
  • Two witnesses must sign the will
  • Witnesses must observe the testator sign (or acknowledge their signature)
  • Witnesses must understand it's a will
  • Witnesses must be "disinterested" (not beneficiaries)

Common technical defects:

  • Only one witness (or no witnesses)
  • Witnesses are beneficiaries
  • Signatures weren't witnessed properly
  • Will wasn't signed at all
  • Electronic signature issues (California generally requires physical signatures)

What Is NOT Valid Grounds to Contest a Will

Many people think they can contest a will for reasons that aren't legally sufficient:

  • "The will is unfair" – Courts don't judge fairness, only validity
  • "I deserve more" – You have no legal right to inherit
  • "I was left out" – People can disinherit anyone except spouses (who have separate protections)
  • "My sibling got more" – Unequal distribution alone isn't grounds for contest
  • "The testator would never do this" – Without evidence of incapacity, undue influence, etc., this isn't enough
  • "The executor is biased" – Executor bias is addressed separately from will validity

Time Limits to Contest a Will in California

California has strict deadlines for will contests:

Primary Deadline: 120 Days After Probate Opens

You have 120 days after the will is admitted to probate to file a will contest. This deadline starts when the court officially accepts the will and issues Letters Testamentary.

How the deadline works:

  1. Someone files a Petition for Probate with the will
  2. Notice is published and mailed to heirs
  3. Court holds a hearing (typically 30-45 days after filing)
  4. Judge admits will to probate
  5. 120-day clock starts

Before Probate Opens

You can also contest a will before probate opens by filing your own Petition for Probate with a different will (if you have one) or objecting when the initial petition is filed.

Missing the Deadline

If you miss the 120-day deadline, you likely lose your right to contest—with very limited exceptions:

  • You weren't given proper notice
  • You discovered fraud after the deadline
  • The court grants an extension for good cause (rare)

Bottom Line: If you're considering a contest, act quickly. Consult an attorney as soon as you learn of potential grounds.

How to Contest a Will: Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Consult a Probate Litigation Attorney

Will contests are complex litigation requiring specialized expertise. Before filing, consult an experienced probate litigation attorney to:

  • Evaluate whether you have valid grounds
  • Assess the strength of available evidence
  • Estimate costs and likelihood of success
  • Advise on strategy

Step 2: Gather Evidence

Build your case by collecting:

  • Medical records
  • Prior wills
  • Financial records
  • Communications (emails, texts, letters)
  • Witness statements
  • Photos or videos
  • Testimony from doctors, caregivers, family

Step 3: File a Will Contest

Your attorney files a formal objection to the will with the probate court. This typically involves:

  • Form DE-111: Petition for Probate (if you have a different will to offer)
  • Form DE-120: Notice of Hearing
  • Written objections stating your grounds
  • Supporting declarations from witnesses

Filing fees: Approximately $435-465 in Los Angeles County

Step 4: Serve Notice

You must provide legal notice to:

  • The executor named in the will
  • All beneficiaries
  • All legal heirs

Step 5: Discovery Process

Both sides exchange evidence through formal discovery:

  • Depositions: Sworn testimony from witnesses
  • Interrogatories: Written questions requiring written answers
  • Document requests: Formal demands for relevant documents
  • Subpoenas: Court orders for testimony or documents

Discovery typically takes 6-12 months.

Step 6: Mediation (Often Required)

Los Angeles County probate courts often require mediation before trial. A neutral mediator helps parties negotiate a settlement.

Many will contests settle during mediation, avoiding the cost and uncertainty of trial.

Step 7: Trial

If no settlement is reached, the case goes to trial. At trial:

  • Each side presents evidence and witness testimony
  • The judge (not a jury in probate court) evaluates credibility
  • The burden of proof depends on the grounds:

Burden of Proof:

  • Will proponent must prove the will was properly executed
  • Will contestant must prove fraud, undue influence, lack of capacity, or duress by "clear and convincing evidence" (a high standard)

Step 8: Court Decision

The judge issues a ruling:

  • Will upheld: Probate proceeds according to the contested will
  • Will invalidated: The estate proceeds under a prior will (if one exists) or intestacy law
  • Partial invalidation: Specific provisions are removed but the rest of the will stands

Step 9: Appeals (Potentially)

Either party can appeal the decision to the California Court of Appeal, adding another 1-2 years.

How Long Does a Will Contest Take?

  • Simple contest (settled quickly): 6-12 months
  • Contested through mediation and settlement: 12-18 months
  • Full trial: 18-36 months
  • With appeals: 3-5 years

How Much Does It Cost to Contest a Will?

Will contest costs vary widely based on complexity:

Attorney Fees:

  • Hourly rates: $350-600/hour for probate litigation attorneys
  • Retainer: $10,000-50,000 upfront
  • Total cost for trial: $50,000-200,000+

Other Costs:

  • Filing fees: $435-465
  • Expert witnesses: $5,000-20,000 (doctors, handwriting analysts, etc.)
  • Depositions and court reporters: $2,000-10,000
  • Investigation and discovery: $5,000-20,000

Fee Arrangements:

  • Hourly billing: Most common for will contests
  • Contingency fees: Rare, but some attorneys may agree (typically 33-40% of recovery)
  • Hybrid: Reduced hourly rate plus percentage of recovery

Can You Recover Attorney Fees?

California allows courts to award attorney fees from the estate if:

  • You prevail in your contest
  • The contest benefited the estate
  • The contest was brought in good faith

However, if you lose, you typically cannot recover fees and may have to pay your own costs.

What Happens to the Estate During a Contest?

When you file a will contest:

  • Probate isn't stopped: The executor can still perform basic duties
  • Distributions are delayed: The court won't authorize final distribution until the contest is resolved
  • Assets are preserved: The executor must maintain assets but can't dissipate them
  • Estate costs continue: Attorney fees, executor compensation, and expenses accumulate

No-Contest Clauses in California

Many wills include "no-contest clauses" (also called "in terrorem" clauses) stating that anyone who contests the will loses their inheritance.

Are They Enforceable?

California law limits no-contest clauses. They're only enforceable if:

  • The contest was filed without probable cause
  • The contestant had no reasonable belief in the contest's merit

Important: If you have legitimate evidence of undue influence, lack of capacity, or fraud, the no-contest clause likely won't apply. Consult an attorney before letting a no-contest clause deter you from a valid contest.

Alternatives to Full Will Contest

1. Settlement Negotiation

Often, parties can negotiate a settlement where contested beneficiaries receive less than the will provides but more than they'd get through intestacy, avoiding litigation.

2. Petition to Remove Executor

If your issue is with the executor's conduct rather than the will itself, petition to remove the executor instead of contesting the will.

3. Accounting Challenges

If you suspect the executor is mishandling assets, demand an accounting and challenge suspicious transactions.

4. Trust Contest (If Applicable)

If assets were in a living trust, you'd contest the trust rather than the will—a similar but separate process.
