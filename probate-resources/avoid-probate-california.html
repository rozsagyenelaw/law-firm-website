How to Avoid Probate in California: 7 Proven Strategies

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 12 min read

California probate is expensive, time-consuming, and completely public. The good news? With proper planning, most families can avoid it entirely.

Here are seven proven strategies to keep your estate out of probate court.

Why Avoid Probate?

Before diving into strategies, here's why California residents work so hard to avoid probate:

  • Cost: Statutory fees alone run 4-7% of gross estate value. A $750,000 estate pays roughly $36,000 just in attorney and executor fees.
  • Time: California probate takes 12-18 months minimum, often longer in Los Angeles County.
  • Public Record: Probate files are public. Anyone can see what you owned, who inherited it, and your family's private information.
  • Court Control: The court supervises everything, requiring approval for many routine decisions.

Strategy 1: Create a Living Trust

A revocable living trust is the most comprehensive way to avoid probate in California.

How it works: You transfer ownership of your assets to the trust during your lifetime. You remain in complete control as trustee. When you pass away, your successor trustee distributes assets according to your instructions—no court involvement required.

What it covers: Real estate, bank accounts, investments, business interests, vehicles, and personal property.

Cost: $2,000-$5,000 for a complete trust package, compared to $30,000+ in probate fees for a moderate estate.

Key requirement: The trust must be properly "funded"—assets must actually be transferred into trust ownership. An unfunded trust provides no probate avoidance.

Strategy 2: Use Beneficiary Designations

Many assets can pass directly to beneficiaries without probate simply by naming them on the account.

Assets with beneficiary designations:

  • Life insurance policies
  • 401(k) and IRA retirement accounts
  • Pension plans
  • Annuities
  • Health savings accounts (HSAs)

What to do: Review and update beneficiary designations regularly. Name primary and contingent beneficiaries. Make sure designations align with your overall estate plan.

Warning: Beneficiary designations override your will. If your will says your son inherits your IRA but your ex-spouse is still named as beneficiary, your ex-spouse gets the IRA.

Strategy 3: Add Transfer-on-Death (TOD) Designations

California allows TOD designations on several types of assets:

  • Vehicles: Complete a TOD beneficiary form with the DMV. The vehicle passes to your named beneficiary without probate.
  • Securities: Stocks, bonds, and brokerage accounts can include TOD beneficiary designations.
  • Real Estate: California's Revocable Transfer on Death Deed allows you to name a beneficiary for your home. Upon death, the property transfers automatically.
  • Bank Accounts: Payable-on-death (POD) designations work similarly—the account passes directly to your named beneficiary.

Strategy 4: Hold Property in Joint Tenancy

Property held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship passes automatically to the surviving owner(s) when one owner dies.

Common uses:

  • Married couples owning their home together
  • Parent adding adult child to property title
  • Business partners

Caution: Joint tenancy has drawbacks. Adding someone to your property title is a gift that may trigger tax consequences. The other owner gains immediate rights to the property. Creditors of the other owner could potentially reach the property.

For most families, a living trust offers better protection and flexibility than joint tenancy.

Strategy 5: Use California's Small Estate Procedures

If the estate is small enough, simplified procedures can avoid formal probate:

Small Estate Affidavit (Personal Property)
Estates with personal property valued at $184,500 or less (as of 2024) can be transferred using a simple affidavit. No court filing required.

Affidavit for Real Property of Small Value
Real estate valued at $69,625 or less (as of 2025) can be transferred by affidavit filed with the county recorder.

Spousal Property Petition
Surviving spouses can often transfer community property and certain other assets through a simplified court petition rather than full probate administration.

Strategy 6: Give Assets Away During Your Lifetime

Assets you don't own can't go through probate. Strategic lifetime gifting reduces your estate.

Annual gift tax exclusion: You can give up to $18,000 per person per year (2024) without gift tax implications.

Direct payments: Paying someone's tuition or medical bills directly to the institution doesn't count against gift limits.

Caution: Don't give away assets you might need. Never gift property just to qualify for Medicaid—there are significant penalties.

Strategy 7: Create a Pour-Over Will as Backup

Even with a trust, you need a "pour-over will" as a safety net. This will directs any assets not in your trust at death to be transferred ("poured over") into the trust.

Assets that pass through the pour-over will still go through probate, but this catches anything you forgot to transfer during your lifetime.

Which Strategy Is Right for You?

  • Single person with modest assets: TOD designations, beneficiary designations, and a simple will may suffice.
  • Homeowner or anyone with real estate: A living trust is almost always the best choice in California.
  • Married couples: Joint trusts, beneficiary designations, and TOD deeds provide comprehensive coverage.
  • Complex estates: Living trust with sub-trust provisions for tax planning, asset protection, or special needs planning.
