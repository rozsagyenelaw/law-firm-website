Died Without a Will in California: Who Inherits?

Rozsa Gyene Updated January 2025 11 min read

When someone dies without a will in California, their estate doesn't go to the state. Instead, California's intestate succession laws determine who inherits and how much they receive. If your loved one passed away without a will, you're probably wondering who gets what and how the process works.

When someone dies without a will in California, their assets pass to their closest living relatives according to a specific legal hierarchy: spouse, children, parents, siblings, and more distant relatives. The exact distribution depends on which relatives survive and whether property is community or separate.

This complete guide explains California's intestate succession laws, who inherits what, and how to navigate probate without a will.

What Is Intestate Succession?

"Intestate" means dying without a valid will. When this happens, California Probate Code Sections 6400-6414 provide default rules for who inherits. These rules attempt to distribute property the way most people would want—prioritizing spouses, children, and close family members.

However, intestate succession follows rigid formulas that may not match the deceased's actual wishes. This is why estate planning attorneys strongly recommend creating a will or trust.

Who Inherits When Someone Dies Without a Will in California?

The distribution depends on which family members survive and whether property is community or separate.

If You're Married or Have a Domestic Partner

Scenario 1: You have a spouse/partner but no children, parents, siblings, or their descendants

Your spouse inherits everything.

Scenario 2: You have a spouse/partner and one child (or that child's descendants)

  • Spouse gets all community property
  • Spouse gets 1/2 of separate property
  • Child gets 1/2 of separate property

Scenario 3: You have a spouse/partner and multiple children or grandchildren

  • Spouse gets all community property
  • Spouse gets 1/3 of separate property
  • Children share 2/3 of separate property equally

Scenario 4: You have a spouse/partner and parents (but no children)

  • Spouse gets all community property
  • Spouse gets 1/2 of separate property
  • Parents share 1/2 of separate property

Scenario 5: You have a spouse/partner and siblings (but no children or parents)

  • Spouse gets all community property
  • Spouse gets 1/2 of separate property
  • Siblings share 1/2 of separate property

If You're Not Married

If you're unmarried and have no domestic partner, your estate passes in this order:

1. To your children equally

If one of your children predeceased you but had their own children, those grandchildren inherit their parent's share.

2. To your parents equally

If both parents are alive, they split everything 50/50. If only one parent survives, they inherit everything.

3. To your siblings equally

Full siblings inherit. Half-siblings inherit only if there are no full siblings.

4. To your grandparents

If your parents and siblings are deceased, grandparents inherit.

5. To more distant relatives

If no close relatives exist, California law traces ancestry through aunts, uncles, cousins, and their descendants.

6. To the State of California

Only if absolutely no relatives can be found does property "escheat" to the state. This is extremely rare.

Community Property vs. Separate Property in California

Understanding the difference between community and separate property is critical to intestate succession because they're distributed differently.

Community Property

Property acquired during marriage through either spouse's earnings, including:

  • Wages and salary earned during marriage
  • Real estate purchased with marital funds
  • Business interests created during marriage
  • Bank accounts funded with marital earnings
  • Retirement accounts funded during marriage

Community property goes 100% to the surviving spouse when someone dies intestate.

Separate Property

Property that belongs to one spouse alone, including:

  • Property owned before marriage
  • Gifts and inheritances received by one spouse
  • Property acquired after permanent separation
  • Personal injury awards (compensation, not punitive damages)
  • Property designated as separate in a prenuptial agreement

Separate property is divided between the spouse and other heirs according to the scenarios above.

In practice, determining what's community vs. separate can be complicated, especially when separate property is commingled with community funds or when community funds improve separate property.

What About Stepchildren?

Under California intestacy law, stepchildren do not automatically inherit. Only biological children, adopted children, and (in some cases) children born through assisted reproduction inherit.

If you want stepchildren to inherit, you must:

  • Create a will naming them as beneficiaries
  • Legally adopt them
  • Put assets in a trust for their benefit

This is a common surprise that underscores the importance of estate planning for blended families.

Do Unmarried Partners Inherit?

No. California intestacy law does not recognize boyfriends, girlfriends, or fiancés—no matter how long you've been together. Unmarried partners inherit nothing unless:

  • You're registered as domestic partners with the State of California (same rights as marriage)
  • You created a will or trust naming them as beneficiaries
  • Property is held in joint tenancy or has a transfer-on-death designation

This is another critical reason for unmarried couples to have estate plans.

The Probate Process Without a Will

Dying without a will doesn't avoid probate. In fact, probate is often more complicated without a will because the court must:

  1. Determine who the legal heirs are
  2. Appoint an administrator (since no executor was named)
  3. Ensure proper distribution according to intestacy law

Step 1: Someone Petitions to Be Administrator

Without a will naming an executor, the court appoints an "administrator" to manage the estate. California law prioritizes applicants in this order:

  1. Surviving spouse or domestic partner
  2. Children
  3. Grandchildren
  4. Other relatives
  5. Public administrator (if no family member steps forward)
  6. Creditors (in rare cases)

Step 2: Notice to Heirs and Creditors

The administrator must notify all legal heirs and publish notice to creditors. This process takes several months.

Step 3: Inventory and Appraisal

The administrator identifies all assets, has them appraised, and files an inventory with the court within 4 months.

Step 4: Pay Debts and Taxes

Before distributing assets, the administrator must pay:

  • Funeral and burial expenses
  • Estate administration costs
  • Outstanding debts
  • Federal and state taxes

Step 5: Distribution to Heirs

After the creditor period (4 months) and after paying debts, the administrator petitions the court for permission to distribute assets according to intestacy law.

Step 6: Close the Estate

The administrator files a final accounting and the court discharges them from duty.

Timeline: Intestate probate typically takes 12-18 months in California, potentially longer with complications.

What Assets Avoid Probate Even Without a Will?

Not everything goes through probate—even without a will. These assets pass directly to beneficiaries:

  • Joint tenancy property: Real estate or bank accounts owned as joint tenants with right of survivorship automatically pass to the surviving owner
  • Living trusts: Assets titled in a revocable living trust avoid probate and pass to named beneficiaries
  • Payable-on-death (POD) accounts: Bank and credit union accounts with POD beneficiaries
  • Transfer-on-death (TOD) securities: Investment accounts with TOD designations
  • Life insurance: Proceeds paid to named beneficiaries
  • Retirement accounts: IRAs, 401(k)s, and other accounts with designated beneficiaries
  • Community property with right of survivorship: California allows this designation for real estate

These "non-probate" assets can constitute the majority of an estate's value, meaning less may go through intestate probate than you initially think.

Intestate Succession: Complex Scenarios

Adopted Children

Legally adopted children have the exact same inheritance rights as biological children. They inherit from their adoptive parents but typically not from their biological parents (unless the adoption didn't terminate parental rights).

Foster Children

Foster children do not automatically inherit. They must be legally adopted to have inheritance rights.

Children Born Out of Wedlock

Children born to unmarried parents inherit from both parents if paternity is established. Paternity can be proven through:

  • The father's signature on the birth certificate
  • A court-ordered paternity test
  • A declaration of paternity signed by both parents
  • Marriage to the mother after birth

Children Conceived After Death

California recognizes inheritance rights for children conceived through assisted reproduction after a parent's death, but only if specific requirements are met (genetic material preserved before death, written consent, etc.).

Half-Siblings

If you have no full siblings, half-siblings inherit. If you have both full and half-siblings, only full siblings inherit (half-siblings are excluded).

Estranged Relatives

Intestacy law makes no exception for estrangement. An estranged spouse or child still inherits according to law. This is a key reason to create a will if you wish to disinherit someone.

Can You Disinherit Someone in California?

You cannot disinherit someone under intestacy law—it follows fixed rules. However, you can create a will to intentionally exclude someone.

Important limitations:

  • You cannot disinherit a spouse without their consent. California community property laws and spousal protection statutes give surviving spouses strong rights.
  • You can disinherit children, but you must explicitly state your intent in a will. Simply omitting them isn't enough.
  • Anyone disinherited in a will has the right to contest it, though grounds for successful challenges are limited.

Advantages of Having a Will vs. Dying Intestate

While intestacy law provides default rules, a will offers significant advantages:

  • Control: You decide who gets what, rather than the state
  • Flexibility: You can give specific items to specific people
  • Include non-relatives: Leave property to friends, charities, or unmarried partners
  • Name an executor: Choose someone you trust rather than having the court appoint someone
  • Guardianship: Nominate guardians for minor children
  • Disinherit: Exclude people you don't want to inherit
  • Tax planning: Structure bequests to minimize taxes
  • Avoid disputes: Clear instructions reduce family conflicts

What If You Can't Find a Will?

Sometimes families believe a will existed but can't locate it. In California:

  • Check with the deceased's attorney
  • Search safe deposit boxes and home safes
  • Check with the county clerk (some counties allow will filing during lifetime)
  • Search computer files for electronic copies

If a will truly cannot be found, the estate proceeds as if the person died intestate. However, if there's evidence a will once existed but was deliberately destroyed, the court may presume the person revoked it intentionally.

Common Misconceptions About Dying Without a Will

Myth 1: "Everything goes to the state."

False. California only inherits if absolutely no relatives exist—extremely rare. Your estate will almost certainly pass to family members.

Myth 2: "My spouse gets everything automatically."

Not necessarily. Your spouse shares separate property with children, parents, or siblings depending on who survives.

Myth 3: "Dying without a will avoids probate."

False. Most estates without wills still go through probate. In fact, intestate probate is often more complicated.

Myth 4: "My oldest child automatically becomes administrator."

False. Any child can petition, and the court decides who to appoint based on who's most qualified and willing.

Myth 5: "My domestic partner gets nothing."

Registered domestic partners in California have the same rights as spouses. Unregistered partners, however, inherit nothing under intestacy law.

What to Do If Your Loved One Died Without a Will

If you're handling an estate without a will:

  1. Secure the property: Change locks, protect valuables, maintain insurance
  2. Locate all assets: Bank statements, deeds, investment accounts, etc.
  3. Identify all heirs: Make a list of everyone entitled to inherit under intestacy law
  4. Consult a probate attorney: California intestacy law is complex, and an attorney can advise on the correct distribution
  5. File a petition for administration: If you want to serve as administrator, file with the probate court
  6. Don't distribute anything yet: Wait until the court authorizes distribution—premature distributions can create liability
