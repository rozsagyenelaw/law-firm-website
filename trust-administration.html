What is Trust Administration?

Trust administration is the legal process of managing and distributing trust assets after the trustor's death. Unlike probate, trust administration is typically faster, more private, and less expensive - but it still requires careful attention to legal requirements.

In California, where a single family home often exceeds $1 million in value, proper trust administration is crucial. As a successor trustee, you have important fiduciary duties to the beneficiaries. Mistakes can lead to personal liability, disputes, and legal complications. Professional guidance ensures you fulfill your duties properly while protecting yourself.

Typically completed in 4-12 months
Private process - no court records
Cost-effective compared to probate
Protects trustees from liability

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about trust administration in California

How long does trust administration take in California?

Trust administration in California typically takes 4-12 months, depending on the complexity of the estate. Simple trusts with liquid assets and no disputes can be completed in 4-6 months. More complex estates with real property, business interests, or beneficiary disputes may take 9-12 months or longer. The timeline is much faster than probate, which typically takes 12-18 months minimum.

What does a successor trustee do in California?

A successor trustee manages trust assets after the grantor's death. Key responsibilities include: notifying beneficiaries within 60 days, inventorying and valuing all trust assets, paying valid debts and taxes, filing required tax returns, managing trust property, and distributing assets to beneficiaries according to the trust terms. The successor trustee has fiduciary duties and can be held personally liable for mistakes or breaches of duty.

How much does trust administration cost in California?

Trust administration costs in California typically range from 1-4% of the estate value for attorney fees. This is significantly less expensive than probate, which costs 4-7% in statutory fees alone. Additional costs may include: accounting fees ($500-$2,000), appraisal fees ($300-$800 per property), trustee compensation (typically 1% or hourly rate), and tax preparation fees ($500-$3,000). We offer both regular retainer and contingency fee arrangements.

What are a successor trustee's first steps after death?

Immediate first steps for a successor trustee include: 1) Secure the deceased's property and change locks if necessary, 2) Locate the original trust document, 3) Order 10-15 certified death certificates, 4) Obtain a tax ID number (EIN) for the trust, 5) Open a trust estate bank account, 6) Notify all beneficiaries within 60 days as required by California law, 7) Begin inventorying trust assets, and 8) Consult with an experienced trust administration attorney to ensure compliance with all legal requirements.

Do you need a lawyer for trust administration in California?

While not legally required, hiring an attorney for trust administration is strongly recommended. California trust law is complex, and trustees can be held personally liable for mistakes. An attorney helps with: understanding fiduciary duties, meeting California's 60-day notice requirements, proper asset valuation and distribution, tax compliance, creditor claims, beneficiary disputes, and protecting the trustee from personal liability. Most trustees find the attorney's guidance invaluable and cost-effective compared to potential liability.

What are trust beneficiary rights in California?

California trust beneficiaries have significant legal rights including: 1) Right to receive the 60-day notice after grantor's death, 2) Right to receive a copy of the trust document, 3) Right to receive accountings showing all trust transactions, 4) Right to be kept reasonably informed about trust administration, 5) Right to distributions according to trust terms, 6) Right to petition court if trustee breaches fiduciary duties, and 7) Right to sue for damages if trustee mismanages assets. These rights are protected under California Probate Code Sections 16060-16069.

What is the 60-day notice requirement for California trusts?

California Probate Code Section 16061.7 requires trustees to serve a written notice to all beneficiaries and heirs within 60 days after the grantor's death or after the trustee accepts the role. The notice must include: the trustee's name and contact information, notification that the person is serving as trustee, the right to request a copy of the trust, and information about the 120-day period to contest the trust. Failure to provide proper notice can extend beneficiaries' time to bring legal challenges.

How is a successor trustee compensated in California?

California law allows trustees to receive "reasonable compensation" for their services. Typical compensation is: 1-2% of trust assets for professional trustees, or hourly rates of $50-$150 for individual trustees performing ordinary services. The trust document may specify different compensation. Trustees can also be reimbursed for reasonable expenses. If the trustee is also a beneficiary, they should carefully consider whether to take fees, as trustee fees are taxable income while inheritances are tax-free.

What if a trustee is not distributing trust assets?

If a trustee is not distributing trust assets as required, beneficiaries have several options: 1) Request a written explanation for the delay, 2) Demand a formal accounting, 3) Send a formal demand letter (often through an attorney), 4) File a petition with the probate court to compel distribution, 5) Petition to remove the trustee for breach of fiduciary duty, and 6) Sue for damages if the delay caused financial harm. California law requires trustees to make distributions within a reasonable time after all debts and taxes are paid.

Is trust administration easier than probate in California?

Yes, trust administration is significantly easier than probate in California. Key advantages: no court supervision required, faster timeline (4-12 months vs 12-18+ months), completely private (no public records), lower costs (1-4% vs 4-7%+ in fees), more flexible for trustees, and immediate access to trust assets. However, trustees still have serious fiduciary responsibilities and must comply with California trust law requirements including the 60-day notice, proper accounting, and prudent asset management.

Trustee Not Distributing Trust California - What Can Beneficiaries Do?

If a California trustee is not distributing trust assets when required, beneficiaries have several legal remedies. Trustees must distribute assets according to the trust terms and within a reasonable time - typically within 12-18 months of the grantor's death for outright distributions. Unreasonable delays constitute a breach of fiduciary duty under California Probate Code Section 16000.

What to do if trustee not communicating with beneficiaries California: First, send a formal written demand for distribution via certified mail with return receipt. Request a full accounting showing all trust transactions, assets, income, expenses, and explain why distribution is delayed. California Probate Code Section 16061 requires trustees to keep beneficiaries reasonably informed. Document all communication attempts - save emails, letters, and phone logs.

If the trustee continues refusing to distribute or communicate, beneficiaries can file a petition in probate court to: 1) Compel distribution, 2) Compel accounting, 3) Remove trustee for breach of duty, 4) Surcharge trustee (hold personally liable for damages), 5) Recover attorney fees from trust. Beneficiaries also can demand to see the trust document under Probate Code Section 16061.7 - trustees cannot refuse this request. 60-Day Notice to Beneficiaries California (Probate Code 16061.7)

California Probate Code Section 16061.7 requires successor trustees to serve a written notice to all beneficiaries and heirs within 60 days after the trustor's death or after the trustee accepts the trusteeship. This is one of the most important - and most commonly missed - requirements in California trust administration.

What must the 60-day notice include? The notice must contain: 1) Trustee's name, address, and phone number, 2) Statement that the person is acting as trustee, 3) Notification of the right to request a copy of the trust terms, 4) Information about the 120-day period to contest the trust, 5) Physical address of the administration location. The notice must be served by mail to all current beneficiaries, deceased trustor's heirs at law (even if not named in trust), and any previous beneficiaries whose interests have terminated.

Consequences of failing to send 60-day notice: If a trustee fails to provide proper notice, beneficiaries' time to challenge the trust does NOT begin to run. This means beneficiaries could have years instead of 120 days to contest the trust or sue for breach of duty. Courts can also remove trustees who fail to comply with notice requirements and surcharge them for damages. Many successor trustees attempt to "do it themselves" without legal guidance, and failing to send proper 60-day notice is the #1 mistake. Our flat-fee trust administration service ensures all statutory notices are properly prepared and timely served, protecting both trustees and beneficiaries. We prepare the notice, handle service, and document compliance - giving trustees peace of mind.

How to Remove Trustee in California - Breach of Fiduciary Duty

California law allows beneficiaries to petition the probate court to remove a trustee for breach of fiduciary duty under Probate Code Section 15642. Common grounds for removal include: trustee not distributing trust assets, mismanagement of trust property, self-dealing, failing to provide accountings, trustee not communicating with beneficiaries, commingling trust funds with personal funds, making unauthorized investments, conflicts of interest, and failure to follow trust terms.

The trustee breach of fiduciary duty California petition process: Beneficiaries must file a Petition for Removal of Trustee with the probate court, stating specific grounds and providing evidence. Courts consider: severity of breach, whether breach caused financial harm, whether continuing in office would be detrimental to trust, and whether less drastic remedies would suffice. Evidence needed includes: denied accounting requests, proof of self-dealing transactions, documentation of mismanagement, financial records showing losses, communications showing refusal to distribute, and expert testimony about proper trust administration.

How long does trustee have to distribute assets in California? While no absolute deadline exists, trustees must distribute within a "reasonable time" - typically 12-18 months for simple estates. Longer delays require justification (pending litigation, tax issues, illiquid assets). If a trustee is taking too long without valid reasons, beneficiaries can petition for removal AND surcharge (personal liability for damages caused by delay). We represent both beneficiaries seeking trustee removal and trustees defending against removal petitions. California probate judges take fiduciary breaches seriously - trustees who ignore their duties face removal, surcharge, denial of compensation, and payment of beneficiaries' attorney fees.

Trust Beneficiary Rights California - Accounting & Communication

California trust beneficiaries have extensive legal rights that many people don't know about. Understanding these rights is crucial for protecting your inheritance. Can a beneficiary demand trust accounting in California? Absolutely. Under Probate Code Section 16062, any current beneficiary can demand a full accounting showing all trust transactions, assets, income, expenses, gains, losses, and distributions. Trustees must provide accounting within 60 days of a proper written request.

California trust beneficiary rights include: 1) Right to receive 60-day notice after grantor's death (Probate Code 16061.7), 2) Right to receive copy of trust document (cannot be denied), 3) Right to demand annual or final accountings, 4) Right to be kept reasonably informed about trust administration, 5) Right to distributions according to trust terms, 6) Right to object to trustee actions, 7) Right to petition court if trustee breaches duties, 8) Right to sue for damages from mismanagement, 9) Right to remove trustee for cause. Trustees owe beneficiaries fiduciary duties of loyalty, impartiality, prudence, and full disclosure.

Can beneficiary see trust document California? Yes - trustees MUST provide a complete copy upon request. Some trustees wrongly believe they can withhold trust provisions or only show beneficiaries "their portion." This is illegal. Beneficiaries entitled to see entire trust, all amendments, and any related estate planning documents. If trustee refuses, beneficiaries can petition court to compel disclosure and recover attorney fees. Our firm helps beneficiaries enforce their rights when trustees refuse to communicate, won't provide accountings, deny access to trust documents, or fail to make required distributions. We also advise trustees on their disclosure obligations to avoid liability. Most trust disputes arise from poor communication - proper transparency prevents litigation.

Flat Fee Trust Administration vs Hourly Billing - Which is Better?

When hiring an affordable trust administration attorney Glendale or anywhere in California, understanding fee structures is critical. Flat fee trust administration means you pay a fixed amount (typically 1-2% of estate value) for complete administration services from start to finish. Hourly billing means paying $300-$500+ per hour for every email, phone call, document review, and court appearance - costs can quickly spiral into $10,000-$50,000+ on complex estates.

Benefits of flat fee trust administration California: Predictable costs with no surprises, incentive for attorney to work efficiently, comprehensive services included (60-day notices, accounting, tax returns, distributions), no "bill shock" from unexpected complexity, and ability to budget estate expenses upfront. Many trustees prefer flat fees because they know exactly what legal services will cost - critical when managing trust assets and planning distributions. Hourly billing can be better for very simple estates (under $200,000) where only limited legal advice is needed, not full administration.

What are reasonable trust administration fees California? Typical ranges: Attorney fees 1-3% of estate value (vs. 4-7% for probate), Accountant fees $500-$2,000, Appraisals $300-$800 per property, Tax preparation $500-$3,000, Trustee compensation 1% or hourly. Total trust administration costs typically 2-5% of estate - substantially less than probate's 6-10% total cost. Low cost trust administration California is possible by: using flat-fee attorneys, successor trustee handling administrative tasks themselves under attorney supervision, avoiding unnecessary appraisals, filing tax returns in-house if qualified, and preventing beneficiary disputes through good communication.
