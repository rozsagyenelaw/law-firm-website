What is Trust Administration?

Trust administration is the legal process of managing and distributing trust assets after the trustor's death. Unlike probate, trust administration is typically faster, more private, and less expensive - but it still requires careful attention to legal requirements.

In California, where a single family home often exceeds $1 million in value, proper trust administration is crucial. As a successor trustee, you have important fiduciary duties to the beneficiaries. Mistakes can lead to personal liability, disputes, and legal complications. Professional guidance ensures you fulfill your duties properly while protecting yourself.

Typically completed in 4-12 months
Private process - no court records
Cost-effective compared to probate
Protects trustees from liability

The Trust Administration Process

A step-by-step guide through trust administration

Immediate Actions

Initial Steps

Obtain death certificate, locate trust documents, secure assets, and notify beneficiaries of their status.

30-60 Days

Asset Inventory

Identify and value all trust assets, obtain appraisals, gather financial statements, and create comprehensive inventory.

60-90 Days

Creditors & Taxes

Pay valid debts, file final tax returns, obtain tax clearance, and handle any outstanding financial obligations.

90-120 Days

Distribution

Distribute assets according to trust terms, obtain receipts from beneficiaries, and prepare final accounting.

120+ Days

Trust Closure

Complete final distributions, close trust accounts, file final documents, and obtain releases from beneficiaries.

Trustee Responsibilities

Your legal duties as a successor trustee

Fiduciary Duty

Act in the best interests of beneficiaries, avoid conflicts of interest, and manage trust assets prudently.

Notice Requirements

Notify all beneficiaries and heirs of the trust administration within 60 days as required by California law.

Accounting

Keep detailed records of all transactions and provide accountings to beneficiaries upon request.

Tax Obligations

File final income tax returns, estate tax returns if required, and obtain tax identification number for trust.

Asset Management

Protect and maintain trust property, collect income, pay expenses, and invest assets prudently.

Communication

Keep beneficiaries reasonably informed about trust administration and respond to reasonable requests.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about trust administration in California

How long does trust administration take in California?

Trust administration in California typically takes 4-12 months, depending on the complexity of the estate. Simple trusts with liquid assets and no disputes can be completed in 4-6 months. More complex estates with real property, business interests, or beneficiary disputes may take 9-12 months or longer. The timeline is much faster than probate, which typically takes 12-18 months minimum.

What does a successor trustee do in California?

A successor trustee manages trust assets after the grantor's death. Key responsibilities include: notifying beneficiaries within 60 days, inventorying and valuing all trust assets, paying valid debts and taxes, filing required tax returns, managing trust property, and distributing assets to beneficiaries according to the trust terms. The successor trustee has fiduciary duties and can be held personally liable for mistakes or breaches of duty.

How much does trust administration cost in California?

Trust administration costs in California typically range from 1-4% of the estate value for attorney fees. This is significantly less expensive than probate, which costs 4-7% in statutory fees alone. Additional costs may include: accounting fees ($500-$2,000), appraisal fees ($300-$800 per property), trustee compensation (typically 1% or hourly rate), and tax preparation fees ($500-$3,000). We offer both regular retainer and contingency fee arrangements.

What are a successor trustee's first steps after death?

Immediate first steps for a successor trustee include: 1) Secure the deceased's property and change locks if necessary, 2) Locate the original trust document, 3) Order 10-15 certified death certificates, 4) Obtain a tax ID number (EIN) for the trust, 5) Open a trust estate bank account, 6) Notify all beneficiaries within 60 days as required by California law, 7) Begin inventorying trust assets, and 8) Consult with an experienced trust administration attorney to ensure compliance with all legal requirements.

Do you need a lawyer for trust administration in California?

While not legally required, hiring an attorney for trust administration is strongly recommended. California trust law is complex, and trustees can be held personally liable for mistakes. An attorney helps with: understanding fiduciary duties, meeting California's 60-day notice requirements, proper asset valuation and distribution, tax compliance, creditor claims, beneficiary disputes, and protecting the trustee from personal liability. Most trustees find the attorney's guidance invaluable and cost-effective compared to potential liability.

What are trust beneficiary rights in California?

California trust beneficiaries have significant legal rights including: 1) Right to receive the 60-day notice after grantor's death, 2) Right to receive a copy of the trust document, 3) Right to receive accountings showing all trust transactions, 4) Right to be kept reasonably informed about trust administration, 5) Right to distributions according to trust terms, 6) Right to petition court if trustee breaches fiduciary duties, and 7) Right to sue for damages if trustee mismanages assets. These rights are protected under California Probate Code Sections 16060-16069.

What is the 60-day notice requirement for California trusts?

California Probate Code Section 16061.7 requires trustees to serve a written notice to all beneficiaries and heirs within 60 days after the grantor's death or after the trustee accepts the role. The notice must include: the trustee's name and contact information, notification that the person is serving as trustee, the right to request a copy of the trust, and information about the 120-day period to contest the trust. Failure to provide proper notice can extend beneficiaries' time to bring legal challenges.

How is a successor trustee compensated in California?

California law allows trustees to receive "reasonable compensation" for their services. Typical compensation is: 1-2% of trust assets for professional trustees, or hourly rates of $50-$150 for individual trustees performing ordinary services. The trust document may specify different compensation. Trustees can also be reimbursed for reasonable expenses. If the trustee is also a beneficiary, they should carefully consider whether to take fees, as trustee fees are taxable income while inheritances are tax-free.

What if a trustee is not distributing trust assets?

If a trustee is not distributing trust assets as required, beneficiaries have several options: 1) Request a written explanation for the delay, 2) Demand a formal accounting, 3) Send a formal demand letter (often through an attorney), 4) File a petition with the probate court to compel distribution, 5) Petition to remove the trustee for breach of fiduciary duty, and 6) Sue for damages if the delay caused financial harm. California law requires trustees to make distributions within a reasonable time after all debts and taxes are paid.

Is trust administration easier than probate in California?

Yes, trust administration is significantly easier than probate in California. Key advantages: no court supervision required, faster timeline (4-12 months vs 12-18+ months), completely private (no public records), lower costs (1-4% vs 4-7%+ in fees), more flexible for trustees, and immediate access to trust assets. However, trustees still have serious fiduciary responsibilities and must comply with California trust law requirements including the 60-day notice, proper accounting, and prudent asset management.
