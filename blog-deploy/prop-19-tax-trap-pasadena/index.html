Understanding Prop 19: What Changed

To understand why Prop 19 is so damaging, you need to understand what came before it.

The Old Rules (Prop 13 + Prop 58)

Since 1978, California's Proposition 13 has limited property tax increases to 2% per year, regardless of how much a property's market value increases. This created enormous disparities between long-time homeowners (paying taxes on decades-old assessed values) and new buyers (paying taxes on current purchase prices).

Then came Proposition 58 (1986), which allowed parents to transfer their primary residence to their children without any reassessment—regardless of value. Children could also receive up to $1 million in assessed value of other properties (like rental homes) without reassessment.

For Pasadena families, this was transformative. A home purchased in 1975 for $75,000 might now be worth $2.5 million, but the property taxes remained based on that original purchase price (plus 2% annual increases). When parents passed away, children could inherit the home and keep the same low tax basis.

The New Rules (Prop 19)

Proposition 19, passed in November 2020 and effective February 2021, dramatically changed these rules:

  1. Primary residence requirement: Children can only exclude inherited property from reassessment if they use it as their primary residence
  2. One-year deadline: The child must move into the property within one year of transfer
  3. Value cap: Even if the child moves in, any appreciation above the "exclusion amount" gets added to the assessed value
  4. No more rental properties: Inherited rental properties, vacation homes, and second homes are fully reassessed

The Bottom Line: Under the old rules, a child could inherit a $2.5 million Pasadena home and keep paying property taxes based on a $75,000 assessed value. Under Prop 19, that same child—unless they move into the home as their primary residence—will see property taxes jump to the current market value. That's the difference between paying ~$2,000/year and ~$31,000/year in property taxes.

The 2025 Prop 19 Exclusion Amount: $1,044,586

Here's where 2025 becomes particularly important. Even if a child does move into an inherited home as their primary residence, Prop 19 limits how much appreciation can be excluded from reassessment.

For 2025, the California State Board of Equalization has set this "exclusion amount" at $1,044,586.

How the Exclusion Works

When a child inherits a parent's primary residence and moves into it as their own primary residence:

  1. Compare the property's current market value to its assessed value (the taxable value)
  2. If the difference is $1,044,586 or less, the child keeps the parent's low tax basis
  3. If the difference exceeds $1,044,586, the excess is added to the assessed value

Let's see how this plays out for typical Pasadena properties:

Example 1: Modest Pasadena Home

Original purchase price (1985) $180,000
Current assessed value $390,000
Current market value $1,350,000
Appreciation above assessed value $960,000
2025 exclusion amount $1,044,586

Result: Since the appreciation ($960,000) is less than the exclusion ($1,044,586), the child who moves in keeps the full low tax basis. Annual property taxes remain approximately $4,875.

Example 2: Historic Old Pasadena Home

Original purchase price (1975) $85,000
Current assessed value $225,000
Current market value $2,400,000
Appreciation above assessed value $2,175,000
Excess above 2025 exclusion $1,130,414

Result: Even if the child moves in, the new assessed value becomes $225,000 + $1,130,414 = $1,355,414. Annual property taxes jump from approximately $2,813 to approximately $16,943—an increase of over $14,000 per year.

Example 3: San Marino-Adjacent Estate

Original purchase price (1970) $120,000
Current assessed value $290,000
Current market value $4,200,000
Appreciation above assessed value $3,910,000
Excess above 2025 exclusion $2,865,414

Result: New assessed value = $290,000 + $2,865,414 = $3,155,414. Annual property taxes increase from approximately $3,625 to approximately $39,443—an increase of nearly $36,000 per year.

The Cruel Math: For many Pasadena families, especially those with historic estates, the property tax increase alone can exceed what they pay in mortgage, forcing them to sell homes that have been in the family for generations.

What If the Child Doesn't Move In?

The examples above assume the child moves into the inherited home as their primary residence. But what if they can't—or don't want to?

Full Reassessment Scenarios

If the inheriting child already has their own home, lives in another city, or wants to keep the inherited property as a rental, the property faces full reassessment at current market value. No exclusion applies.

Using Example 3 above (the $4.2 million estate), full reassessment means:

  • Old annual property taxes: ~$3,625
  • New annual property taxes: ~$52,500
  • Annual increase: ~$48,875

That's over $48,000 per year in additional property taxes—$4,000+ per month—just to keep a property the family has owned for 55 years.

The Forced Sale Reality

For many Pasadena families, these numbers make keeping inherited property impossible. Adult children who have their own homes and mortgages simply cannot absorb $20,000-$50,000 in additional annual property taxes. The result: forced sales of historic family properties that have been treasured for generations.

Real Impact: I've worked with Pasadena families who had to sell Craftsman homes that had been in their families since the 1920s. Properties that survived the Great Depression, World War II, and every economic downturn since—lost because of a property tax change most people never saw coming.

Estate Planning Strategies for Prop 19

While Prop 19 cannot be avoided entirely, proper estate planning can help minimize its impact. As an experienced estate planning attorney, here are the strategies I discuss with Pasadena families:

Strategy 1: Lifetime Transfers (With Caution)

Parents can transfer property to children during their lifetime rather than at death. However, this comes with significant considerations:

  • Gift tax implications: Transfers may use gift/estate tax exemption
  • Loss of step-up in basis: Children lose the income tax benefit of inheriting at death
  • Loss of control: Once transferred, parents no longer own the property
  • Medicaid planning: May affect qualification for long-term care benefits

This strategy works best when parents are confident they won't need to sell the property for their own care and when the income tax implications are carefully analyzed.

Strategy 2: Creating an Affordable Living Trust

While a living trust doesn't avoid Prop 19 reassessment, it provides essential benefits that support other Prop 19 strategies:

  • Avoids probate: Property transfers immediately without 18-24 months in court
  • Faster timeline: Children can make the primary residence decision within the one-year window
  • Clear instructions: Trust specifies who receives property and any conditions
  • Flexibility: Trust can be structured to support various Prop 19 strategies

Without a trust, property must go through probate, which can take 18-24 months in Los Angeles County. This delay can complicate meeting Prop 19's one-year primary residence deadline and leave the property in limbo. To avoid probate in California, a living trust is essential.

Strategy 3: Planning for the Primary Residence Exclusion

If a child will make the inherited property their primary residence, planning ahead can maximize the exclusion:

  • Timing considerations: Understanding when to sell a current home vs. when to move into the inherited property
  • Documentation: Ensuring proper paperwork to claim the exclusion
  • Multiple children: Planning when only one child will occupy the property

Strategy 4: Insurance and Financial Planning

For families who want to preserve property for children who won't move in, financial planning may help:

  • Life insurance: Providing funds to cover increased property taxes
  • Reserve accounts: Setting aside funds specifically for property tax increases
  • Rental income analysis: Determining if rental income can cover new tax burden

Strategy 5: Entity Structuring

In some cases, LLCs or other entities may provide planning opportunities, particularly for:

  • Rental properties
  • Multi-generational family ownership
  • Properties with business use

These strategies are complex and require careful analysis with both legal and tax professionals.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Prop 19 exclusion amount for 2025?

For 2025, the Prop 19 inflation-adjusted exclusion amount is $1,044,586. This means if you inherit your parent's home and it has appreciated more than $1,044,586 above its assessed value, you will face property tax reassessment on the amount exceeding this threshold. The threshold is adjusted annually for inflation by the California State Board of Equalization.

How does Prop 19 affect inherited property in Pasadena?

Prop 19 significantly impacts inherited Pasadena properties because of the area's high home values. Before Prop 19, children could inherit a parent's home and keep the low property tax basis regardless of market value. Now, unless the child moves into the home as their primary residence within one year, the property will be fully reassessed at current market value. Even if the child does move in, any appreciation above $1,044,586 (2025) will be added to the assessed value.

Can a living trust help reduce Prop 19 property tax impact?

A living trust alone does not exempt you from Prop 19, but proper estate planning can help minimize its impact. Strategies include transferring property during your lifetime, ensuring beneficiary designations support your child's ability to claim the primary residence exclusion, coordinating the timing of transfers, and structuring the trust to maximize available exclusions. An experienced estate planning attorney can analyze your specific situation and recommend the best approach.

What happens if I inherit my parent's Pasadena home but don't move in?

If you inherit your parent's Pasadena home and don't make it your primary residence within one year, the property will be fully reassessed at current market value. For a home purchased decades ago with a low Prop 13 basis, this can mean property taxes increasing from a few thousand dollars annually to $20,000-$40,000 or more. Many heirs are forced to sell inherited homes because they cannot afford the new tax burden.

Does Prop 19 apply to properties held in a trust?

Yes, Prop 19 applies to properties held in trusts just as it does to properties held directly. When the trust settlor (creator) dies and the property passes to beneficiaries, Prop 19 rules determine whether the property will be reassessed. The key factors are whether the beneficiary is a qualifying child or grandchild, whether they will use the property as their primary residence, and whether the appreciation exceeds the $1,044,586 threshold (2025). Having a trust helps avoid probate but does not avoid Prop 19 reassessment.
