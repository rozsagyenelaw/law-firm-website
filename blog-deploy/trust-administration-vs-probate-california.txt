7:T33a6,<p>When someone dies in California, their assets must be transferred to beneficiaries. How that happens depends on whether they had a living trust or just a will.</p>
<p>Understanding the differences between trust administration and probate helps families know what to expect‚Äîand shows why estate planning matters.</p>
<h2>The Fundamental Difference</h2>
<p><strong>Probate:</strong> A court-supervised process to validate a will and distribute assets. Required when someone dies with assets in their name alone and no trust.</p>
<p><strong>Trust Administration:</strong> A private process where a successor trustee distributes assets according to trust terms. No court involvement required.</p>
<p>Both accomplish the same goal‚Äîtransferring assets to beneficiaries‚Äîbut through very different paths.</p>

<h2>Privacy Comparison</h2>
<h3>Probate: Completely Public</h3>
<p>Everything filed in probate becomes public record:</p>
<ul>
<li>The will</li>
<li>List of all assets and values</li>
<li>Names and addresses of beneficiaries</li>
<li>Creditor claims</li>
<li>Disputes and litigation</li>
<li>Final distribution details</li>
</ul>
<p>Anyone can go to the courthouse and see what you owned and who received it.</p>
<p><strong>Real-world consequence:</strong> "Heir hunters," scammers, and salespeople regularly search probate records to target beneficiaries.</p>
<h3>Trust Administration: Completely Private</h3>
<p>Trust administration happens outside the court system:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trust document is not filed publicly</li>
<li>Asset lists remain private</li>
<li>Beneficiary information stays confidential</li>
<li>Only involved parties know the details</li>
</ul>
<p>The only people who see trust information are those entitled to it under California law (beneficiaries, heirs who receive the required notice).</p>
<h2>Control and Flexibility</h2>
<h3>Probate: Court Controlled</h3>
<p>The executor must:</p>
<ul>
<li>Follow statutory procedures exactly</li>
<li>Get court approval for many actions</li>
<li>File regular reports with the court</li>
<li>Attend multiple hearings</li>
<li>Wait for court schedules</li>
</ul>
<p>Even with Independent Administration authority (IAEA), some actions require court involvement.</p>
<h3>Trust Administration: Trustee Controlled</h3>
<p>The trustee:</p>
<ul>
<li>Follows trust terms (not court procedures)</li>
<li>Makes most decisions independently</li>
<li>Doesn't need court approval for routine matters</li>
<li>Works on their own timeline</li>
<li>Has flexibility to respond to circumstances</li>
</ul>
<p>The trust document‚Äînot a judge‚Äîcontrols the process.</p>
<h2>Out-of-State Property</h2>
<h3>Probate: Multiple Proceedings</h3>
<p>If someone dies owning real estate in multiple states, their family faces:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Domiciliary probate:</strong> In the state where they lived</li>
<li><strong>Ancillary probate:</strong> In each additional state where they owned property</li>
</ul>
<p>Each ancillary probate means:</p>
<ul>
<li>Hiring local attorneys</li>
<li>Filing separate court cases</li>
<li>Paying additional fees</li>
<li>Extended timelines</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> California resident owns a vacation home in Arizona. Family must complete full probate in California AND separate probate in Arizona.</p>
<h3>Trust Administration: One Process</h3>
<p>A properly funded living trust avoids this entirely:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trust covers property in all states</li>
<li>One administration process</li>
<li>One set of professionals</li>
<li>No ancillary proceedings</li>
</ul>
<p>The successor trustee simply transfers property in each state according to trust terms.</p>
<h2>Incapacity Planning</h2>
<h3>Will Only: No Protection</h3>
<p>A will only takes effect at death. It provides no help if you become incapacitated while alive.</p>
<p>Without a trust, if you become unable to manage your affairs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Family may need court conservatorship</li>
<li>Conservatorship is expensive and intrusive</li>
<li>Court supervises all financial decisions</li>
<li>Annual accountings required</li>
</ul>
<h3>Living Trust: Built-In Protection</h3>
<p>A living trust includes incapacity planning:</p>
<ul>
<li>If you become incapacitated, successor trustee takes over</li>
<li>No court proceeding needed</li>
<li>Seamless transition of management</li>
<li>Privacy maintained</li>
</ul>
<p>This is a significant benefit many people overlook when comparing trusts to wills.</p>
<h2>When Probate Is Unavoidable</h2>
<p>Even with a trust, you might face probate if:</p>
<h3>Assets weren't transferred to the trust</h3>
<p>A trust only controls assets actually transferred to it. Property left in your individual name goes through probate.</p>
<h3>Small amounts left outside trust</h3>
<p>If amounts are under $184,500, simplified procedures may apply‚Äîbut it's still a form of probate.</p>
<h3>Trust is invalid</h3>
<p>If the trust wasn't properly executed, assets may go through probate.</p>
<h2>When Trust Administration Has Court Involvement</h2>
<p>While most trust administration is private, courts get involved for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trust contests by beneficiaries or heirs</li>
<li>Disputes between trustee and beneficiaries</li>
<li>Requests for instructions on ambiguous terms</li>
<li>Trustee removal petitions</li>
<li>Trust modification requests</li>
</ul>
<p>These situations are relatively rare for well-drafted trusts with clear terms.</p>
<h2>Which Is Better?</h2>
<p>For most California families‚Äîespecially homeowners‚Äîtrust administration is clearly better:</p>
<p>‚úì Faster (months vs. years)
‚úì Cheaper (saves $30,000-$80,000+ for typical estates)
‚úì Private (no public record)
‚úì Flexible (trustee-controlled)
‚úì Includes incapacity planning
‚úì Simpler for multi-state property</p>
<p><strong>The catch:</strong> You must create the trust AND fund it properly during your lifetime. The upfront cost ($2,000-$5,000) pays for itself many times over.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Can I avoid probate if I only have a will?</h3>
<p>No. A will does not avoid probate‚Äîit actually ensures probate will occur. Wills only take effect after death through the probate process. To avoid probate, you need a properly funded living trust that holds your assets during your lifetime and transfers them to beneficiaries after death without court involvement.</p>
<h3>How much does it cost to set up a trust to avoid probate?</h3>
<p>Creating a living trust in California typically costs between $2,000 and $5,000 for professional preparation and funding. While this seems expensive, it pays for itself many times over by avoiding probate costs of $30,000 to $80,000 or more for typical estates. The upfront investment saves your family significant money and time later.</p>
<h3>What happens if some assets were left out of my trust?</h3>
<p>Assets not transferred to your trust go through probate unless they qualify for simplified procedures. California allows estates under $184,500 to use simplified transfer methods, but this is still a form of probate. This is why proper trust funding is critical‚Äîevery asset should be titled in the trust's name or have beneficiary designations to avoid probate entirely.</p>
<h3>Is trust administration completely private or can anyone access the trust?</h3>
<p>Trust administration is private‚Äîthe trust document is not filed with any court and does not become public record. Only entitled parties can access trust information: current beneficiaries, trustees, and heirs who receive the legally required 60-day notice. Unlike probate where anyone can go to the courthouse and see your will, assets, and beneficiaries, trust administration keeps your family's financial affairs confidential.</p>
<h3>Do I need an attorney for trust administration or can the trustee do it alone?</h3>
<p>While trustees can legally administer trusts without an attorney, most benefit from professional guidance. Trust administration involves tax filings, creditor notices, asset valuations, accounting requirements, and distribution timing‚Äîmistakes can lead to personal liability. Attorney fees for trust administration ($3,000-$10,000) are far less than probate costs and help ensure the trustee fulfills all legal duties properly.</p>
