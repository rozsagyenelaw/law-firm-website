7:T706b,<h1>Trust Administration for Business Owners: Handling Business Interests in California</h1>
<p>When a trust holds interests in a business‚Äîwhether a sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, or corporation‚Äîtrust administration becomes significantly more complex. Business interests require specialized knowledge about valuation, operating agreements, buy-sell provisions, ongoing management, and tax implications.</p>
<p>This guide helps trustees navigate the unique challenges of administering business interests held in California trusts.</p>
<h2>Types of Business Interests in Trusts</h2>
<h3>Sole Proprietorships</h3>
<p><strong>What it is:</strong>
Unincorporated business owned by individual, not a separate legal entity.</p>
<p><strong>Trust administration challenges:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business dies with owner (no continuing entity)</li>
<li>Assets must be liquidated or transferred</li>
<li>Ongoing operations difficult</li>
<li>Personal liability concerns</li>
<li>No formal valuation mechanism</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Typical actions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Close business and liquidate assets</li>
<li>Sell business as going concern</li>
<li>Transfer assets to beneficiary to continue operating</li>
</ul>
<h3>Partnerships (General and Limited)</h3>
<p><strong>General Partnership:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Two or more owners sharing profits, losses, and management</li>
<li>Partnership agreement governs transfer rights</li>
<li>Partners have personal liability</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Limited Partnership:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>General partners (manage, liable)</li>
<li>Limited partners (passive investors, limited liability)</li>
<li>Trust typically holds limited partnership interest</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key issues:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Partnership agreement may restrict transfers</li>
<li>May require partner consent for transfer</li>
<li>Ongoing management decisions</li>
<li>Valuation complexities</li>
<li>Income distributions during administration</li>
</ul>
<h3>Limited Liability Companies (LLCs)</h3>
<p><strong>Most common structure for family businesses</strong></p>
<p><strong>Single-member LLC:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Trust owns 100% of LLC</li>
<li>Easier to manage and transfer</li>
<li>Operating agreement still governs</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Multi-member LLC:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Trust owns percentage interest</li>
<li>Other members may have rights</li>
<li>Operating agreement controls transfer rights</li>
<li>May have buy-sell provisions triggered by death</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key advantages:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liability protection continues</li>
<li>Business operations continue uninterrupted</li>
<li>Separate legal entity</li>
<li>Flexible management structure</li>
</ul>
<h3>Corporations (C-Corp and S-Corp)</h3>
<p><strong>C-Corporation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Separate taxpayer</li>
<li>Double taxation (corporate + individual)</li>
<li>Shares freely transferable (unless restricted)</li>
<li>May have shareholder agreements</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>S-Corporation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Pass-through taxation</li>
<li>Stricter ownership requirements</li>
<li>Trust must be eligible shareholder</li>
<li>May lose S-election if improper trust type</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key issues:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Shareholder agreements may restrict transfers</li>
<li>Buy-sell provisions often triggered by death</li>
<li>Valuation for estate tax purposes</li>
<li>Corporate formalities must continue</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 1: Identify and Secure Business Interests</h2>
<h3>Immediate Actions</h3>
<p><strong>Within first week:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Locate all business documents:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Operating agreements / partnership agreements / bylaws</li>
<li>Shareholder agreements / buy-sell agreements</li>
<li>Tax returns (last 3 years)</li>
<li>Financial statements</li>
<li>Business bank account information</li>
<li>Membership certificates or stock certificates</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Notify business partners/co-owners:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Inform of settlor's death</li>
<li>Confirm your authority as trustee</li>
<li>Request copies of operating documents</li>
<li>Discuss transition plan</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Secure business assets:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Change locks if necessary</li>
<li>Update bank signature cards</li>
<li>Protect intellectual property</li>
<li>Secure inventory and equipment</li>
<li>Maintain insurance coverage</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Review cash flow needs:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Immediate operating expenses</li>
<li>Payroll obligations</li>
<li>Accounts payable</li>
<li>Loan payments</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h3>Review Governing Documents</h3>
<p><strong>Operating Agreement / Partnership Agreement:</strong></p>
<p>Critical provisions to identify:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Provision</th>
<th>What to Look For</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Transfer restrictions</td>
<td>Can you transfer membership interest?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Buy-sell triggers</td>
<td>Is death a triggering event?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Valuation method</td>
<td>How is business valued?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Right of first refusal</td>
<td>Must you offer to other members first?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Mandatory purchase</td>
<td>Are other members required to buy?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Payment terms</td>
<td>Lump sum or installments?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Management succession</td>
<td>Who manages after death?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Voting rights</td>
<td>Does trust have voting rights?</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Shareholder Agreements:</strong></p>
<p>Similar issues for corporations:</p>
<ul>
<li>Restrictions on stock transfer</li>
<li>Buy-sell provisions</li>
<li>Valuation formulas</li>
<li>Right of first refusal</li>
<li>Drag-along / tag-along rights</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 2: Determine Trustee's Authority</h2>
<h3>Review Trust Document</h3>
<p><strong>Questions to answer:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Can you continue operating the business?</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Some trusts require liquidation</li>
<li>Others allow continued operation</li>
<li>May have time limits</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Do you have management authority?</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Can you make business decisions?</li>
<li>Are you limited to passive ownership?</li>
<li>Must you appoint professional management?</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Can you sell or transfer the business?</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Does trust require distribution to specific beneficiary?</li>
<li>Must you obtain beneficiary consent?</li>
<li>Are there restrictions on sale?</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h3>California Statutory Authority</h3>
<p><strong>Probate Code ¬ß16200-16249</strong> grants trustees broad powers over trust property, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Operating a business (¬ß16249)</li>
<li>Continuing existing business</li>
<li>Incorporating, dissolving, or reorganizing business</li>
<li>Voting shares</li>
<li>Entering contracts on behalf of business</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>However:</strong> Trust document controls. Explicit restrictions in trust override statutory authority.</p>
<h3>Operating Agreement Restrictions</h3>
<p>Even if trust document allows business operations, the operating agreement may:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prohibit management by non-members</li>
<li>Require unanimous consent for certain actions</li>
<li>Trigger mandatory buyout upon member's death</li>
<li>Restrict trustee's authority</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 3: Obtain Professional Business Valuation</h2>
<h3>Why Valuation Is Critical</h3>
<p><strong>You need accurate valuation for:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Estate tax purposes</strong> (if estate over $13.61M)</li>
<li><strong>Capital gains basis</strong> for future sale</li>
<li><strong>Equal distribution</strong> among beneficiaries</li>
<li><strong>Buy-sell transactions</strong> if agreement triggered</li>
<li><strong>Financial planning</strong> for trust administration</li>
<li><strong>Defending against beneficiary disputes</strong></li>
</ol>
<h3>Valuation Methods</h3>
<p><strong>Asset-based valuation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business value = assets minus liabilities</li>
<li>Used for asset-heavy businesses</li>
<li>Ignores earning potential</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Income-based valuation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Discounted cash flow analysis</li>
<li>Capitalization of earnings</li>
<li>Used for profitable, ongoing businesses</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Market-based valuation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Comparable company analysis</li>
<li>Comparable transaction multiples</li>
<li>Used when comparables exist</li>
</ul>
<h3>Valuation Discounts</h3>
<p><strong>Minority interest discount:</strong>
If trust owns less than 50%:</p>
<ul>
<li>No control over business decisions</li>
<li>Cannot force sale or distributions</li>
<li>Discount typically 20-40%</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Lack of marketability discount:</strong>
Private company interests are hard to sell:</p>
<ul>
<li>No public market</li>
<li>Transfer restrictions</li>
<li>Limited buyers</li>
<li>Discount typically 20-35%</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Pro-rata value of 30% interest: $1,000,000</li>
<li>Minus minority discount (25%): -$250,000</li>
<li>Minus marketability discount (30%): -$225,000</li>
<li><strong>Fair market value: $525,000</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>These discounts can significantly reduce estate tax.</p>
<h3>Hire Qualified Appraiser</h3>
<p><strong>Qualifications to look for:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Accredited Senior Appraiser (ASA)</li>
<li>Certified Valuation Analyst (CVA)</li>
<li>Accredited in Business Valuation (ABV)</li>
<li>Experience with your industry</li>
<li>Experience with estate/gift tax valuations</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cost:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Small businesses: $5,000-$15,000</li>
<li>Complex businesses: $15,000-$50,000+</li>
<li>Litigation-quality valuations: $25,000-$100,000+</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Timeline:</strong>
Expect 4-8 weeks for comprehensive valuation.</p>
<h2>Step 4: Handle Buy-Sell Agreements</h2>
<h3>Types of Buy-Sell Agreements</h3>
<p><strong>Cross-purchase agreement:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Other owners buy deceased owner's interest</li>
<li>Personally, not through business entity</li>
<li>Common in partnerships and small LLCs</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Entity redemption:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business entity buys deceased owner's interest</li>
<li>LLC or corporation becomes sole buyer</li>
<li>Simpler than cross-purchase</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Hybrid (wait-and-see):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business entity has first option</li>
<li>If entity doesn't buy, other owners must buy</li>
<li>Flexibility for tax planning</li>
</ul>
<h3>Triggering Events</h3>
<p><strong>Common triggers:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Death</li>
<li>Disability</li>
<li>Retirement</li>
<li>Termination of employment</li>
<li>Bankruptcy</li>
<li>Divorce</li>
<li>Voluntary sale</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Death almost always triggers buy-sell provisions.</strong></p>
<h3>Valuation Under Buy-Sell</h3>
<p><strong>Fixed price:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Agreement states specific dollar amount</li>
<li>Often outdated if not regularly updated</li>
<li>Binds IRS for estate tax if legitimate</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Formula:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Book value</li>
<li>Multiple of earnings (3x-5x EBITDA)</li>
<li>Revenue multiple</li>
<li>Must apply formula as of date of death</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Appraisal:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Independent appraiser determines value</li>
<li>Most accurate but most expensive</li>
<li>May specify appraiser selection process</li>
</ul>
<h3>Payment Terms</h3>
<p><strong>Lump sum:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Buyers pay full amount at closing</li>
<li>Requires financing or insurance proceeds</li>
<li>Clean and final</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Installment sale:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Payments over time (typically 5-10 years)</li>
<li>Interest required (AFR rates)</li>
<li>Trust receives payments during administration</li>
<li>Creates note receivable</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Life insurance funded:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business or owners maintained life insurance</li>
<li>Insurance proceeds fund purchase</li>
<li>Most common and cleanest method</li>
<li>Verify policies are current and adequate</li>
</ul>
<h3>Enforcing Buy-Sell Terms</h3>
<p>If agreement is triggered:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Notify other parties</strong> of death and triggering event</li>
<li><strong>Provide required documentation</strong> (death certificate, trust documents)</li>
<li><strong>Initiate valuation process</strong> per agreement terms</li>
<li><strong>Negotiate closing</strong> (timing, payment method, transition)</li>
<li><strong>Prepare purchase documents</strong> (assignment, bill of sale)</li>
<li><strong>Close transaction</strong> and transfer ownership</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>If other parties won't comply:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Send formal demand letter</li>
<li>Consult business litigation attorney</li>
<li>File lawsuit for specific performance</li>
<li>Seek damages for breach of contract</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 5: Manage Business During Administration</h2>
<h3>Decision: Continue or Liquidate?</h3>
<p><strong>Factors to consider:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Continue operating if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business is profitable and stable</li>
<li>Competent management is in place</li>
<li>Beneficiary intends to take over</li>
<li>Buy-sell process will take time</li>
<li>Trust authorizes continuation</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Liquidate if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business dependent on deceased's skills</li>
<li>No qualified management available</li>
<li>Business is unprofitable</li>
<li>Trust requires liquidation</li>
<li>High liability risk</li>
<li>Beneficiaries want cash, not business</li>
</ul>
<h3>Ongoing Management Decisions</h3>
<p>If continuing operations:</p>
<p><strong>Financial management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Review financial statements monthly</li>
<li>Monitor cash flow carefully</li>
<li>Ensure bills and payroll are paid</li>
<li>Maintain adequate insurance</li>
<li>File all required tax returns</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Operational oversight:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ensure competent management in place</li>
<li>Make major decisions (new contracts, major purchases)</li>
<li>Protect business assets</li>
<li>Maintain customer relationships</li>
<li>Preserve goodwill</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Legal compliance:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>File annual reports with California Secretary of State</li>
<li>Maintain good standing</li>
<li>Comply with employment laws</li>
<li>Renew licenses and permits</li>
<li>Maintain required insurance</li>
</ul>
<h3>Delegating Management</h3>
<p><strong>You're not required to personally manage the business.</strong></p>
<p>Options:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Existing management continues:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Manager, president, or GM stays in place</li>
<li>You oversee from governance level</li>
<li>Review financial reports</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Hire professional management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Recruit qualified manager</li>
<li>Set compensation</li>
<li>Monitor performance</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Appoint special trustee:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Co-trustee with business expertise</li>
<li>Handles only business decisions</li>
<li>You handle other trust matters</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Family member takes over:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>If qualified and trust allows</li>
<li>Must pay reasonable compensation</li>
<li>Avoid conflicts of interest</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h2>Step 6: Address Tax Issues</h2>
<h3>Entity-Level Taxes</h3>
<p><strong>Partnership/LLC (pass-through):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>File Form 1065 (partnership return)</li>
<li>Issue K-1s to trust</li>
<li>Trust reports income on Form 1041</li>
<li>Income may be distributed to beneficiaries</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>C-Corporation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>File Form 1120 (corporate return)</li>
<li>Corporation pays its own taxes</li>
<li>Dividends to trust are taxable income</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>S-Corporation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>File Form 1120-S</li>
<li>Pass-through taxation</li>
<li><strong>Critical:</strong> Ensure trust is eligible S-corp shareholder</li>
</ul>
<h3>S-Corporation Trust Rules</h3>
<p><strong>Eligible trust types:</strong></p>
<p>After death, trust typically qualifies as:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Qualified Subchapter S Trust (QSST)</strong> - If single beneficiary, all income distributed</li>
<li><strong>Electing Small Business Trust (ESBT)</strong> - More flexible, but higher tax rates</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Election must be filed:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>QSST: Beneficiary files Form 2553 within 2 months and 16 days</li>
<li>ESBT: Trustee files Form 2553 within same timeframe</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Missing deadline = Loss of S-election = Corporate double tax</strong></p>
<h3>Estate Tax</h3>
<p><strong>Business included in gross estate:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Fair market value as of date of death</li>
<li>Valuation discounts apply</li>
<li>Buy-sell agreement may establish value</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Special valuation rules:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>IRC ¬ß2032A - Special use valuation for family farms/businesses</li>
<li>IRC ¬ß6166 - Deferred estate tax payments for business interests</li>
</ul>
<h3>Income During Administration</h3>
<p><strong>Business income received by trust:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reported on Form 1041</li>
<li>Taxed at trust rates (reach 37% quickly)</li>
<li>Consider distributing income to beneficiaries (lower tax rates)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Deductions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Trust administration expenses</li>
<li>Business losses (if any)</li>
<li>Depreciation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Step 7: Transfer to Beneficiaries</h2>
<h3>Distribution Options</h3>
<p><strong>Distribute business interest in-kind:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Transfer membership interest/shares to beneficiary</li>
<li>Beneficiary takes stepped-up basis</li>
<li>Beneficiary continues or sells business</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Sell business and distribute proceeds:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Liquidate business interest</li>
<li>Distribute cash to beneficiaries</li>
<li>Cleaner if multiple beneficiaries want different things</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Partial distribution:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Distribute percentage interests to multiple beneficiaries</li>
<li>Creates co-ownership</li>
<li>May lead to future disputes</li>
</ul>
<h3>Transfer Mechanics</h3>

<li>File amended annual reports</li>

<ol>
<li>Transfer individual assets (equipment, inventory, contracts)</li>
<li>Transfer business name registration</li>
<li>Transfer licenses and permits (if transferable)</li>
<li>Close business accounts in trust's name</li>
<li>Open new accounts in beneficiary's name</li>
</ol>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Can I continue operating the business while administering the trust?</h3>
<p>Whether you can continue business operations depends on the trust document and the business's operating agreement. California law grants trustees broad authority to operate businesses under Probate Code Section 16249, but the trust document may impose restrictions or require liquidation within a specific timeframe. Additionally, the operating agreement or shareholder agreement may restrict who can manage the business or trigger mandatory buy-sell provisions upon the owner's death. Review both documents carefully before making operational decisions.</p>
<h3>How do I determine the value of a business held in a trust?</h3>
<p>Business valuation requires hiring a qualified appraiser with credentials such as ASA, CVA, or ABV designation. They'll use one or more methods: asset-based valuation for asset-heavy businesses, income-based valuation using discounted cash flow for profitable ongoing businesses, or market-based valuation using comparable companies. For minority interests or private companies, expect significant valuation discounts of 20-40% for minority interest and 20-35% for lack of marketability. Professional valuations typically cost between $5,000-$50,000 depending on complexity.</p>
<h3>What happens if the trust owns S-corporation stock?</h3>
<p>S-corporations have strict ownership requirements that continue after death. After the settlor dies, the trust must qualify as either a Qualified Subchapter S Trust (QSST) or an Electing Small Business Trust (ESBT) to avoid losing the S-election. You must file the appropriate election with the IRS within 2 months and 16 days of the death. Missing this deadline causes the corporation to lose its S-election status and triggers double taxation as a C-corporation, which can create massive tax liability.</p>
<h3>Does a buy-sell agreement automatically transfer ownership when someone dies?</h3>
<p>Not automatically, but death typically triggers the buy-sell provisions. Most buy-sell agreements require either the business entity or co-owners to purchase the deceased owner's interest, but you must initiate the process. Start by notifying all parties, providing required documentation, initiating the valuation process specified in the agreement, and negotiating the closing timeline and payment method. If other parties refuse to comply with the agreement terms, you may need to send a formal demand letter or file a lawsuit for specific performance.</p>
<h3>Can I sell the business instead of transferring it to beneficiaries?</h3>
<p>Your authority to sell depends on the trust document's provisions. Some trusts require distribution of the business to specific beneficiaries, while others give the trustee discretion to sell and distribute proceeds. Selling may be the better option when beneficiaries want cash rather than business ownership, the business was dependent on the deceased's personal skills, no qualified family member can take over, or liquidation provides better value than forced transfer. Always review the trust terms and consult with beneficiaries before making major decisions about business disposition.</p>
