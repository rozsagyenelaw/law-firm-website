7:T49b7,<p>"The court appointed a probate referee to appraise my father's estate. What does this mean and how much will it cost?"</p>
<p>As a Glendale probate attorney, I explain probate referees to every client because they're a mandatory‚Äîand often misunderstood‚Äîpart of California probate. Probate referees are court-appointed appraisers who determine the fair market value of estate assets for the official inventory.</p>
<p>Understanding what probate referees do, why they're required, how much they cost (0.1% of appraised value), and how their valuations affect probate helps executors navigate this required step.</p>
<p>This comprehensive guide explains everything about California probate referees: their role, appointment process, what assets they appraise, how appraisals are conducted, fees, timeline, challenging appraisals, and how valuations impact probate costs and taxes.</p>
<h2>What Is a Probate Referee?</h2>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Sections 8900-8929</strong></p>
<p><strong>Probate referee:</strong> A professional appraiser appointed by the court to determine the fair market value of estate assets for probate purposes.</p>
<h3>Purpose of Probate Referees</h3>
<p><strong>Required for:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Completing inventory and appraisal (Form DE-160)</li>
<li>Establishing official estate value</li>
<li>Calculating statutory probate fees</li>
<li>Determining estate tax liability</li>
<li>Setting minimum sale prices for real property</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Why court-appointed:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ensures independent, unbiased valuations</li>
<li>Protects beneficiaries from undervaluation</li>
<li>Protects creditors from overvaluation</li>
<li>Provides official values for legal purposes</li>
</ul>
<h3>Who Can Be a Probate Referee</h3>
<p><strong>Requirements:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Qualifications (California Probate Code Section 8901):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Designated by court as probate referee for that county</li>
<li>Qualified by education and experience</li>
<li>Covered by fidelity bond</li>
<li>Good moral character</li>
<li>Not related to case parties</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cannot serve if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Interested in estate</li>
<li>Related to decedent, executor, or beneficiaries</li>
<li>Has conflict of interest</li>
</ul>
<h2>When Are Probate Referees Required?</h2>
<h3>Mandatory Appointment</h3>
<p><strong>Probate referee required</strong> in all formal probate proceedings in California.</p>
<p><strong>Exception:</strong> Small estate procedures (under $184,500) don't require probate referee.</p>
<h3>Timing of Appointment</h3>
<p><strong>Appointed early in probate:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Executor files petition for probate</li>
<li>Court grants petition and issues letters</li>
<li><strong>Court appoints probate referee at same time</strong></li>
<li>Referee notified of appointment</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Executor doesn't choose</strong> - court assigns referee.</p>
<h3>What Triggers the Appraisal</h3>
<p><strong>Within 4 months of letters issued:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Executor must:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>File inventory and appraisal (Form DE-160)</li>
<li>List all estate assets</li>
<li>State values for certain assets</li>
<li>Send inventory to probate referee for appraisal</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Referee appraises</strong> assets requiring appraisal (not all assets).</p>
<h2>What Do Probate Referees Appraise?</h2>
<h3>Assets Referee DOES Appraise</h3>
<p><strong>Real property:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Houses, condos, land</li>
<li>Commercial property</li>
<li>Rental property</li>
<li>Undeveloped land</li>
<li>Out-of-state real property</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Personal property:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Vehicles (cars, boats, RVs, motorcycles)</li>
<li>Business interests</li>
<li>Partnership interests</li>
<li>Stock in closely-held corporations</li>
<li>Tangible personal property (furniture, jewelry, art, collectibles)</li>
<li>Machinery and equipment</li>
<li>Intellectual property rights</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Generally:</strong> Assets without readily ascertainable values.</p>
<h3>Assets Referee Does NOT Appraise</h3>
<p><strong>Executor states values (not referee):</strong></p>
<p><strong>Cash assets:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Bank account balances (as of date of death)</li>
<li>Certificates of deposit</li>
<li>Money market accounts</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Publicly traded securities:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Stocks listed on major exchanges</li>
<li>Bonds</li>
<li>Mutual funds</li>
<li><strong>Value:</strong> Mean between high/low on date of death</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Life insurance:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>If payable to estate</li>
<li><strong>Value:</strong> Policy proceeds</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Retirement accounts:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>If payable to estate (rare)</li>
<li><strong>Value:</strong> Account balance at death</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Accounts with POD/TOD:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Usually don't go through probate</li>
<li>Not included in probate inventory</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Why executor values these:</strong> Values are readily ascertainable from statements or published prices.</p>
<h2>The Appraisal Process</h2>
<h3>Step 1: Executor Prepares Inventory</h3>
<p><strong>Executor completes Inventory and Appraisal form (DE-160):</strong></p>
<p><strong>Lists all assets:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Real property (addresses)</li>
<li>Personal property (descriptions)</li>
<li>Cash and securities (with values)</li>
<li>Business interests</li>
<li>Other assets</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Executor states values</strong> for cash, publicly traded securities, and cash equivalents.</p>
<p><strong>Leaves blank</strong> values for assets requiring referee appraisal.</p>
<h3>Step 2: Executor Sends Inventory to Referee</h3>
<p><strong>After listing assets:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Send copy of inventory to appointed probate referee</li>
<li>Include property addresses for inspection</li>
<li>Provide access for referee to inspect property</li>
<li>Include relevant documents (deeds, business records, etc.)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Timeline:</strong> Should send promptly to meet 4-month filing deadline.</p>
<h3>Step 3: Referee Conducts Appraisals</h3>
<p><strong>Referee's process:</strong></p>
<p><strong>For real property:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reviews county records</li>
<li>May conduct site inspection</li>
<li>Reviews comparable sales</li>
<li>Considers condition, location, market</li>
<li>Determines fair market value as of date of death</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>For personal property:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Inspects items if necessary</li>
<li>Reviews market data</li>
<li>Considers condition, age, demand</li>
<li>Determines fair market value</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>For business interests:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reviews financial statements</li>
<li>Analyzes business operations</li>
<li>Considers market multiples</li>
<li>May engage business valuation expert</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Standard:</strong> Fair market value on date of death (price willing buyer would pay willing seller).</p>
<h3>Step 4: Referee Completes Appraisal</h3>
<p><strong>Referee fills in values</strong> on inventory form for appraised assets.</p>
<p><strong>Signs under penalty of perjury.</strong></p>
<p><strong>Returns completed form to executor.</strong></p>
<p><strong>Timeline:</strong> Usually 1-3 weeks after receiving inventory.</p>
<h3>Step 5: Executor Files Inventory with Court</h3>
<p><strong>Executor files completed Inventory and Appraisal</strong> with probate court.</p>
<p><strong>Must be filed within 4 months</strong> of letters being issued.</p>
<p><strong>Includes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>All assets listed</li>
<li>Executor's values for certain assets</li>
<li>Referee's appraisal values for other assets</li>
<li>Total gross estate value</li>
</ul>
<h2>Probate Referee Fees</h2>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 8960</strong></p>
<h3>Fee Structure</h3>
<p><strong>Referee compensation:</strong> 0.1% (one-tenth of one percent) of total appraised value</p>
<p><strong>Calculated on:</strong> Gross value of property appraised by referee (not entire estate)</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Property appraised at $500,000 = $500 fee</li>
<li>Property appraised at $1,000,000 = $1,000 fee</li>
<li>Property appraised at $2,000,000 = $2,000 fee</li>
</ul>
<h3>Who Pays Referee Fees</h3>
<p><strong>Paid by estate</strong> before distribution to beneficiaries.</p>
<p><strong>Executor responsibility</strong> to ensure payment.</p>
<p><strong>Timing:</strong> Paid after referee completes appraisal.</p>
<h3>Minimum and Maximum Fees</h3>
<p><strong>Minimum fee:</strong> None statutory, but referees typically charge minimum (e.g., $250-$500)</p>
<p><strong>Maximum fee:</strong> 0.1% of appraised value (no statutory cap above percentage)</p>
<h3>What's NOT Included in Standard Fee</h3>
<p><strong>Additional charges possible for:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Out-of-county property inspection</li>
<li>Extraordinary services</li>
<li>Complex valuations requiring special expertise</li>
<li>Multiple property locations</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>These require court approval</strong> if exceeding standard 0.1%.</p>
<h2>How Referee Valuations Affect Probate</h2>
<h3>1. Statutory Probate Fees</h3>
<p><strong>Executor and attorney fees</strong> calculated based on gross estate value.</p>
<p><strong>Higher appraisals = Higher fees</strong></p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>House appraised at $900,000 vs. $1,100,000</li>
<li>Difference: $200,000</li>
<li>Impact on fees: $200,000 √ó 2% = $4,000 additional executor fees + $4,000 additional attorney fees = $8,000 total</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Beneficiaries may have interest</strong> in lower appraisals.
<strong>Executor/attorney may have interest</strong> in higher appraisals (more fees).</p>
<p><strong>Referee's duty:</strong> Independent, accurate valuation regardless of fee impact.</p>
<h3>2. Minimum Sale Price for Real Property</h3>
<p><strong>If real property sold during probate:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Without IAEA authority:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Court confirmation required</li>
<li>Minimum acceptable offer: 90% of appraised value</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Example:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>House appraised at $1,000,000</li>
<li>Cannot accept offer below $900,000 without court approval</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Higher appraisal</strong> = Higher minimum sale price</p>
<h3>3. Estate Tax Threshold</h3>
<p><strong>Federal estate tax exemption:</strong> $13.61 million (2024)</p>
<p><strong>Accurate valuation necessary</strong> for estates approaching threshold.</p>
<p><strong>California:</strong> No state estate tax</p>
<h3>4. Basis Step-Up for Beneficiaries</h3>
<p><strong>Beneficiaries receive property</strong> with basis stepped up to date of death value.</p>
<p><strong>Higher appraisal</strong> = Higher basis = Lower capital gains tax when beneficiaries eventually sell</p>
<p><strong>This usually benefits beneficiaries.</strong></p>
<h2>Challenging Probate Referee Appraisals</h2>
<h3>When to Challenge</h3>
<p><strong>Consider challenging if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Appraisal significantly differs from market value</li>
<li>Referee didn't inspect property</li>
<li>Referee lacked expertise for asset type</li>
<li>Recent sale price differs substantially</li>
<li>Other appraisals show different value</li>
</ul>
<h3>How to Challenge</h3>
<p><strong>Process:</strong></p>
<p><strong>1. Obtain independent appraisal</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hire separate professional appraiser</li>
<li>Get written appraisal report</li>
<li>Must be credible and well-supported</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>2. File objection with court</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Petition objecting to referee appraisal</li>
<li>Provide independent appraisal</li>
<li>Explain why referee's value is incorrect</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>3. Court hearing</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Present evidence</li>
<li>Referee may defend appraisal</li>
<li>Judge determines correct value</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Burden:</strong> Party challenging appraisal must prove referee's value is incorrect.</p>
<h3>Supplemental Inventory</h3>
<p><strong>If assets discovered after original inventory:</strong></p>
<p><strong>File supplemental inventory and appraisal:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Lists newly discovered assets</li>
<li>Sent to probate referee for appraisal</li>
<li>Filed with court</li>
<li>Additional referee fee applies</li>
</ul>
<h2>Special Situations</h2>
<h3>Out-of-State Property</h3>
<p><strong>California probate referee appraises</strong> out-of-state real property for California probate.</p>
<p><strong>May rely on:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Local appraisals</li>
<li>Market data from property state</li>
<li>Remote valuation methods</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Separate ancillary probate</strong> may be required in property's state.</p>
<h3>Complex Business Valuations</h3>
<p><strong>Closely-held businesses may require:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Business valuation expert</li>
<li>Financial analysis</li>
<li>Goodwill assessment</li>
<li>Multiple valuation methods</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Referee may:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Engage business valuation specialist</li>
<li>Charge additional fees (with court approval)</li>
<li>Request business records from executor</li>
</ul>
<h3>Property Sold Before Inventory</h3>
<p><strong>If property sold between death and inventory:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Two values possible:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Fair market value at death</strong> (what referee appraises)</li>
<li><strong>Actual sale price</strong> (what property sold for)</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Referee uses date-of-death value,</strong> not sale price.</p>
<p><strong>Sale price used</strong> only if sale was arm's length transaction close to death date.</p>
<h3>Date of Death vs. Alternate Valuation</h3>
<p><strong>Standard:</strong> Date of death valuation</p>
<p><strong>Alternate valuation date:</strong> Six months after death (for estate tax purposes only)</p>
<p><strong>Probate referee uses date of death</strong> for probate inventory.</p>
<h2>Working With Probate Referees</h2>
<h3>Executor's Responsibilities</h3>
<p><strong>To facilitate appraisal:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Provide information:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Property addresses</li>
<li>Access to inspect property</li>
<li>Relevant documents (deeds, financial statements)</li>
<li>Answer referee's questions</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Coordinate timing:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Send inventory promptly</li>
<li>Allow time for appraisal before 4-month deadline</li>
<li>Arrange property access</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Don't:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Try to influence valuation</li>
<li>Withhold information</li>
<li>Pressure referee for certain value</li>
</ul>
<h3>Communication</h3>
<p><strong>Appropriate contact:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Provide factual information</li>
<li>Answer questions about property</li>
<li>Coordinate logistics</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Inappropriate contact:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Suggesting specific values</li>
<li>Offering inducements</li>
<li>Threatening referee</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Referee is independent</strong> and owes duties to court, not parties.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Can executor choose the probate referee?</h3>
<p>No. Court appoints probate referee from list of qualified referees in that county. Assignment is usually rotating.</p>
<h3>How long does probate referee appraisal take?</h3>
<p>Typically 1-3 weeks after referee receives inventory, depending on complexity and number of properties to appraise.</p>
<h3>What if executor disagrees with appraisal?</h3>
<p>Executor can file objection with court and present independent appraisal showing different value. Court decides.</p>
<h3>Does referee physically inspect property?</h3>
<p>For real estate, referee usually conducts exterior inspection and may inspect interior. For personal property, referee inspects if necessary.</p>
<h3>Can referee appraisal be used for selling property?</h3>
<p>Referee appraisal establishes baseline value. For sales, executor may want additional current market appraisal from real estate agent.</p>
<h3>What if property value changed significantly since death?</h3>
<p>Referee uses date-of-death value regardless of subsequent changes. Current market value matters for sales but not inventory.</p>
<h3>Are probate referee fees negotiable?</h3>
<p>No. Fee is set by statute at 0.1% of appraised value. Court must approve any additional fees.</p>
