7:T324c,<p>If you've just become a successor trustee in California, one of your first legal obligations is sending proper notice to beneficiaries and heirs. Miss this requirement, and you could face serious consequences.</p>
<p>Here's everything you need to know about California's trust notification rules.</p>
<h2>What Is the 60-Day Notice?</h2>
<p>California Probate Code ¬ß16061.7 requires successor trustees to notify beneficiaries and heirs when a revocable trust becomes irrevocable‚Äîtypically at the trust creator's death.</p>
<p>This notification:</p>
<ul>
<li>Informs people of their interest in the trust</li>
<li>Starts the 120-day period for contesting the trust</li>
<li>Protects the trustee from later claims of inadequate notice</li>
<li>Gives recipients the right to request trust information</li>
</ul>
<h2>Who Must Receive Notice?</h2>
<p>You must send notice to:</p>
<h3>1. Trust Beneficiaries</h3>
<p>Anyone named as a beneficiary in the trust, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Primary beneficiaries who receive distributions</li>
<li>Contingent beneficiaries (who receive if primary beneficiaries don't)</li>
<li>Remainder beneficiaries (who receive after current beneficiaries)</li>
</ul>
<h3>2. Legal Heirs</h3>
<p>Anyone who would inherit under California intestate succession if there were no trust, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Surviving spouse</li>
<li>Children</li>
<li>Grandchildren (if their parent predeceased)</li>
<li>Parents (if no spouse or children)</li>
<li>Siblings (if no closer relatives)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Important:</strong> You must notify heirs even if they receive nothing under the trust. The law assumes they might want to contest.</p>
<h3>3. The Person Who Created the Prior Trust</h3>
<p>If the current trust amended or replaced an earlier trust, notify beneficiaries of both versions.</p>
<h2>What Must the Notice Include?</h2>
<p>California law specifies required content:</p>
<h3>1. Identity of the settlor(s)</h3>
<p>Name of the person who created the trust</p>
<h3>2. Trust name and date</h3>
<p>Title of the trust and date it was executed</p>
<h3>3. That the trust has become irrevocable</h3>
<p>Typically because the settlor died</p>
<h3>4. Trustee contact information</h3>
<p>Name, address, and phone number of the trustee</p>
<h3>5. Attorney contact information</h3>
<p>Name, address, and phone number of trustee's attorney (if any)</p>
<h3>6. Notification of right to receive trust terms</h3>
<p>Statement that the recipient can request a copy of trust provisions affecting their interest</p>
<h3>7. The 120-day contest period</h3>
<p>Statement that any contest must be brought within 120 days of receipt of notice</p>
<h3>8. Date of death</h3>
<p>When the settlor died</p>
<h2>The 60-Day Deadline</h2>
<h3>When does the clock start?</h3>
<p>The 60-day deadline runs from the later of:</p>
<ul>
<li>The date of the settlor's death, OR</li>
<li>The date you become trustee</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Example 1:</strong> Settlor dies January 1. You're named successor trustee in the trust. Your deadline is March 1 (60 days from death).</p>
<p><strong>Example 2:</strong> Original successor trustee declines to serve. You're appointed on February 15 after settlor died January 1. Your deadline is April 15 (60 days from becoming trustee).</p>
<h2>How to Send Notice</h2>
<h3>Proper Delivery Methods</h3>
<p>California allows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Personal delivery (hand-delivered)</li>
<li>First-class mail to last known address</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Keep proof:</strong> Use certified mail with return receipt, or have someone sign an acknowledgment of personal delivery.</p>
<h3>For Recipients Whose Address Is Unknown</h3>
<p>Make reasonable efforts to find them:</p>
<ul>
<li>Check deceased's records and address book</li>
<li>Search online</li>
<li>Ask family members</li>
<li>Consider professional locator services</li>
</ul>
<p>Document your search efforts. If you truly cannot locate someone after reasonable effort, that's generally sufficient.</p>
<h2>The 120-Day Contest Period</h2>
<p>The notice triggers a critical timeline:</p>
<p><strong>120 days from receipt of notice = deadline to contest the trust</strong></p>
<p>After 120 days, recipients generally lose the right to challenge:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trust validity</li>
<li>Trustee appointment</li>
<li>Trust terms</li>
</ul>
<h3>Why This Matters for Trustees</h3>
<p>Until the 120-day period expires, there's risk that someone could contest the trust. Most trustees:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wait until 120 days pass before final distributions</li>
<li>Make only preliminary distributions (if any) before then</li>
<li>Reserve funds for potential contest</li>
</ul>
<h3>Why This Matters for Beneficiaries</h3>
<p>If you have concerns about the trust, you have only 120 days to act. After that, your ability to challenge is severely limited.</p>
<h2>What Happens If You Don't Send Notice?</h2>
<h3>For the Trustee</h3>
<p>Failure to send required notice can result in:</p>
<p><strong>Extended contest period:</strong> The 120-day clock never starts, meaning trust can be contested indefinitely.</p>
<p><strong>Personal liability:</strong> Beneficiaries who didn't receive proper notice may sue the trustee.</p>
<p><strong>Removal as trustee:</strong> Court may remove trustee for failure to perform duties.</p>
<p><strong>Surcharge:</strong> Trustee may have to pay damages from personal funds.</p>
<h3>For the Administration</h3>
<p>Without proper notice, you cannot safely:</p>
<ul>
<li>Make final distributions</li>
<li>Close the trust</li>
<li>Obtain releases from beneficiaries</li>
</ul>
<h2>Sample Notice Letter</h2>
<p>While you should use a proper legal form (or have an attorney prepare it), here's the general content:</p>
<pre><code>NOTIFICATION BY TRUSTEE
(California Probate Code ¬ß16061.7)

To: [Recipient Name and Address]

You are hereby notified that:

1. [Settlor Name] ("Settlor"), the creator of the [Trust Name]
   dated [Trust Date] ("Trust"), died on [Date of Death].

2. [Your Name] is now serving as Trustee of the Trust.

3. The Trust has become irrevocable due to the Settlor's death.

4. You have the right to request a copy of the terms of the Trust
   that affect your interest.

5. If you wish to contest the Trust, you must bring an action
   within 120 days of the date you receive this notification.

Trustee Contact Information:
[Name, Address, Phone]

Attorney Contact Information:
[Name, Address, Phone] (or "None")

Date: [Date]

Signature: [Trustee Signature]
</code></pre>
<h2>Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<h3>Missing the 60-Day Deadline</h3>
<p>Set a calendar reminder immediately upon becoming trustee. This deadline is firm.</p>
<h3>Forgetting Heirs Who Aren't Beneficiaries</h3>
<p>You must notify intestate heirs even if they get nothing under the trust. A disinherited child still receives notice.</p>
<h3>Using Wrong Addresses</h3>
<p>Make reasonable efforts to find current addresses. Notice sent to an old address may not be effective.</p>
<h3>Not Keeping Proof</h3>
<p>Without proof of mailing, you can't demonstrate compliance. Always use certified mail or get signed acknowledgments.</p>
<h3>Omitting Required Content</h3>
<p>The notice must include all elements specified by statute. Incomplete notices may not start the 120-day clock.</p>
<h3>Sending Notice Too Early</h3>
<p>If you send notice before formally becoming trustee, it may not be valid. Wait until you're officially serving.</p>
<h2>What About Amended or Restated Trusts?</h2>
<p>If the trust was amended multiple times:</p>
<ul>
<li>Send notice to beneficiaries of the current (final) version</li>
<li>Also notify beneficiaries of prior versions if their interests were affected</li>
<li>Include notice about all relevant trust documents</li>
</ul>
<h2>After Sending Notice</h2>
<h3>Document Everything</h3>
<p>Maintain records showing:</p>
<ul>
<li>Who received notice</li>
<li>What address was used</li>
<li>Date sent</li>
<li>Proof of mailing/delivery</li>
</ul>
<h3>Respond to Requests</h3>
<p>Recipients may request copies of trust terms affecting their interest. Provide these promptly.</p>
<h3>Track the 120-Day Period</h3>
<p>Calculate when the contest period expires for each recipient. Don't make final distributions until all periods have run.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>What happens if I miss the 60-day notice deadline in California?</h3>
<p>If you miss the 60-day deadline, the 120-day contest period never starts running. This means beneficiaries and heirs can potentially contest the trust indefinitely. You may also face personal liability, removal as trustee, and surcharge for any damages caused by the delay. Send the notice as soon as possible even if late.</p>
<h3>Who must receive the 60-day trust notice in California?</h3>
<p>You must send notice to all trust beneficiaries (primary, contingent, and remainder), all legal heirs who would inherit under California intestate succession laws (even if they receive nothing under the trust), and beneficiaries of any prior versions of the trust if it was amended.</p>
<h3>Can I make distributions before the 120-day contest period expires?</h3>
<p>Most trustees wait until the 120-day period expires before making final distributions. You may make preliminary distributions if the estate clearly has sufficient assets, but you should reserve funds for potential contests. Making full distributions before 120 days creates risk if someone contests the trust.</p>
<h3>What must be included in a California trust notification?</h3>
<p>The notice must include: the settlor's identity, trust name and date, statement that trust is irrevocable, trustee contact information, attorney contact information (if any), beneficiary's right to receive trust terms, the 120-day contest deadline, and the settlor's date of death.</p>
<h3>How do I send the 60-day notice properly?</h3>
<p>California allows personal delivery or first-class mail to the recipient's last known address. Use certified mail with return receipt to prove delivery. Keep copies of all notices sent, along with proof of mailing. If an address is unknown, document your reasonable efforts to locate the person.</p>
