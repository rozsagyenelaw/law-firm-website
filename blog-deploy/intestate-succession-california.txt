7:T5b44,<p>When someone dies without a valid will in California, state law determines who inherits through a process called <strong>intestate succession</strong>. California Probate Code Sections 6400-6455 create a rigid formula based on family relationships at the time of death.</p>
<p>As a Glendale probate attorney who regularly handles intestate estates, I've seen how California's intestate succession laws surprise families‚Äîspouses don't always inherit everything, children from prior marriages complicate distributions, and unmarried partners inherit nothing.</p>
<p>This comprehensive guide explains exactly who inherits under California intestate succession, the priority order of heirs, how community property affects distributions, special rules for different family situations, and why these default rules probably don't match what most people actually want.</p>
<h2>What Is Intestate Succession?</h2>
<p><strong>Intestate succession</strong> is the legal process that determines who inherits property when someone dies without a valid will.</p>
<p><strong>"Intestate"</strong> means dying without a will.</p>
<p><strong>"Testate"</strong> means dying with a valid will.</p>
<h3>When Intestate Succession Applies</h3>
<p>California's intestate succession laws apply when:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>No will exists</strong> at death</li>
<li><strong>Will is invalid</strong> due to:
<ul>
<li>Improper execution (missing signatures, witnesses)</li>
<li>Lack of testamentary capacity</li>
<li>Undue influence or fraud</li>
<li>Successful will contest</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Will doesn't dispose of all property</strong> (partial intestacy)</li>
</ol>
<h3>What Intestate Succession Controls</h3>
<p>Intestate succession determines distribution of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Real estate in deceased's name</li>
<li>Bank accounts without beneficiary designations</li>
<li>Investment accounts without TOD designations</li>
<li>Personal property</li>
<li>Business interests</li>
<li>Any assets not passing by other means</li>
</ul>
<h3>What Intestate Succession Does NOT Control</h3>
<p>These assets pass outside intestate succession:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Joint tenancy property</strong> ‚Üí Passes to surviving joint tenant</li>
<li><strong>Life insurance</strong> ‚Üí Passes to named beneficiary</li>
<li><strong>Retirement accounts</strong> ‚Üí Passes to designated beneficiary</li>
<li><strong>Trust assets</strong> ‚Üí Passes per trust terms</li>
<li><strong>POD/TOD accounts</strong> ‚Üí Passes to named beneficiaries</li>
<li><strong>Community property with right of survivorship</strong> ‚Üí Passes to surviving spouse</li>
</ul>
<h2>California's Intestate Succession Priority Order</h2>
<p>California law creates a strict priority system for determining heirs.</p>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6400:</strong> "Any part of the estate of a decedent not effectively disposed of by will passes to the decedent's heirs as prescribed in this part."</p>
<h3>Priority Hierarchy (Simplified)</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Surviving spouse or domestic partner</strong></li>
<li><strong>Children</strong> (and descendants of deceased children)</li>
<li><strong>Parents</strong></li>
<li><strong>Siblings</strong> (and descendants of deceased siblings)</li>
<li><strong>Grandparents</strong> and their descendants</li>
<li><strong>Great-grandparents</strong> and their descendants</li>
<li><strong>Predeceased spouse's next of kin</strong></li>
<li><strong>State of California</strong> (escheat)</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Important:</strong> Lower-priority heirs only inherit if no higher-priority heirs exist.</p>
<h2>Community Property vs. Separate Property</h2>
<p>Before determining who inherits, you must classify property as community or separate.</p>
<h3>Community Property</h3>
<p><strong>California Family Code Section 760:</strong> Property acquired during marriage by either spouse is presumed to be community property.</p>
<p><strong>Includes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Wages and salaries earned during marriage</li>
<li>Real estate purchased during marriage</li>
<li>Bank accounts funded during marriage</li>
<li>Investments acquired during marriage</li>
<li>Retirement benefits earned during marriage</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Each spouse owns:</strong> 50% of all community property</p>
<p><strong>At death:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Surviving spouse automatically keeps their 50%</li>
<li>Deceased spouse's 50% passes per intestate succession</li>
</ul>
<h3>Separate Property</h3>
<p><strong>Includes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Property owned before marriage</li>
<li>Gifts and inheritances (even during marriage)</li>
<li>Property acquired after legal separation</li>
<li>Property purchased with separate funds</li>
<li>Rents and income from separate property</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>At death:</strong> 100% of separate property passes per intestate succession</p>
<h3>Why This Matters</h3>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
$1 million house purchased during marriage.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Community property:</strong> $500K is already surviving spouse's, only $500K passes through intestate succession</li>
<li><strong>Separate property:</strong> Full $1 million passes through intestate succession</li>
</ul>
<p>This distinction dramatically affects distributions.</p>
<h2>Spousal Share Under Intestate Succession</h2>
<h3>Scenario 1: Spouse Only (No Children, No Parents, No Siblings)</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6401(a)</strong></p>
<p><strong>Spouse inherits:</strong> 100% of everything (both community and separate property)</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
Married couple, no children. Decedent owns:</p>
<ul>
<li>$500K community property (house)</li>
<li>$200K separate property (inheritance)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Distribution:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Spouse gets: $250K (own community share) + $250K (deceased's community share) + $200K (deceased's separate) = $700K total</li>
</ul>
<h3>Scenario 2: Spouse + Children (All from Current Relationship)</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6401(c)(1)</strong></p>
<p><strong>Community property:</strong> 100% to spouse
<strong>Separate property:</strong> 100% to spouse (if one child) OR 50% to spouse, 50% to children (if multiple children)</p>
<p><strong>Example with one child:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Spouse inherits 100% of everything</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Example with two children:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Community property: 100% to spouse</li>
<li>Separate property: 50% to spouse, 25% to each child</li>
</ul>
<h3>Scenario 3: Spouse + Children from Prior Relationship</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6401(c)(2)</strong></p>
<p><strong>Community property:</strong> 100% to spouse
<strong>Separate property:</strong> 50% to spouse, 50% to ALL children</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
Decedent has two children from first marriage. Remarried with $1M community property home, $400K separate property.</p>
<p><strong>Distribution:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Community property ($1M): Spouse gets $500K (own share) + $500K (deceased's share) = $1M total</li>
<li>Separate property ($400K): Spouse gets $200K, each child gets $100K</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Problem:</strong> Spouse and children from first marriage now co-own house pro rata.</p>
<h3>Scenario 4: Spouse + Parents (No Children)</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6401(b)</strong></p>
<p><strong>Community property:</strong> 100% to spouse
<strong>Separate property:</strong> 50% to spouse, 50% to parents</p>
<h3>Scenario 5: Spouse + Siblings (No Children, No Parents)</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6401(c)</strong></p>
<p><strong>Community property:</strong> 100% to spouse
<strong>Separate property:</strong> 50% to spouse, 50% to siblings</p>
<h2>Distribution When No Surviving Spouse</h2>
<p>When there's no surviving spouse or domestic partner, distribution follows this order:</p>
<h3>Level 1: Children</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402(a)</strong></p>
<p><strong>All children inherit equally.</strong></p>
<p><strong>If child predeceased:</strong> Their children (decedent's grandchildren) inherit by right of representation.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
Three children: Anna, Ben, Carlos. Carlos predeceased, leaving two children.</p>
<ul>
<li>Anna: 1/3</li>
<li>Ben: 1/3</li>
<li>Carlos's two children: 1/6 each (splitting Carlos's 1/3)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Level 2: Parents</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402(b)</strong></p>
<p><strong>If no children:</strong> Parents inherit 100%, divided equally if both alive.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
Unmarried decedent, no children, both parents alive.</p>
<ul>
<li>Mother: 50%</li>
<li>Father: 50%</li>
</ul>
<h3>Level 3: Siblings</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402(c)</strong></p>
<p><strong>If no children or parents:</strong> Siblings inherit equally.</p>
<p><strong>If sibling predeceased:</strong> That sibling's children (nieces/nephews) inherit by representation.</p>
<h3>Level 4: Grandparents and Descendants</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402(d)</strong></p>
<p><strong>If no closer relatives:</strong> Estate divided:</p>
<ul>
<li>50% to paternal grandparents (or their descendants)</li>
<li>50% to maternal grandparents (or their descendants)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Can include:</strong> Aunts, uncles, first cousins</p>
<h3>Level 5: Next of Kin</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402(e)</strong></p>
<p>If none of above exist, estate passes to "next of kin" determined by relationship degree.</p>
<h3>Level 6: Predeceased Spouse's Relatives</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6402.5</strong></p>
<p>In limited circumstances, property can pass to deceased spouse's relatives if:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property was community property or received from predeceased spouse</li>
<li>No other heirs exist</li>
</ul>
<h3>Level 7: State of California</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6800</strong></p>
<p>If absolutely no relatives exist, property "escheats" to California.</p>
<p><strong>Rare:</strong> Happens in fewer than 1% of estates.</p>
<h2>Right of Representation (Per Stirpes)</h2>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 240</strong></p>
<p>When an heir dies before the decedent, that heir's descendants take their place.</p>
<h3>How It Works</h3>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
John dies with three children: Alice, Bob, Carol.
Bob predeceased John, leaving two children (John's grandchildren).</p>
<p><strong>Distribution:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Alice: 1/3</li>
<li>Carol: 1/3</li>
<li>Bob's two children: 1/6 each (they split Bob's 1/3 share)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key point:</strong> Bob's children don't inherit directly‚Äîthey inherit by representation of Bob.</p>
<h3>Multiple Generations</h3>
<p>If Bob's children also predeceased, Bob's grandchildren would inherit Bob's share, divided among them.</p>
<h2>Special Intestate Succession Rules</h2>
<h3>Half-Siblings</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6406</strong></p>
<p>Half-siblings inherit <strong>equally</strong> with full siblings.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong>
Two full siblings, one half-sibling ‚Üí each inherits 1/3.</p>
<h3>Adopted Children</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6450-6451</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Adopted children</strong> inherit from adoptive parents <strong>same as biological children</strong></li>
<li><strong>Generally do NOT</strong> inherit from biological parents</li>
<li><strong>Exception:</strong> Stepparent adoption maintains rights from birth parent</li>
</ul>
<h3>Children Born Out of Wedlock</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6453</strong></p>
<p><strong>From mother:</strong> Automatic relationship</p>
<p><strong>From father:</strong> Relationship established by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Marriage to mother</li>
<li>Court order of paternity</li>
<li>Father's acknowledgment</li>
<li>DNA evidence after death</li>
</ul>
<h3>Posthumous Children</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 6407</strong></p>
<p>Children conceived before death but born after death inherit as if born during parent's lifetime.</p>
<h3>Domestic Partners</h3>
<p><strong>California Family Code Section 297.5</strong></p>
<p><strong>Registered domestic partners</strong> have <strong>identical rights</strong> to married spouses.</p>
<p><strong>Requirements:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Must be registered with California Secretary of State</li>
<li>Informal partnerships don't qualify</li>
</ul>
<h3>Stepchildren</h3>
<p><strong>Generally do NOT inherit</strong> unless:</p>
<ul>
<li>Legally adopted, OR</li>
<li>Very limited circumstances under Probate Code Section 6454 (relationship began during minority and continued throughout lives)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Relatives of the Half-Blood</h3>
<p><strong>Inherit equally</strong> with whole-blood relatives.</p>
<h3>Posthumous Relatives</h3>
<p>Heirs conceived before but born after decedent's death inherit.</p>
<h2>Who CANNOT Inherit Under Intestate Succession</h2>
<p><strong>Excluded parties:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Unmarried partners</strong> (not registered)</li>
<li><strong>Stepchildren</strong> (unless adopted)</li>
<li><strong>Foster children</strong> (unless adopted)</li>
<li><strong>Friends</strong></li>
<li><strong>Caregivers</strong></li>
<li><strong>Charities</strong></li>
<li><strong>Former spouses</strong> (after divorce finalized)</li>
<li><strong>Pets</strong> (cannot legally inherit)</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Exception:</strong> Registered domestic partners have full rights.</p>
<h2>How Intestate Succession Works: The Process</h2>
<h3>Step 1: Someone Opens Probate</h3>
<p>Interested party files Petition for Letters of Administration in probate court.</p>
<h3>Step 2: Court Appoints Administrator</h3>
<p><strong>Priority for appointment (Probate Code ¬ß8461):</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Surviving spouse/partner</li>
<li>Children</li>
<li>Grandchildren</li>
<li>Other heirs</li>
<li>Public administrator</li>
<li>Creditors</li>
<li>Any other person</li>
</ol>
<h3>Step 3: Administrator Identifies Heirs</h3>
<p>Administrator must:</p>
<ul>
<li>Determine all legal heirs</li>
<li>Prove relationships</li>
<li>Locate heirs</li>
<li>Provide notice to heirs</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 4: Notice to Heirs</h3>
<p><strong>California Probate Code Section 1220</strong></p>
<p>All heirs must receive notice of probate proceeding.</p>
<h3>Step 5: Creditor Claims Period</h3>
<p><strong>Minimum 4 months</strong> for creditors to file claims.</p>
<h3>Step 6: Administrator Manages Estate</h3>
<ul>
<li>Gather assets</li>
<li>Pay debts</li>
<li>File taxes</li>
<li>Manage property</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 7: Final Distribution</h3>
<p>After minimum waiting periods and all obligations satisfied:</p>
<ul>
<li>Court approves final accounting</li>
<li>Assets distributed per intestate succession</li>
<li>Probate closes</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Timeline:</strong> 12-24 months typically</p>
<p><strong>Costs:</strong> $20,000-$100,000+ depending on estate value</p>
<h2>Problems Created by Intestate Succession</h2>
<h3>Problem #1: Wrong People May Inherit</h3>
<p><strong>Estranged relatives:</strong> Still inherit regardless of relationship quality</p>
<p><strong>Unmarried partners:</strong> Inherit nothing, even after decades together</p>
<p><strong>Stepchildren:</strong> Excluded even if raised as own children</p>
<p><strong>Charities:</strong> Cannot inherit (no provision for charitable giving)</p>
<h3>Problem #2: No Control Over Timing</h3>
<p><strong>Children inherit at age 18</strong> with no restrictions.</p>
<p>Most 18-year-olds lack maturity to manage substantial inheritances.</p>
<p><strong>No ability to:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Delay distribution until older age</li>
<li>Create gradual distributions</li>
<li>Impose conditions</li>
<li>Protect from creditors or divorces</li>
</ul>
<h3>Problem #3: Increased Family Conflict</h3>
<p><strong>Without written instructions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Disputes over who should be administrator</li>
<li>Arguments over asset values</li>
<li>Conflicts about selling property</li>
<li>Questions about decedent's wishes</li>
</ul>
<h3>Problem #4: Co-Ownership Problems</h3>
<p>Intestate succession often creates multiple co-owners of property (especially homes).</p>
<p><strong>Issues:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>All co-owners must agree on decisions</li>
<li>Selling requires unanimous consent</li>
<li>One heir can force partition sale</li>
<li>Management disputes</li>
</ul>
<h3>Problem #5: No Guardian Nomination</h3>
<p><strong>Critical for parents with minor children</strong></p>
<p>Without will, court decides who raises your children.</p>
<h3>Problem #6: No Asset Protection</h3>
<p><strong>No protection for:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Beneficiaries with substance abuse issues</li>
<li>Financially irresponsible heirs</li>
<li>Beneficiaries in bad marriages</li>
<li>Minor children (court-supervised guardianship required)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Problem #7: No Tax Planning</h3>
<p>Large estates may pay unnecessary taxes.</p>
<h3>Problem #8: Higher Costs</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bond required:</strong> Intestate estates typically require administrator to post bond</li>
<li><strong>Additional attorney time:</strong> Determining heirs and shares takes more work</li>
<li><strong>Family disputes:</strong> Conflict resolution adds costs</li>
</ul>
<h3>Problem #9: Probate Cannot Be Avoided</h3>
<p>With living trust, assets avoid probate.</p>
<p>Without planning, full probate required (12-24 months, $20K-$100K+).</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Does surviving spouse automatically inherit everything?</h3>
<p>No. It depends on whether you have children from other relationships and whether property is community or separate. See scenarios above.</p>
<h3>Can unmarried partners inherit through intestate succession?</h3>
<p>Only if registered as domestic partners with California Secretary of State. Informal partnerships don't qualify.</p>
<h3>What if I want to disinherit someone?</h3>
<p>You cannot disinherit via intestate succession. The only way is to create a will that specifically provides otherwise.</p>
<h3>Do stepchildren inherit from stepparents?</h3>
<p>Generally no, unless legally adopted or very limited circumstances under Probate Code Section 6454.</p>
<h3>What happens if no relatives can be found?</h3>
<p>After extensive search, property escheats to State of California. This is extremely rare.</p>
<h3>Can creditors still collect debts?</h3>
<p>Yes. Creditors have priority over heirs. Debts must be paid before distribution to heirs.</p>
<h3>How long does intestate probate take?</h3>
<p>Same as regular probate: 12-24 months typically.</p>
<h3>Is intestate probate more expensive?</h3>
<p>Statutory fees are same, but additional costs include bond premiums and extra attorney time to determine heirs.</p>
<h3>What if someone contests their share?</h3>
<p>They can petition court claiming they should inherit more. Court determines rights under intestate succession law.</p>
<h3>Can heirs agree to different distribution?</h3>
<p>Yes, if all heirs agree in writing, they can redistribute differently than intestate succession requires.</p>
