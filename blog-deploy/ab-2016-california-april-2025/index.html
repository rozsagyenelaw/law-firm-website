You may have heard the news: starting April 1, 2025, California will allow heirs to transfer a primary residence worth up to $750,000 without going through formal probate. This is a dramatic increase from the current threshold of approximately $184,500. For some families, this sounds like the end of expensive probate and the beginning of easy estate transfers.

What AB 2016 Actually Does

Assembly Bill 2016, signed into law in 2024, makes two significant changes to California's small estate procedures effective April 1, 2025:

Change #1: Higher Threshold for Primary Residences

The law creates a new, higher threshold specifically for a decedent's primary residence:

Asset Type Before April 2025 After April 2025
Primary residence $184,500 $750,000
Other real property $184,500 $184,500*
Personal property $184,500 $184,500*

*These thresholds are adjusted annually for inflation

This means that if someone dies owning a primary residence worth less than $750,000, their heirs may be able to transfer that property using a simplified affidavit procedure rather than going through the full California probate process.

Change #2: Simplified Recording Process

The law also streamlines the process for recording the affidavit with the county recorder, making it somewhat easier to complete the transfer.

What This Means in Practice: If your mother owned a $650,000 home as her primary residence and passed away after April 1, 2025, you could potentially transfer that home to yourself using an affidavit rather than going through probate. Before this law, you'd need formal probate for any home worth over ~$184,500.

The Catch-22: Why This Law May Hurt More Than It Helps

On the surface, AB 2016 looks like a win for California families. But dig deeper, and you'll find a troubling "Catch-22" that makes this law far less helpful than it appears:

Catch #1: The 6-Month Waiting Period

To use the small estate affidavit for real property, you must wait at least 6 months after the person's death. During this time:

  • The property sits in limbo
  • You cannot sell or refinance the home
  • Property taxes, insurance, and maintenance continue
  • Mortgage payments (if any) must be made—but you have no legal authority over the property

With a living trust, property transfers immediately. There's no waiting period. Your successor trustee has authority the day you pass away.

Catch #2: No Privacy Protection

The small estate affidavit is a public document filed with the county recorder. It includes:

  • The property address
  • The property value
  • The names and addresses of all heirs
  • Information about other estate assets

This information becomes permanently searchable public record. Scammers, marketers, and anyone else can find it.

A living trust, by contrast, is a private document. It's never filed with the court or recorder. No one—except the people you choose—knows what you owned or who inherited it.

Catch #3: Liability for Debts

When you use a small estate affidavit, you become personally liable for the decedent's debts up to the value of the property you receive. This liability lasts for years after the transfer.

Did your parent have:

  • Credit card debt?
  • Medical bills?
  • Unpaid taxes?
  • Medi-Cal recovery claims?

With a small estate affidavit, creditors can come after you personally.

Catch #4: No Protection During Incapacity

AB 2016 only helps after death. It does absolutely nothing if you become incapacitated—unable to manage your own affairs due to illness, injury, or dementia.

Without a living trust and power of attorney, your family would need to go to court for a conservatorship—a process that's more expensive and invasive than probate.

A living trust with proper powers of attorney provides seamless management if you become incapacitated. No court involvement. No public proceedings. No delays.

Catch #5: Only Works for Primary Residence

The $750,000 threshold only applies to the decedent's primary residence. Other real property—rental homes, vacation properties, inherited land—still has the old ~$184,500 threshold.

If you own any real property beyond your primary residence, AB 2016 won't help you avoid probate for those assets.

The Bottom Line: AB 2016 offers a complicated, delayed, public, liability-creating procedure that only works after death and only for primary residences under $750,000. A living trust offers immediate, private, liability-free transfer of ALL your assets—during incapacity AND after death—for the same cost or less.

The Medi-Cal "Cliff": Why LA County Seniors Should Be Especially Concerned

There's another critical factor that makes AB 2016 particularly risky for aging Los Angeles County residents: the convergence of changing Medi-Cal rules in 2025 and 2026.

What's Happening with Medi-Cal Asset Limits

California has been gradually expanding Medi-Cal eligibility by eliminating asset limits for many beneficiaries. However, this creates a complicated situation:

  • Eligibility: More people qualify for Medi-Cal because asset limits have been eliminated or expanded
  • Estate Recovery: California still pursues estate recovery—seeking reimbursement from deceased beneficiaries' estates for Medi-Cal costs
  • The Cliff: People who qualify for Medi-Cal more easily may not realize their home is exposed to recovery claims after death

How AB 2016 Makes This Worse

The small estate affidavit procedure creates a public record that makes Medi-Cal recovery easier:

  1. You file the affidavit, declaring the property value and your identity
  2. This filing is easily searchable by the Department of Health Care Services
  3. DHCS can assert a claim against you personally (remember—you're liable for the decedent's debts)
  4. You may owe tens or hundreds of thousands in Medi-Cal reimbursement

With a living trust properly structured, there are strategies to minimize Medi-Cal recovery exposure. But the small estate affidavit offers no such protection—and actually makes recovery easier by creating a clear public trail.

The 2025/2026 "Cliff": As Medi-Cal eligibility expands, more seniors are receiving benefits. Many don't realize that their home—their largest asset—may be subject to recovery claims after death. Relying on AB 2016 instead of proper trust planning could cost families hundreds of thousands of dollars in Medi-Cal liens.

The April 2025 Trap: Why Waiting Is Dangerous

Some people are thinking: "I'll wait until April 2025, see how the new law works, and then decide if I need a trust."

This is a dangerous gamble for several reasons:

Reason #1: You Can't Predict When You'll Need It

Estate planning isn't something you need "someday"—it's something you might need tomorrow. Accidents, sudden illness, and unexpected death don't wait for convenient timing.

If something happens before April 2025, your family faces the old rules and full probate. Even if something happens after April 2025, the small estate affidavit has a 6-month waiting period—plus all the other drawbacks.

Reason #2: Home Values Keep Rising

Los Angeles County home values continue to appreciate. A home worth $700,000 today might be worth $780,000 by next year. Then AB 2016 doesn't help you at all.

A living trust protects you regardless of your home's value. Create it now, and you're covered whether your home is worth $500,000 or $5,000,000.

Reason #3: The Law Could Change

AB 2016 was passed by the legislature and could be modified or repealed by a future legislature. Laws change. Relying on a specific law that hasn't even taken effect yet is risky.

A living trust is your own private document that doesn't depend on legislative whims.

Reason #4: The Medi-Cal Window Is Closing

The 2025/2026 Medi-Cal changes create a narrow window for planning. Seniors who might need long-term care in the coming years should be planning now—not waiting to see how AB 2016 works out.

Frequently Asked Questions About AB 2016

What is AB 2016 and when does it take effect?

AB 2016 is a California law that raises the small estate threshold for real property from approximately $184,500 to $750,000 for primary residences. It takes effect on April 1, 2025. This allows heirs to transfer a deceased person's primary residence valued under $750,000 using a simplified affidavit procedure instead of full probate. However, important limitations apply, and this procedure does not provide the same benefits as a living trust.

Does AB 2016 mean I don't need a living trust anymore?

No. AB 2016 is not a replacement for a living trust. While it may allow some estates to avoid formal probate, the small estate affidavit procedure has significant limitations: it requires waiting 6 months after death, creates a public record, may trigger Medi-Cal recovery claims, offers no protection during incapacity, and provides no privacy. A living trust avoids all these problems and costs as little as $575.

What are the requirements to use AB 2016's small estate affidavit for real property?

To use the small estate affidavit procedure for real property under AB 2016, you must: (1) wait at least 6 months after death, (2) the property must be the decedent's primary residence, (3) the property value must be under $750,000, (4) no probate proceeding can be pending, (5) there must be no disputes among heirs, and (6) California estate taxes cannot be owed. You must also file the affidavit with the county recorder and may face liability for decedent's debts.

How does AB 2016 affect Medi-Cal estate recovery?

AB 2016 does not protect against Medi-Cal estate recovery claims. If the deceased received Medi-Cal benefits, the state can still seek reimbursement from the estate, including the primary residence. Using the small estate affidavit creates a public record that makes it easier for Medi-Cal to identify and pursue recovery. This is especially concerning for aging LA County residents who may need long-term care.

Why should I act before April 1, 2025?

While AB 2016 takes effect April 1, 2025, waiting to plan is risky for several reasons: (1) home values can appreciate above $750,000 quickly, (2) Medi-Cal asset limits are changing in 2025-2026, creating potential estate recovery exposure, (3) AB 2016 only helps after death—not during incapacity, (4) a living trust provides immediate benefits while costing the same as you might pay in affidavit filing fees. Planning now ensures protection regardless of future law changes.
