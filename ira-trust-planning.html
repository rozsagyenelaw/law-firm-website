Understanding the SECURE Act & IRA Trusts

The Setting Every Community Up for Retirement Enhancement (SECURE) Act, passed in 2019, fundamentally changed inherited IRA rules. Previously, non-spouse beneficiaries could "stretch" IRA distributions over their lifetime, potentially extending tax-deferred growth for decades. This made IRAs one of the best assets to inherit.

The SECURE Act eliminated the stretch IRA for most beneficiaries. Now, most non-spouse beneficiaries must withdraw all IRA funds within 10 years of the account owner's death. This can create massive tax bills, forcing beneficiaries to take large distributions during their peak earning years when they're already in high tax brackets. A $1 million inherited IRA could generate $370,000+ in income taxes if withdrawn quickly.

IRA trusts (also called retirement benefit trusts) are specialized trusts designed to be beneficiaries of IRAs, 401(k)s, and other retirement accounts. When properly structured as "see-through trusts," they qualify for the same distribution rules as individual beneficiaries while providing asset protection, creditor protection, divorce protection, and control over distributions to spendthrift or vulnerable beneficiaries.

Two main types exist: Conduit trusts automatically distribute all IRA distributions to beneficiaries immediately, providing no asset protection but ensuring optimal tax treatment. Accumulation trusts allow trustees to hold distributions in trust, providing maximum asset protection and control, but distributions held in trust are taxed at compressed trust tax rates (37% bracket starts at just $15,200 in 2024). We help you choose the right structure balancing tax efficiency with protection and control based on your unique situation and beneficiary needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

How did the SECURE Act change inherited IRA rules?
The SECURE Act eliminated the "stretch IRA" for most beneficiaries. Previously, non-spouse beneficiaries could take Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) over their lifetime, potentially stretching distributions for decades with continued tax-deferred growth. Now, most beneficiaries must withdraw all funds within 10 years of the account owner's death, potentially creating massive tax bills. Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (surviving spouses, minor children until age of majority, disabled/chronically ill individuals, and beneficiaries within 10 years of age of the deceased) can still stretch distributions over their lifetime.
What is a see-through trust and why do I need one for my IRA?
A see-through trust (also called a qualifying trust) is an IRA beneficiary trust that meets specific IRS requirements, allowing the trust to be "looked through" to the individual trust beneficiaries for distribution purposes. This is critical because naming a non-qualifying trust can force immediate taxation of the entire IRA within 5 years. See-through trusts must: be valid under state law, be irrevocable upon death, have identifiable beneficiaries who are individuals, and provide required documentation to the IRA custodian by October 31 of the year following death.
Should I use a conduit trust or accumulation trust for my IRA?
Conduit trusts automatically distribute all IRA distributions directly to beneficiaries immediately. They provide no asset protection or control but ensure RMDs qualify for optimal treatment. Accumulation trusts allow trustees to hold distributions in trust rather than distributing them, providing creditor protection, divorce protection, and control over spendthrift beneficiaries. However, distributions held in trust are taxed at compressed trust tax rates (37% bracket starts at just $15,200 in 2024 vs. $609,350 for individuals). Choice depends on whether protection and control outweigh additional taxes.
Can I leave my IRA to my living trust?
Generally no - naming your revocable living trust as IRA beneficiary usually requires the entire IRA to be distributed and taxed within 5 years (or 10 years under SECURE Act), losing all future tax-deferred growth. This is because revocable trusts typically don't qualify as see-through trusts, and even if they do, they often have multiple beneficiaries of different ages or charities as beneficiaries, which affects distribution rules. However, a properly drafted standalone IRA trust or specific see-through trust provisions in your living trust can preserve tax-deferred treatment while providing control and protection.
What are the tax implications of inherited IRAs?
Inherited traditional IRA distributions are taxed as ordinary income to beneficiaries at their marginal tax rate. Under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule, beneficiaries must withdraw all funds within 10 years, potentially forcing large withdrawals during peak earning years in high tax brackets. For example, a 40-year-old inheriting a $1 million IRA might be forced to withdraw $100,000+ annually (if spread evenly) when already earning $150,000, pushing them into the 32-35% federal bracket plus state taxes. Roth IRA inheritances are tax-free but still subject to the 10-year rule, meaning tax-free growth ends after 10 years. Strategic planning can spread distributions to minimize total taxes.
