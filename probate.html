How Long Does Probate Take in California?

California probate typically takes 12-18 months for straightforward estates, though complex cases can take 2-3+ years. The minimum timeline is about 9 months due to statutory waiting periods mandated by California Probate Code.

Why does probate take so long in Los Angeles County? Several factors cause delays: court backlogs (hearings scheduled 60-90 days apart), mandatory 4-month creditor claim period, real estate sales (3-12 months), will contests or litigation (adds 6-24+ months), and executor delays filing required documents.

How to speed up probate: Request Independent Administration (IAEA) authority to avoid multiple court hearings, file all documents correctly the first time, respond promptly to creditor claims, and work with an experienced probate attorney who knows Los Angeles Superior Court procedures. With IAEA and no complications, probate can be completed in 9-12 months.

California Small Estate Affidavit - Avoid Probate Under $184,500

If the deceased's estate is valued at $184,500 or less, you can use a California Small Estate Affidavit (Form DE-305) instead of going through full probate. This simplified procedure takes days or weeks instead of 12-18 months and costs under $100 instead of thousands in probate fees.

What is the $184,500 California probate limit? This threshold applies to the gross value of personal property (bank accounts, vehicles, stocks, personal belongings) - not equity after debts. Real estate has a separate $61,500 limit. If the estate exceeds these amounts, full probate is required unless assets are in a living trust.

How to use small estate affidavit: Wait 40 days from death, complete Form DE-305 listing all assets and heirs, attach a certified death certificate, and present the affidavit to banks, DMV, or other asset holders. They must release the assets to rightful heirs without court proceedings. Most California homeowners exceed the $184,500 limit, which is why living trusts are essential for probate avoidance.

What Happens If You Die Without a Will in California?

When someone dies without a will in California (called "intestate"), their assets are distributed according to California's intestate succession laws under Probate Code Sections 6400-6455. The court appoints an administrator (not executor) to manage the estate through probate.

Who inherits without a will in California? The distribution order is: surviving spouse (gets community property plus portion of separate property), children (share equally if no spouse), parents (if no spouse or children), siblings, then more distant relatives. If no relatives are found, the estate goes to the State of California (escheat). Importantly, unmarried partners and step-children inherit NOTHING without a will - only legal spouses and blood/adopted relatives have inheritance rights.

The probate process is the same whether someone died with or without a will, but intestacy can cause family disputes and unintended distributions. California intestate succession may not align with your actual wishes, which is why creating a will or living trust is essential.

Executor Duties in California - Letters Testamentary & Responsibilities

California executors have extensive legal responsibilities managing the deceased's estate through probate. After filing the Petition for Probate, the executor receives letters testamentary - the court document giving legal authority to act on behalf of the estate.

What does an executor do? Key duties include: filing probate petition and will with court, obtaining letters testamentary, notifying beneficiaries and creditors, inventorying and appraising all assets, managing estate property, paying debts and taxes, selling real estate if necessary, distributing assets to beneficiaries, filing final accounting, and closing the estate. Executors must act in beneficiaries' best interests and follow California Probate Code requirements.

How much does an executor get paid in California? Executor fees are statutory: 4% of first $100,000, 3% of next $100,000, 2% of next $800,000, 1% of next $9 million. For a $500,000 estate, executor compensation is $13,000. Executors can request additional fees for extraordinary services like litigation or business management. Beneficiary Rights in California Probate - Accounting & Timeline

California beneficiaries have significant legal rights during probate. Under Probate Code Section 10950, beneficiaries can demand accounting showing all estate transactions, including assets received, income earned, expenses paid, and distributions made.

How long does a beneficiary have to wait for inheritance? Beneficiaries typically wait 9-18 months to receive their inheritance through probate. What Does an Executor Do in California?

An executor (also called a personal representative) is the person named in a will to manage the deceased person's estate through probate. If there is no will, the court appoints an administrator who performs the same duties. In California, serving as an executor is a serious legal responsibility that requires careful attention to detail, fiduciary duty, and compliance with California Probate Code sections 8400-8573.

The executor's primary role is to protect estate assets, pay valid debts and taxes, and distribute remaining assets to beneficiaries according to the will or California law. This process typically takes 9-18 months and involves court supervision to ensure proper administration.

Complete Executor Responsibilities Checklist

  1. Locate and secure the will - File the original will with the Los Angeles County Superior Court within 30 days of death (Probate Code 8200)
  2. File petition for probate - Submit Form DE-111 and pay filing fees (currently $435-$465 depending on estate value)
  3. Obtain Letters Testamentary - Receive official court authorization to act as executor (Form DE-150)
  4. Notify all beneficiaries and heirs - Send formal notice within 60 days using Form DE-154
  5. Publish notice to creditors - Run newspaper notice for three consecutive weeks in Los Angeles County
  6. Send direct notice to known creditors - Mail Form DE-157 within four months of appointment
  7. Inventory all estate assets - Complete Form DE-160 within four months, including all real estate, bank accounts, investments, vehicles, and personal property
  8. Obtain professional appraisals - Hire court-appointed probate referee for non-cash assets
  9. Open estate bank account - Establish separate account using your EIN for estate finances
  10. Pay valid debts and taxes - Review claims, pay legitimate creditors, file final tax returns (1040 and 1041)
  11. Manage estate property - Maintain insurance, pay mortgages, collect rents, secure vacant homes
  12. Sell assets if necessary - Obtain court approval for real estate sales using Form DE-260
  13. File accounting with court - Submit detailed financial report (Form DE-165) showing all receipts and disbursements
  14. Request distribution approval - File petition for final distribution (Form DE-295)
  15. Distribute assets to beneficiaries - Transfer property after court approval and obtain receipts
  16. Close estate - File final report and request discharge as executor

Letters Testamentary Explained

Letters Testamentary (Form DE-150) are official court documents that prove your legal authority to act as executor. You'll need certified copies of these letters to access bank accounts, sell property, collect assets, and conduct estate business. In Los Angeles County, you typically receive Letters Testamentary 6-8 weeks after filing your petition, assuming no contests or complications arise.

If the will requests Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, your Letters Testamentary will include this designation, allowing you to take many actions without prior court approval. Banks, title companies, and financial institutions will require certified copies (with the court seal) dated within 60 days.

Executor Compensation in California

California law provides statutory compensation for executors based on the gross value of the estate (Probate Code 10810). Executor fees are calculated as follows:

  • 4% of the first $100,000
  • 3% of the next $100,000
  • 2% of the next $800,000
  • 1% of the next $9,000,000
  • 0.5% of the next $15,000,000
  • Reasonable amount for estates over $25,000,000

For example, an executor of a $1,000,000 estate would receive $23,000 in statutory fees. You may also request additional "extraordinary" compensation for unusual services like managing a business, handling litigation, or selling complex assets. Compensation is subject to income tax and must be approved by the court before payment.

Can an Executor Be Removed in California?

Yes. Under Probate Code 8502, the court can remove an executor for several reasons, including:

  • Wasting, embezzling, or mismanaging estate assets
  • Failing to perform duties or file required accountings
  • Gross negligence or incompetence
  • Self-dealing or conflicts of interest
  • Refusing to follow court orders
  • Insolvency, conviction of a felony, or becoming incapacitated
  • Acting in bad faith or with hostility toward beneficiaries

Beneficiaries can petition the court to remove an executor by filing Form DE-200. The court will hold a hearing to determine if removal is warranted. If the executor is removed, the court will appoint a successor administrator. Removed executors may also face personal liability for losses caused by their misconduct and could forfeit their right to compensation.

When You Need an Attorney's Help

While California law doesn't require executors to hire an attorney, most probate courts and legal professionals strongly recommend it. You should definitely consult a probate attorney if:

  • The estate is worth more than $500,000
  • The estate includes real property or complex assets
  • There are disputes among beneficiaries
  • Someone is contesting the will
  • You're unfamiliar with probate procedures and court requirements
  • The deceased owned a business or had significant debts
  • There are tax implications (estate tax, capital gains, etc.)
  • You're concerned about personal liability

Attorney fees in probate are also statutory and calculated on the same schedule as executor fees. Legal representation protects you from personal liability, ensures compliance with all legal requirements, and typically speeds up the probate process significantly. In Los Angeles County, most probate cases with attorney representation close 4-6 months faster than those without.

How to File Probate in Los Angeles County: Step-by-Step Guide

Navigate the entire probate timeline from petition to distribution with this comprehensive procedural guide

California's probate process follows a structured court-supervised procedure designed to ensure proper estate administration. Understanding each step, required forms, and timeline expectations helps executors and beneficiaries navigate the process with confidence. Here's exactly what happens from start to finish in Los Angeles County Superior Court.

The Complete Probate Timeline

  1. File Petition for Probate (Month 1) - The executor or proposed administrator files Form DE-111 (Petition for Probate) with the Los Angeles County Superior Court. Filing fees range from $435-$465 depending on estate value. You must file the original will and death certificate. Processing time: 2-3 weeks for initial review.
  2. Notice of Petition to Administer Estate (Months 1-2) - After filing, you must provide notice to all heirs and beneficiaries. This includes publishing Form DE-121 in a newspaper of general circulation for three consecutive weeks and mailing notice to all interested parties at least 15 days before the hearing. Los Angeles requires publication in the Los Angeles Daily Journal or Metropolitan News.
  3. Probate Hearing (Month 2-3) - The court schedules a hearing typically 6-8 weeks after filing. If there are no objections or contests, the judge approves the petition and issues Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (Form DE-150). If you request Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, it's granted at this hearing.
  4. Obtain Letters and Bond (Month 3) - After the hearing, obtain certified copies of your Letters Testamentary from the court clerk. If the will doesn't waive bond, you must purchase a surety bond equal to the estate's value. Use these letters to access bank accounts, collect assets, and conduct estate business.
  5. Notice to Creditors (Months 3-4) - Within four months of appointment, send Form DE-157 (Notice of Administration to Creditors) to all known creditors. Also publish a creditor notice in the newspaper for three consecutive weeks. Creditors have the later of four months from appointment or 60 days from notice to file claims.
  6. Inventory and Appraisal (Month 4) - Complete Form DE-160 (Inventory and Appraisal) listing all estate assets with their date-of-death values. A court-appointed probate referee appraises non-cash assets. File the completed inventory with the court within four months of appointment. This establishes the estate's gross value for fee calculation.
  7. Pay Debts and Claims (Months 5-8) - Review all creditor claims filed with the court. Pay valid debts, reject improper claims, and negotiate disputed amounts. Handle ongoing expenses like mortgage payments, property taxes, insurance, and maintenance. Open an estate bank account using the estate's EIN for all financial transactions.
  8. Tax Returns and Clearances (Months 6-10) - File the decedent's final Form 1040 individual tax return. If the estate generates income, file Form 1041 estate income tax returns. If the estate exceeds federal exemption thresholds, file Form 706 estate tax return (due 9 months after death). Obtain tax clearances from IRS and California Franchise Tax Board before final distribution.
  9. Sell Assets if Necessary (Months 6-12) - If estate assets must be sold to pay debts or for distribution, obtain court approval. Real estate sales require filing Form DE-260 and court confirmation hearing unless you have IAEA authority. Handle title transfers, deeds, and distribution of sale proceeds.
  10. File First Accounting (Month 9-12) - Prepare Form DE-165 (First and Final Report and Account) detailing all estate receipts and disbursements. This accounting shows opening inventory, income received, expenses paid, distributions made, and remaining assets. Accuracy is critical as beneficiaries can object to unauthorized expenditures.
  11. Petition for Final Distribution (Month 12-15) - File Form DE-295 (Petition for Final Distribution) requesting court approval to distribute remaining assets to beneficiaries. Include a proposed distribution plan showing exactly what each beneficiary receives. The court schedules a hearing 4-6 weeks after filing.
  12. Final Hearing and Distribution Order (Month 13-16) - Attend the final distribution hearing. If the accounting and distribution plan are approved, the judge issues an Order for Final Distribution. Wait for the order to become final (usually 30 days) before making distributions. Transfer assets according to the court order.
  13. Distribute Assets and Obtain Receipts (Month 14-18) - Transfer property to beneficiaries according to the court order. Obtain signed receipts (Form DE-305) from each beneficiary acknowledging receipt. File receipts with the court showing distribution is complete.
  14. Close Estate and Discharge (Month 15-18) - File Form DE-295 requesting discharge as executor. Submit all beneficiary receipts proving complete distribution. The court issues an order discharging you from duties and closing the estate. Your executor responsibilities are complete.

Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA)

The Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) is a critical tool that allows executors to take many actions without prior court approval. Under Probate Code 10400-10592, IAEA authority significantly streamlines probate by eliminating the need for court petitions and hearings for routine transactions.

With IAEA authority, you can:

  • Sell real estate without court confirmation (subject to 15-day notice to beneficiaries)
  • Sell personal property without approval
  • Invest estate funds
  • Borrow money secured by estate property
  • Lease property for more than one year
  • Make repairs and improvements
  • Compromise claims and litigation

IAEA is typically requested in the original petition for probate and granted at the first hearing unless an interested party objects. Using IAEA can reduce probate duration by 3-6 months and save thousands in attorney fees and court costs. However, beneficiaries can file objections to proposed actions by submitting a written objection within 15 days of notice.

In Los Angeles County, probate typically takes 9-18 months from filing to closing. Simple estates with IAEA authority, no disputes, and straightforward assets can close in 9-12 months. Complex estates with real estate sales, tax issues, creditor disputes, or will contests may take 18-24 months or longer.

Factors affecting probate duration: Estate size and complexity, whether you have IAEA authority, real estate sales requirements, creditor claims volume, tax return complexity, beneficiary cooperation, court calendar availability, and whether an attorney is representing the estate. Hiring experienced probate counsel typically accelerates the process by avoiding procedural errors and court delays.

Died Without a Will? California Intestate Succession Explained

Understanding who inherits when there is no will under California's intestate succession statutes

When someone dies without a valid will in California, their estate passes according to intestate succession laws found in Probate Code 6400-6455. These statutes establish a strict hierarchy determining who inherits based on family relationships. Understanding intestacy rules is essential for families navigating probate without a will.

Who Inherits When There Is No Will in California?

California intestate succession follows this priority order:

1. Surviving Spouse or Domestic Partner

The spouse's share depends on what other relatives survive:

  • 100% of community property always goes to the surviving spouse
  • 100% of separate property if no children, parents, siblings, or their descendants survive
  • 1/2 of separate property if one child or descendants of one deceased child survive
  • 1/3 of separate property if more than one child survives, or one child plus descendants of deceased children
  • 1/2 of separate property if no children but parents or siblings survive

2. Children and Descendants

If no spouse survives, the entire estate goes to children equally. If a child predeceased the decedent, that child's share goes to their children (the decedent's grandchildren) by right of representation. California recognizes:

  • Biological children (legitimate and illegitimate)
  • Legally adopted children (same rights as biological)
  • Stepchildren and foster children (generally no inheritance rights unless legally adopted)
  • Children conceived before death but born afterward

3. Parents

If no spouse, children, or descendants survive, the estate goes to the decedent's parents equally. If one parent is deceased, the surviving parent inherits the entire estate.

4. Siblings and Their Descendants

If no parents survive, the estate goes to siblings equally. If a sibling is deceased, their share goes to their children (the decedent's nieces and nephews). Half-siblings inherit the same as full siblings.

5. More Distant Relatives

If none of the above survive, the estate continues to more distant relatives in this order: grandparents, aunts/uncles, cousins, and other next of kin. If absolutely no heirs can be found, the estate escheats to the State of California.

Administrator vs. Executor: What's the Difference?

When someone dies with a will, the person named to handle the estate is called an executor. When someone dies without a will, the court appoints an administrator to perform the same duties. Both roles have identical responsibilities and powers under California law.

Priority for appointment as administrator (Probate Code 8461):

  1. Surviving spouse or domestic partner
  2. Children
  3. Grandchildren
  4. Other issue (descendants)
  5. Parents
  6. Siblings
  7. Issue of siblings (nieces and nephews)
  8. Other next of kin
  9. Public administrator (if no family member petitions)
  10. Creditors
  11. Any other person (with court approval)

When Does Intestacy Apply?

Intestate succession applies when:

  • The decedent never created a will
  • The will is invalid (improper execution, lack of capacity, undue influence, fraud)
  • The will was revoked and not replaced
  • All beneficiaries named in the will predeceased the testator
  • The will only disposes of part of the estate (partial intestacy for remaining assets)
  • Assets weren't titled properly in a trust or didn't have beneficiary designations

Problems with Dying Intestate

Dying without a will creates several significant problems:

  • No control over distribution: State law decides who gets what, which may not match your wishes
  • Unmarried partners receive nothing: Boyfriends, girlfriends, and partners without legal marriage/domestic partnership have zero inheritance rights
  • No guardian nomination: Court decides who raises minor children without your input
  • Estranged family may inherit: Relatives you're not close to may receive assets while close friends receive nothing
  • Extended probate: Intestate probate typically takes longer and costs more
  • Bond requirement: Administrator must usually purchase costly surety bond
  • Family disputes: Intestacy often creates conflict among relatives over administration and distribution
  • No executor choice: Court appoints administrator based on priority list, not your preference
  • Tax inefficiency: Miss opportunities for tax planning and estate minimization strategies
  • Charitable giving impossible: State law doesn't allow inheritance to charities or non-relatives

Creating a will or living trust ensures your wishes are honored and dramatically simplifies the estate administration process for your family. Even a simple will is far better than no estate plan at all.

Small Estate Affidavit California: Avoid Full Probate

Fast-track estate administration for estates under $184,500 using simplified California procedures

California offers several simplified procedures to avoid formal probate for small estates. These alternatives save substantial time and money, typically resolving estates in 30-90 days instead of 9-18 months. If you qualify, small estate procedures can eliminate court supervision entirely and reduce administration costs by thousands of dollars.

California Small Estate Limit 2024

$184,500

Maximum estate value for small estate procedures in 2024

Under Probate Code 13100, estates valued at $184,500 or less (excluding certain property like vehicles, salary/wages, and joint tenancy assets) qualify for simplified small estate procedures. This threshold adjusts every three years for inflation. The $184,500 limit applies to the gross value of property subject to probate, not the net value after debts.

Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property (Form DE-5200)

The Small Estate Affidavit (Probate Code 13100-13116) is the simplest method to collect estate assets without formal probate. This procedure works for personal property only (not real estate) and requires no court filing or hearing.

How the Small Estate Affidavit Works:

  1. Wait 40 days: You must wait at least 40 days after the person's death before using this procedure
  2. Complete Form DE-5200: Fill out the Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value, providing details about the decedent, estate value, and your right to inherit
  3. Attach certified death certificate: Obtain official death certificate from LA County Registrar-Recorder

Contest a Will in California: Undue Influence & Executor Misconduct

Understanding will contests, executor removal, and beneficiary rights in California probate court

Probate disputes can arise when family members question the validity of a will, challenge executor decisions, or dispute asset distribution. California law provides specific grounds for contesting wills and removing executors, along with strict deadlines and procedures. Understanding your rights and the litigation process is essential if you're involved in a probate dispute.

Valid Grounds for Contesting a Will in California

Under Probate Code 8250-8272, you can contest a will only on specific legal grounds. Personal disappointment with your inheritance or family disagreements aren't sufficient. Valid grounds include:

1. Lack of Testamentary Capacity

The testator must have been of "sound mind" when signing the will (Probate Code 6100.5). To have capacity, the testator must understand: (1) the nature of making a will, (2) the extent of their property, and (3) their relationship to beneficiaries. Medical records, doctor testimony, and witness observations can establish lack of capacity due to dementia, Alzheimer's, mental illness, or medication effects.

2. Undue Influence

Someone used excessive persuasion, coercion, or manipulation to override the testator's free will. Undue influence often involves a confidential relationship (caregiver, attorney-in-fact, advisor) who benefited from the will while isolating the testator from family. California has a presumption of undue influence when someone in a fiduciary relationship unduly benefits (Probate Code 21380). Evidence includes: sudden will changes, isolation from family, unequal distribution, and the influencer controlling access to the testator.

3. Fraud or Forgery

Someone deceived the testator about material facts that induced them to sign the will, or the testator's signature was forged. Fraud includes lying about family members' conduct, forging the testator's signature, or substituting pages in the will document. Handwriting experts and document examiners can provide forensic evidence in forgery cases.

4. Improper Execution

The will doesn't meet California's formal execution requirements. Under Probate Code 6110, a valid will must be: (1) in writing, (2) signed by the testator (or by someone at testator's direction), and (3) witnessed by at least two competent witnesses who were present together and understood they were witnessing a will. Holographic wills (handwritten, no witnesses) have different requirements under 6111.

5. Revocation

The will was properly revoked by the testator before death. Revocation occurs by: (1) physically destroying the will with intent to revoke, (2) executing a later will that revokes prior wills, or (3) written declaration of revocation (Probate Code 6120). A presumption of revocation exists when a will last in the testator's possession cannot be found after death.

Critical Deadline: 120 Days to Contest a Will

IMPORTANT: You have only 120 days after the will is admitted to probate to file a will contest in California (Probate Code 8226). This deadline is strictly enforced.

The 120-day period begins when the court admits the will to probate and issues Letters Testamentary. If you miss this deadline, you generally cannot contest the will even if you have valid grounds. If you weren't properly notified of the probate proceeding, different deadlines may apply under 8227, but you must act quickly once you learn of the probate.

Executor Misconduct and Removal

Executors owe fiduciary duties to beneficiaries and must act with utmost good faith, loyalty, and honesty. Common types of executor misconduct include:

  • Self-dealing: Using estate assets for personal benefit, borrowing from estate, selling assets to themselves
  • Embezzlement: Taking estate funds, hiding assets, transferring property to themselves or relatives
  • Negligent management: Failing to maintain property, allowing insurance to lapse, letting assets deteriorate
  • Improper investments: Making risky investments, commingling estate funds with personal accounts
  • Failure to account: Not providing required financial reports, hiding transactions from beneficiaries
  • Delays and inaction: Unreasonably prolonging administration, failing to file required documents
  • Hostility toward beneficiaries: Refusing to communicate, withholding information, treating estate as personal property
  • Conflict of interest: Favoring certain beneficiaries, making distributions contrary to will terms

How to remove an executor in California: File a petition for removal (Form DE-200) under Probate Code 8502. The petition must state specific grounds and provide evidence of misconduct. The court will schedule a hearing where both sides present evidence. If removal is granted, the court appoints a successor executor or administrator. The removed executor may be ordered to reimburse the estate for losses caused by misconduct and may forfeit compensation.

Beneficiary Rights in California Probate

As a beneficiary of a California probate estate, you have important legal rights:

  • Right to notice: Formal notification of probate proceedings, hearings, and accountings
  • Right to information: Access to estate inventory, financial records, and accounting reports
  • Right to object: File objections to accountings, proposed distributions, or executor actions
  • Right to petition court: Request orders compelling executor to act, remove executor, or compel accounting
  • Right to contest will: Challenge will validity within 120 days on proper grounds
  • Right to timely distribution: Receive inheritance within reasonable time per statute
  • Right to legal representation: Hire attorney to protect interests (fees may be payable from estate)
  • Right to court supervision: Request court intervention if executor isn't properly administering estate

Costs of Probate Litigation

Probate litigation can be expensive. Will contests and executor removal proceedings typically cost:

  • Simple contests: $15,000-$50,000 in attorney fees and costs
  • Complex litigation: $50,000-$150,000+ for trials with expert witnesses
  • Trial preparation: Depositions, discovery, expert witnesses (forensic accountants, handwriting experts, medical experts)
  • Court costs: Filing fees, deposition transcripts, court reporter fees

Who pays for probate litigation? Generally, each party pays their own attorney fees unless the court orders otherwise. Under Probate Code 11003, the court may award attorney fees from the estate to beneficiaries who successfully challenge executor misconduct. If you lose a frivolous contest, you may be ordered to pay the estate's legal costs.

Many probate disputes settle through mediation before trial. Settlement negotiations can resolve conflicts while avoiding the time and expense of litigation. Experienced probate litigation attorneys often achieve favorable settlements that address beneficiaries' concerns without the uncertainty and cost of trial.

Probate Real Estate in California

Selling a House in Probate California

To sell real estate during probate:

  1. Obtain Letters Testamentary: You need court authority before listing property
  2. Get property appraised: Probate referee provides date-of-death value for inventory
  3. Hire real estate agent: List property at market value (appraisal + market conditions)

  5. File petition for sale: Form DE-260 requesting court approval (unless you have IAEA authority)
  6. Provide notice: Send 15-day notice to beneficiaries of proposed sale
  7. Court confirmation hearing: Judge reviews sale terms and approves if in estate's best interest

Transfer House After Death California

To transfer real property to beneficiaries without selling:

  1. Include property in petition for final distribution
  2. Obtain court order approving distribution to specific beneficiary
  3. Prepare Executor's Deed (transfer deed from estate to beneficiary)
  4. Record deed with Los Angeles County Recorder
  5. File Preliminary Change of Ownership Report with Assessor
  6. Beneficiary assumes responsibility for property taxes, insurance, maintenance

Property tax considerations: Under Proposition 19 (effective Feb 2021), transfers to children no longer receive automatic property tax reassessment exclusion except for primary residences (with value limits) and family farms.   • Wait 40 days after death
  • Complete Form REG 5

For Probate Estates:

  • Submit certified copy of Letters Testamentary

  • Executor signs as "Executor of Estate of [Name]"

Bank Accounts for Deceased Person California

Bank accounts require different procedures depending on ownership type:

  • Joint accounts with right of survivorship: Pass automatically to surviving account holder - no probate needed. Present death certificate to bank.
  • POD/TOD accounts: Pass to named beneficiary outside probate. Beneficiary presents death certificate and ID to claim funds.
  • Individual accounts (probate estate): Frozen at death. Executor presents Letters Testamentary to access and transfer to estate account.
  • Trust accounts: If properly funded, pass per trust terms without probate. Successor trustee manages distribution.

Important: Banks will freeze individual accounts immediately upon notification of death. Family members cannot access funds even with bills to pay until the executor provides Letters Testamentary. This is why proper estate planning with joint accounts or trusts is crucial.

Personal Property in Probate

Personal property (furniture, jewelry, collectibles, household items) requires:

  • Inventory: List all items with estimated values on Form DE-160
  • Appraisal: Probate referee appraises valuable items (jewelry, art, collectibles over $1,000)
  • Security: Store valuables safely, maintain insurance
  • Distribution: Distribute per will or intestacy after court approval
  • Sale: Sell items if necessary to pay debts (estate sales, auction houses)

Digital Assets in Probate

Under California's Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (Probate Code 870-884):

  • Email accounts: Request access from providers (Google, Yahoo, etc.) with Letters Testamentary
  • Social media: Request account deletion or memorialization per platform policies
  • Cryptocurrency: Access digital wallets, transfer to estate account, report on inventory
  • Online storage: Access iCloud, Dropbox, Google Drive to retrieve important documents
  • Digital photos/files: Download and preserve for beneficiaries
  • Online accounts: Close subscriptions, recurring charges, memberships

Business Interests in Probate

Business ownership requires special handling:

  • Sole proprietorship: Business assets included in probate estate, executor may continue operations temporarily
  • Partnership: Review partnership agreement for buy-sell provisions and succession terms
  • LLC/Corporation: Transfer membership interests or stock per operating agreement and will
  • Valuation: Hire business appraiser for date-of-death valuation
  • Ongoing operations: May need court approval to continue business during probate

Frequently Asked Questions About California Probate

Get answers to the most common questions about probate in California, from executor duties to court procedures.

What does an executor do in California?

An executor in California is responsible for managing the deceased person's estate through probate. Key duties include filing the will with the probate court, obtaining letters testamentary, inventorying and appraising estate assets, paying debts and taxes, managing estate property, distributing assets to beneficiaries, and providing a final accounting to the court. The executor must act in the best interests of the estate and beneficiaries, following California Probate Code requirements.

What are executor duties and responsibilities in California?

California executor responsibilities include: 1) Filing the petition for probate and will with the court, 2) Obtaining letters testamentary from the court, 3) Notifying all beneficiaries and heirs, 4) Publishing notice to creditors, 5) Inventorying and appraising all estate assets, 6) Managing estate property and investments, 7) Paying valid debts, expenses, and taxes, 8) Selling real estate if necessary, 9) Distributing assets to beneficiaries per the will, 10) Filing final accounting with the court, and 11) Closing the estate. Executors have a fiduciary duty to act in good faith and can be held personally liable for mistakes.

How do I get letters testamentary in California?

To obtain letters testamentary in California: 1) File a Petition for Probate (Form DE-111) with the superior court in the county where the deceased resided, 2) Attach the original will and death certificate, 3) Pay the filing fee, 4) Attend the probate hearing (typically 30-60 days after filing), 5) The court will issue Letters Testamentary (Form DE-150) if the petition is approved. Letters testamentary give the executor legal authority to act on behalf of the estate, access accounts, sell property, and manage estate affairs. You must take an oath and may need to post a bond unless waived by the will.

How long does an executor have to settle an estate in California?

In California, executors typically have 12-18 months to settle an estate, though complex estates may take longer. The minimum timeline is about 6 months due to the creditor claim period (4 months from notice publication). Key timeframes: Petition filing (within 30 days of death recommended), court hearing (30-60 days after filing), creditor claims period (4 months after notice published), inventory due (4 months after letters issued), and final distribution (after all debts paid and creditor period expired). Delays can occur due to will contests, estate tax issues, real estate sales, or beneficiary disputes. Executors should work diligently to avoid unnecessary delays that could result in removal.

Can an executor be removed in California?

Yes, an executor can be removed in California for several reasons: 1) Breach of fiduciary duty, 2) Mismanagement or waste of estate assets, 3) Fraud or embezzlement, 4) Failure to perform duties (not filing required documents, not distributing assets), 5) Conflicts of interest, 6) Becoming incapacitated or incompetent, 7) Being convicted of a crime. Any interested party (beneficiary, heir, creditor) can petition the court for removal under California Probate Code Section 8500-8502. The court will hold a hearing and may remove the executor if there is good cause. A successor executor or administrator will then be appointed to complete the probate process.

What if the executor is not distributing the estate in California?

If an executor is not distributing estate assets in California, beneficiaries have several options: 1) Request a status update in writing, 2) Review the probate file at the courthouse (public record), 3) Demand an accounting under Probate Code Section 10950, 4) File a petition to compel distribution, 5) File a petition to remove the executor for breach of duty, 6) Report executor misconduct to the court. Common reasons for delays include unresolved creditor claims, pending litigation, tax issues, or real estate sales. However, unreasonable delays or refusal to distribute without valid cause is grounds for removal. Executors must act in good faith and cannot unreasonably delay distributions.

How do I file probate in Los Angeles County?

To file probate in Los Angeles County: 1) Obtain certified death certificates, 2) Locate the original will if one exists, 3) Complete Petition for Probate (Form DE-111), 4) Complete other required forms (DE-140 if independent administration requested), 5) File documents at LA Superior Court Probate Division (111 N Hill St, Los Angeles or 600 E Broadway, Glendale), 6) Pay filing fee (approximately $465), 7) Court schedules hearing in 30-60 days, 8) Publish notice in local newspaper for 3 consecutive weeks, 9) Mail notice to all heirs and beneficiaries, 10) Attend hearing and receive letters testamentary/administration. What California probate forms do I need?

What is the independent administration of estates act (IAEA) in California?

The Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) in California allows executors to take many actions without prior court approval, making probate faster and less expensive. With IAEA authority (full or limited), executors can sell real estate, distribute assets, pay claims, and make other decisions without a court hearing. Full IAEA authority requires all beneficiaries to consent or be properly noticed. Limited IAEA excludes certain powers. IAEA authority is requested when filing the Petition for Probate (Form DE-140). This is the preferred method for most estates as it reduces delays, court appearances, and legal costs while maintaining accountability through required notices and accountings.

What happens if someone died without a will in California?

When someone dies without a will in California (intestate), their assets are distributed according to California's intestate succession laws under Probate Code Sections 6400-6455. The court appoints an administrator (not executor) to manage the estate. Distribution depends on surviving relatives: spouse inherits community property and may inherit all or part of separate property depending on other survivors; if no spouse, children inherit equally; if no children, parents inherit; then siblings, then nieces/nephews, then grandparents, and so on. Without any relatives, assets go to the state. The probate process is the same, but following intestacy laws instead of a will. An administrator must be appointed by filing a Petition for Letters of Administration.

Who inherits when there is no will in California?

California intestate succession determines who inherits without a will: 1) Spouse inherits all community property; for separate property, spouse gets 100% if no children, parents, or siblings, or splits with children if they exist; 2) Children (biological, adopted, sometimes step-children) share equally if no spouse; 3) Parents inherit if no spouse or children; 4) Siblings inherit if no spouse, children, or parents; 5) More distant relatives (nieces/nephews, grandparents, aunts/uncles, cousins) in order of closeness; 6) If no relatives, estate escheats to California. Half-relatives inherit half-shares. This applies only to probate assets - assets with beneficiary designations or in living trusts pass outside intestacy.

What is a small estate affidavit in California and when can I use it?

A small estate affidavit in California allows heirs to transfer estate assets without formal probate when the total estate value is $184,500 or less (as of 2024). There are two types: 1) Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property (Probate Code 13100) for assets like bank accounts, stocks, personal property - can be used 40 days after death, and 2) Affidavit re Real Property (Probate Code 13200) for real estate valued at $61,500 or less. To use it, complete form DE-305, wait the required time, present the affidavit to the holder of the asset (bank, DMV, etc.) with a death certificate. This avoids the time and expense of full probate, typically taking days instead of months.

How do I contest a will in California?

To contest a will in California: 1) You must have legal standing (named beneficiary in a prior will, heir under intestacy, or person who would benefit from contest), 2) File a Will Contest petition before the court admits the will to probate or within 120 days after the will is admitted, 3) State valid grounds: lack of testamentary capacity, undue influence, fraud, forgery, improper execution, or revocation, 4) The burden of proof is on the contestant to prove the claim, 5) Discovery and investigation of circumstances surrounding will creation, 6) Trial or settlement. Time limits are strict - you must act quickly. Will contests are complex; hire an experienced probate litigation attorney. If successful, the will may be invalidated in whole or part, and prior wills or intestacy laws apply.

What is undue influence in a California will contest?

Undue influence in a California will contest occurs when someone exerts excessive pressure or manipulation on the testator, overpowering their free will and causing them to make a will they wouldn't have made otherwise. California Probate Code Section 86 defines it as pressure that overcomes free will. Evidence includes: 1) Confidential relationship between influencer and testator, 2) Active participation in procuring the will, 3) Unnatural disposition (excluding natural heirs), 4) Testator's vulnerability (age, illness, mental weakness), 5) Isolation from family, 6) Sudden changes to estate plan favoring influencer. Common in cases involving caregivers, new romantic partners, or family members who isolated the deceased. If proven, provisions benefiting the influencer may be invalidated.

What constitutes executor misconduct in California?

Executor misconduct in California includes: 1) Stealing or embezzling estate assets, 2) Self-dealing (buying estate assets for personal benefit), 3) Commingling estate funds with personal accounts, 4) Failing to preserve or protect estate property, 5) Making risky investments, 6) Failing to file required tax returns, 7) Not notifying beneficiaries or creditors, 8) Unreasonable delays in administration, 9) Favoring some beneficiaries over others, 10) Refusing to provide accountings, 11) Unauthorized distributions. Executors owe fiduciary duties of loyalty, care, and impartiality. Misconduct can result in removal, surcharge (personal liability for losses), denial of executor fees, and criminal prosecution in severe cases. Beneficiaries can petition court to remove executor and recover damages.

What are beneficiary rights in California probate?

California beneficiary rights include: 1) Notice of probate proceedings, 2) Copy of the will, 3) Opportunity to object to executor appointment, 4) Request and receive accountings showing all estate transactions, 5) Object to executor actions or proposed distributions, 6) Petition to compel executor performance of duties, 7) Petition to remove executor for cause, 8) Contest the will if grounds exist, 9) Receive their inheritance per the will or intestacy, 10) Information about estate administration, 11) Timely distribution of assets. Beneficiaries can hire their own attorney to protect their interests. Executors must act in beneficiaries' best interests and keep them reasonably informed. Violations can result in executor removal and personal liability.

How do I transfer a house after death in California?

To transfer a house after death in California: 1) If in a living trust: trustee records Affidavit of Death of Trustee and new deed transferring to beneficiaries, 2) If joint tenancy: survivor records Affidavit of Death of Joint Tenant, 3) If community property with right of survivorship: surviving spouse records spousal property petition or affidavit, 4) If in deceased's name alone and estate under $61,500: Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value, 5) If valuable estate requiring probate: obtain court order for distribution after full probate, get letters testamentary, then record deed. Each method requires recording documents with the county recorder along with death certificate. Property taxes and reassessment considerations apply. Consult a probate attorney to determine the correct transfer method.

How do I sell a house in probate in California?

To sell probate real estate in California: 1) Executor/administrator must have letters and authority to sell (IAEA preferred), 2) Hire a real estate agent experienced in probate sales, 3) Get property appraisal or broker price opinion, 4) List property for sale at fair market value, 5) Accept offer and open escrow, 6) With full IAEA authority: give notice of proposed action (15 days) and close if no objections, 7) Without IAEA: file petition for court confirmation, notice hearing, attend confirmation hearing where court approves or takes overbids, 8) After court approval or IAEA period expires: complete sale and deposit net proceeds into estate account. Sale commissions and expenses are estate expenses. All proceeds go to the estate for distribution per will or intestacy.

How do I transfer a car title after death in California?

To transfer a vehicle title after death in California: 1) If vehicle value under $184,500 and no probate: Complete Affidavit for Transfer Without Probate (Form REG 5), attach certified death certificate, provide proof of heir/beneficiary status, submit to DMV with title and fees, 2) If probate required: Obtain letters testamentary/administration and court order for distribution, complete standard title transfer with DMV, 3) If joint owners with right of survivorship: Surviving owner completes title transfer with death certificate, 4) If payable-on-death registration: Beneficiary presents death certificate and identification. Wait 40 days from death to use affidavit method. DMV forms and current fees apply. Vehicle must be registered in California.

What happens to bank accounts when someone dies in California?

What happens to bank accounts after death in California depends on account type: 1) Payable-on-Death (POD) accounts: Pass directly to named beneficiaries outside probate upon presenting death certificate, 2) Joint accounts with right of survivorship: Pass to surviving account holder, 3) Trust accounts: Pass per trust terms to beneficiaries, 4) Individual accounts with no beneficiary: Frozen upon death, require probate or small estate affidavit to access, 5) If total individual accounts under $184,500: Heirs can use small estate affidavit 40 days after death, 6) Larger estates: Executor with letters testamentary can access and manage. Banks require death certificate and proper legal authority. Always inform banks promptly of death to prevent fraud and unauthorized access.

What is probate litigation in Los Angeles?

Probate litigation in Los Angeles involves court disputes over estate administration, including: will contests (challenging validity due to undue influence, lack of capacity, fraud, or improper execution), trust contests, removal of executors or trustees for misconduct, beneficiary disputes over asset distribution, creditor claims disputes, accounting disputes, elder financial abuse claims, conservatorship disputes, and interpretation of will or trust provisions. Los Angeles Superior Court Probate Division handles these cases. Probate litigation is complex, time-consuming, and expensive, often requiring expert witnesses, discovery, and trials. Common in high-value estates or family conflicts. Mediation is often required before trial. Hire experienced probate litigation attorney familiar with LA County procedures and judges.

What is a spousal property petition in California?

A Spousal Property Petition (Probate Code Section 13500) allows a surviving spouse in California to transfer assets to themselves without full probate when the deceased spouse left all assets to the surviving spouse or when assets are community property. Use Form DE-221. Benefits: faster than full probate (hearing in 30 days), less expensive, simplified process. Can be used for all types of assets including real estate, bank accounts, vehicles, and personal property. Requirements: 1) Petitioner must be surviving spouse, 2) At least 40 days since death, 3) Assets pass to spouse under will or by operation of law, 4) No one else has been appointed representative. Court issues order confirming property to surviving spouse, which is recorded to transfer title. Excellent alternative to lengthy probate for surviving spouses.

How long do I have to contest a will in California?

In California, the deadline to contest a will is strict: you must file your will contest before the will is admitted to probate OR within 120 days after the will is admitted to probate, whichever is later (Probate Code Section 8226). If you have legal standing and grounds to contest, it's critical to act quickly. The 120-day period begins when the court admits the will and appoints the executor. After this deadline, you generally lose the right to contest except in rare circumstances (like later-discovered fraud or forgery). If you believe a will is invalid, consult a probate litigation attorney immediately. Delays can be fatal to your case. The court may allow contests after the deadline only in exceptional situations.

How much does probate cost in California?

California probate costs are set by statute based on estate value. Attorney and executor fees are each: 4% of the first $100,000, 3% of the next $100,000, 2% of the next $800,000, 1% of the next $9 million, and 0.5% above $10 million. For a $500,000 estate, statutory fees are $13,000 each for attorney and executor ($26,000 total). Additional costs include court filing fees ($465), publication costs ($300-$500), bond premiums (if required), appraisal fees, and accounting fees. Total probate costs typically range from 4-7% of the gross estate value. Extraordinary services (litigation, tax disputes, business sales) add extra fees. This is why living trusts that avoid probate save significant money - a trust costs $1,000-$2,000 versus $26,000+ in probate fees.

How long does probate take in Los Angeles County?

Probate in Los Angeles County typically takes 9-18 months for straightforward cases, though complex estates can take 2+ years. The timeline includes: petition filing and hearing (30-60 days), creditor claim period (minimum 4 months from notice publication), inventory and appraisal (4 months from letters issued), claims resolution and asset liquidation (varies), final accounting and distribution (after creditor period ends), and closing (1-2 months after final distribution). Delays occur due to will contests, creditor disputes, tax issues, real estate sales, beneficiary disagreements, or executor delays. LA County court calendars can add time. Independent Administration (IAEA) speeds the process by reducing required court hearings. Complex estates with business interests, tax disputes, or litigation take substantially longer.

Do all estates go through probate in California?

No, not all estates require probate in California. Probate is NOT needed for: 1) Assets in a revocable living trust (most common probate avoidance method), 2) Jointly owned property with right of survivorship, 3) Community property with right of survivorship, 4) Payable-on-death (POD) or transfer-on-death (TOD) accounts, 5) Life insurance and retirement accounts with named beneficiaries, 6) Small estates under $184,500 (small estate affidavit instead), 7) Assets passing via spousal property petition. However, probate IS required for assets in the deceased's name alone without these arrangements. Most California homeowners need probate unless they created a living trust, as median home values exceed the $184,500 small estate threshold. Proper estate planning with trusts and beneficiary designations avoids probate entirely.

What is the difference between probate and trust administration in California?

Probate is court-supervised estate administration for assets in deceased's name alone; trust administration is non-court successor trustee management of trust assets. Key differences: Court involvement (probate requires extensive court filings and hearings; trust administration is private with no court), Timeline (probate takes 9-18+ months; trust administration takes weeks to months), Cost (probate costs 4-7% of estate; trust administration costs 1-3%), Privacy (probate is public record; trusts remain private), Complexity (probate has strict procedures and deadlines; trusts are more flexible), and Control (probate follows court rules; trust follows trust terms). Trust administration still requires notices to beneficiaries, asset valuations, debt payments, and accountings, but without court supervision. This is why living trusts are preferred - they provide control, privacy, and efficiency while avoiding probate costs and delays.

Can I avoid probate without a living trust in California?

Yes, several methods avoid probate without a living trust in California: 1) Joint tenancy with right of survivorship - property automatically passes to surviving co-owner, 2) Community property with right of survivorship (married couples only), 3) Transfer-on-death (TOD) deeds for real estate, 4) Payable-on-death (POD) designations on bank accounts, 5) Named beneficiaries on life insurance, retirement accounts (401k, IRA), and investment accounts, 6) Small estate affidavit for estates under $184,500, 7) Spousal property petition for surviving spouses. However, each method has limitations and potential problems. Living trusts provide comprehensive probate avoidance, incapacity planning, control over distributions, and flexibility that other methods don't. For most California homeowners, a living trust is still the most effective probate avoidance strategy.

What happens if executor steals from estate in California?

If an executor steals from a California estate (embezzlement or conversion), serious consequences follow: 1) Removal from executor position, 2) Surcharge - personal liability to repay stolen amounts plus interest, 3) Denial of executor compensation, 4) Criminal prosecution for theft, embezzlement, or elder financial abuse, 5) Civil lawsuit for damages, 6) Beneficiaries can file petition to remove and surcharge executor. Evidence needed includes: missing assets, unauthorized withdrawals, transfers to executor or family, commingled funds, or failure to account. Beneficiaries should act quickly: review estate accounts, demand accounting, file removal petition, report to District Attorney if criminal, and freeze accounts if possible. Courts take executor theft seriously and will impose harsh penalties. Victims may recover attorneys' fees. Executors who steal face both civil and criminal liability.

How is executor compensation calculated in California?

California executor compensation is statutory based on estate value (Probate Code Section 10800): 4% of the first $100,000, 3% of the next $100,000, 2% of the next $800,000, 1% of the next $9 million, 0.5% of the next $15 million, and reasonable amount above $25 million. For a $500,000 estate, compensation is $13,000. For a $1 million estate, it's $23,000. This is for ordinary services. Executors can request additional compensation for extraordinary services like managing a business, handling litigation, tax disputes, or especially time-consuming estates - court must approve extraordinary fees. Executors can waive compensation (common for family members to reduce estate taxes). Compensation is taxable income to executor. Multiple executors divide the total fee. Compensation is separate from reimbursement of actual expenses.

Can family members be paid for helping with probate in California?

Yes, family members can be paid for probate work in California under certain conditions: 1) If appointed executor/administrator - entitled to statutory compensation, 2) For extraordinary services beyond normal executor duties (business management, extensive property maintenance, litigation assistance) - must petition court for approval and prove work was necessary and compensation reasonable, 3) For professional services at their normal rates if they're licensed professionals (accountant, real estate agent, appraiser) - must be approved by court and arms-length, 4) Reimbursement for actual out-of-pocket expenses with receipts. However, beneficiaries doing normal assistance (cleaning house, sorting papers, attending hearings) are typically not compensated separately - that's considered part of beneficiary responsibility. Any family member compensation beyond statutory executor fees requires court approval and must be reasonable and necessary. Courts scrutinize family payments to prevent estate depletion.

What are probate referee fees in California?

California probate referees are court-appointed appraisers who value estate assets. Fees are set by statute at 0.1% (one-tenth of one percent) of the appraised value of assets they appraise, with a $75 minimum per estate (Probate Code Section 8960). For a $500,000 estate, the referee fee is $500. Referees appraise real estate, business interests, personal property, collectibles, and other assets requiring professional valuation. They don't appraise assets with readily ascertainable values like bank accounts, stocks, bonds, or vehicles. The executor selects the referee from the court's approved panel. Referee fees are estate expenses paid from estate funds. The referee provides a formal Inventory and Appraisal that must be filed with the court within 4 months of receiving letters. Accurate appraisals are critical for estate tax purposes and proper distribution.

How do creditors make claims in California probate?

California probate creditor claims process: 1) Executor publishes Notice to Creditors in local newspaper for 3 consecutive weeks, 2) Executor mails notice to known creditors, 3) Creditors have 4 months from first publication OR 60 days from mailing of notice, whichever is later, to file claims, 4) Creditors file Creditor's Claim form (DE-172) with probate court and serve copy on executor, 5) Executor has 30 days to allow or reject claim, 6) If rejected, creditor must sue within 90 days or claim is barred, 7) If allowed, executor pays from estate assets in priority order: administration costs, funeral expenses, secured debts, taxes, medical expenses, then general unsecured debts. Late claims filed after deadline are generally barred. Executors should carefully review all claims for validity before paying. Disputed claims may require court hearing.

What is a Notice of Proposed Action in California probate?

A Notice of Proposed Action (NOPA) under California Probate Code Section 16502 is used by executors with Independent Administration authority to notify beneficiaries of significant actions before taking them. Common uses include: selling real estate, distributing assets, borrowing money, continuing or selling a business, or making other important estate decisions. The executor sends Form DE-165 to all beneficiaries and heirs describing the proposed action. Recipients have 15 days to object. If no objections, executor can proceed. If someone objects, executor must either abandon the action or petition court for approval. NOPA streamlines probate by allowing many actions without court hearing, saving time and legal fees. However, executor must wait the full 15-day period before acting. Some actions still require court approval even with IAEA authority.

Can an executor live in the deceased's house during probate in California?

An executor can live in the deceased's house during California probate only under specific circumstances: 1) If executor is also a beneficiary who inherited the house and other beneficiaries consent, 2) If the will specifically allows it, 3) If executor pays fair market rent to the estate, 4) If living there is necessary to maintain/protect the property. However, executors cannot: move into the house rent-free for personal benefit (self-dealing), exclude beneficiaries who have equal rights, prevent sale if sale is needed to pay debts, or damage beneficiary interests. Rent-free occupancy without authorization is breach of fiduciary duty and grounds for removal and surcharge. If executor needs to live there, they should: pay rent at fair market rate to estate, get beneficiary consent in writing, or petition court for approval. Courts scrutinize executors who benefit personally from estate assets.

What is the family allowance in California probate?

California family allowance (Probate Code Sections 6540-6545) provides money from the estate to support the deceased's surviving spouse and minor children during probate. The court determines a reasonable allowance based on: family's previous standard of living, estate size and liquidity, and family needs. The allowance is payable from estate assets and has priority over most other claims. It can continue for up to one year from death (longer if needed). Common amounts range from $2,000-$10,000+ per month depending on circumstances. The surviving spouse or guardian petitions the court for allowance on Form DE-172. No allowance is needed if surviving spouse has adequate separate income/assets or if estate is community property passing to spouse. Family allowance is in addition to spouse's inheritance rights. Purpose is to prevent hardship while probate proceeds.

Can I be forced to be an executor in California?

No, you cannot be forced to serve as executor in California. Being named executor in a will does not obligate you to serve. If you don't want the position, you can: 1) Decline nomination by not petitioning for probate and filing a declination, 2) Renounce the position in writing filed with the court, 3) Resign after appointment if circumstances change. Valid reasons to decline include: too time-consuming, too complex, conflicts with beneficiaries, health issues, lack of knowledge, distance, or uncomfortable managing estates. If primary executor declines, successor executors named in the will are next in line. If no one is willing or available, interested parties can petition for court-appointed administrator. There is no penalty for declining executor position. It's better to decline than serve poorly or neglect duties, which could result in personal liability.

How long does executor have to notify beneficiaries in California?

California executors must notify beneficiaries within specific timeframes: 1) Notice of Petition to Administer Estate must be mailed to heirs and beneficiaries at least 15 days before the probate hearing, 2) After letters are issued, executor must mail Duties and Liabilities of Personal Representative (Form DE-147) to all beneficiaries and heirs within 90 days, 3) Notice to Creditors must be published and mailed within 4 months of letter issuance, 4) Inventory and Appraisal must be mailed to beneficiaries within 4 months of letters. Failure to properly notice beneficiaries can result in delayed proceedings, removal, or personal liability. Beneficiaries have right to notice of all significant actions and court hearings. Executor should maintain proof of mailing (certified mail return receipts) for all notices. Late or missed notices extend statutory deadlines and can restart claim periods.

What happens to debt when someone dies in California?

When someone dies in California, their debts don't disappear - they become estate obligations: 1) Secured debts (mortgages, car loans) - must be paid or creditor can foreclose/repossess, lien stays with property, 2) Unsecured debts (credit cards, personal loans) - creditors must file claims in probate within 4 months or be barred, 3) Priority debts (taxes, medical expenses) - paid before general creditors, 4) Joint debts - surviving co-borrowers remain liable, 5) Authorized user/community property state rules - spouse may be liable for some debts. Debts are paid from estate assets in priority order before distribution to beneficiaries. If estate is insolvent (debts exceed assets), creditors receive pro-rata shares and beneficiaries get nothing. Family members are NOT personally liable for deceased's debts unless they co-signed or it's community debt. Executor should never pay creditors with personal funds.
