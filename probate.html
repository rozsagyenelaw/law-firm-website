Executor & Administrator Services in California

Comprehensive guidance for executors and administrators navigating probate responsibilities in California

What Does an Executor Do in California?

An executor (also called a personal representative) is the person named in a will to manage the deceased person's estate through probate. If there is no will, the court appoints an administrator who performs the same duties. In California, serving as an executor is a serious legal responsibility that requires careful attention to detail, fiduciary duty, and compliance with California Probate Code sections 8400-8573.

The executor's primary role is to protect estate assets, pay valid debts and taxes, and distribute remaining assets to beneficiaries according to the will or California law. This process typically takes 9-18 months and involves court supervision to ensure proper administration.

Complete Executor Responsibilities Checklist

  1. Locate and secure the will - File the original will with the Los Angeles County Superior Court within 30 days of death (Probate Code Â§ 8200)
  2. File petition for probate - Submit Form DE-111 and pay filing fees (currently $435-$465 depending on estate value)
  3. Obtain Letters Testamentary - Receive official court authorization to act as executor (Form DE-150)
  4. Notify all beneficiaries and heirs - Send formal notice within 60 days using Form DE-154
  5. Publish notice to creditors - Run newspaper notice for three consecutive weeks in Los Angeles County
  6. Send direct notice to known creditors - Mail Form DE-157 within four months of appointment
  7. Inventory all estate assets - Complete Form DE-160 within four months, including all real estate, bank accounts, investments, vehicles, and personal property
  8. Obtain professional appraisals - Hire court-appointed probate referee for non-cash assets
  9. Open estate bank account - Establish separate account using your EIN for estate finances
  10. Pay valid debts and taxes - Review claims, pay legitimate creditors, file final tax returns (1040 and 1041)
  11. Manage estate property - Maintain insurance, pay mortgages, collect rents, secure vacant homes
  12. Sell assets if necessary - Obtain court approval for real estate sales using Form DE-260
  13. File accounting with court - Submit detailed financial report (Form DE-165) showing all receipts and disbursements
  14. Request distribution approval - File petition for final distribution (Form DE-295)
  15. Distribute assets to beneficiaries - Transfer property after court approval and obtain receipts
  16. Close estate - File final report and request discharge as executor

Letters Testamentary Explained

Letters Testamentary (Form DE-150) are official court documents that prove your legal authority to act as executor. You'll need certified copies of these letters to access bank accounts, sell property, collect assets, and conduct estate business. In Los Angeles County, you typically receive Letters Testamentary 6-8 weeks after filing your petition, assuming no contests or complications arise.

If the will requests Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, your Letters Testamentary will include this designation, allowing you to take many actions without prior court approval. Banks, title companies, and financial institutions will require certified copies (with the court seal) dated within 60 days.

Executor Compensation in California

California law provides statutory compensation for executors based on the gross value of the estate (Probate Code Â§ 10810). Executor fees are calculated as follows:

  • 4% of the first $100,000
  • 3% of the next $100,000
  • 2% of the next $800,000
  • 1% of the next $9,000,000
  • 0.5% of the next $15,000,000
  • Reasonable amount for estates over $25,000,000

For example, an executor of a $1,000,000 estate would receive $23,000 in statutory fees. You may also request additional "extraordinary" compensation for unusual services like managing a business, handling litigation, or selling complex assets. Compensation is subject to income tax and must be approved by the court before payment.

Can an Executor Be Removed in California?

Yes. Under Probate Code Â§ 8502, the court can remove an executor for several reasons, including:

  • Wasting, embezzling, or mismanaging estate assets
  • Failing to perform duties or file required accountings
  • Gross negligence or incompetence
  • Self-dealing or conflicts of interest
  • Refusing to follow court orders
  • Insolvency, conviction of a felony, or becoming incapacitated
  • Acting in bad faith or with hostility toward beneficiaries

Beneficiaries can petition the court to remove an executor by filing Form DE-200. The court will hold a hearing to determine if removal is warranted. If the executor is removed, the court will appoint a successor administrator. Removed executors may also face personal liability for losses caused by their misconduct and could forfeit their right to compensation.

When You Need an Attorney's Help

While California law doesn't require executors to hire an attorney, most probate courts and legal professionals strongly recommend it. You should definitely consult a probate attorney if:

  • The estate is worth more than $500,000
  • The estate includes real property or complex assets
  • There are disputes among beneficiaries
  • Someone is contesting the will
  • You're unfamiliar with probate procedures and court requirements
  • The deceased owned a business or had significant debts
  • There are tax implications (estate tax, capital gains, etc.)
  • You're concerned about personal liability

Attorney fees in probate are also statutory and calculated on the same schedule as executor fees. Legal representation protects you from personal liability, ensures compliance with all legal requirements, and typically speeds up the probate process significantly. In Los Angeles County, most probate cases with attorney representation close 4-6 months faster than those without.

California Probate Process: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

Navigate the entire probate timeline from petition to distribution with this comprehensive procedural guide

California's probate process follows a structured court-supervised procedure designed to ensure proper estate administration. Understanding each step, required forms, and timeline expectations helps executors and beneficiaries navigate the process with confidence. Here's exactly what happens from start to finish in Los Angeles County Superior Court.

The Complete Probate Timeline

  1. File Petition for Probate (Month 1) - The executor or proposed administrator files Form DE-111 (Petition for Probate) with the Los Angeles County Superior Court. Filing fees range from $435-$465 depending on estate value. You must file the original will and death certificate. Processing time: 2-3 weeks for initial review.
  2. Notice of Petition to Administer Estate (Months 1-2) - After filing, you must provide notice to all heirs and beneficiaries. This includes publishing Form DE-121 in a newspaper of general circulation for three consecutive weeks and mailing notice to all interested parties at least 15 days before the hearing. Los Angeles requires publication in the Los Angeles Daily Journal or Metropolitan News.
  3. Probate Hearing (Month 2-3) - The court schedules a hearing typically 6-8 weeks after filing. If there are no objections or contests, the judge approves the petition and issues Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (Form DE-150). If you request Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) authority, it's granted at this hearing.
  4. Obtain Letters and Bond (Month 3) - After the hearing, obtain certified copies of your Letters Testamentary from the court clerk. If the will doesn't waive bond, you must purchase a surety bond equal to the estate's value. Use these letters to access bank accounts, collect assets, and conduct estate business.
  5. Notice to Creditors (Months 3-4) - Within four months of appointment, send Form DE-157 (Notice of Administration to Creditors) to all known creditors. Also publish a creditor notice in the newspaper for three consecutive weeks. Creditors have the later of four months from appointment or 60 days from notice to file claims.
  6. Inventory and Appraisal (Month 4) - Complete Form DE-160 (Inventory and Appraisal) listing all estate assets with their date-of-death values. A court-appointed probate referee appraises non-cash assets. File the completed inventory with the court within four months of appointment. This establishes the estate's gross value for fee calculation.
  7. Pay Debts and Claims (Months 5-8) - Review all creditor claims filed with the court. Pay valid debts, reject improper claims, and negotiate disputed amounts. Handle ongoing expenses like mortgage payments, property taxes, insurance, and maintenance. Open an estate bank account using the estate's EIN for all financial transactions.
  8. Tax Returns and Clearances (Months 6-10) - File the decedent's final Form 1040 individual tax return. If the estate generates income, file Form 1041 estate income tax returns. If the estate exceeds federal exemption thresholds, file Form 706 estate tax return (due 9 months after death). Obtain tax clearances from IRS and California Franchise Tax Board before final distribution.
  9. Sell Assets if Necessary (Months 6-12) - If estate assets must be sold to pay debts or for distribution, obtain court approval. Real estate sales require filing Form DE-260 and court confirmation hearing unless you have IAEA authority. Handle title transfers, deeds, and distribution of sale proceeds.
  10. File First Accounting (Month 9-12) - Prepare Form DE-165 (First and Final Report and Account) detailing all estate receipts and disbursements. This accounting shows opening inventory, income received, expenses paid, distributions made, and remaining assets. Accuracy is critical as beneficiaries can object to unauthorized expenditures.
  11. Petition for Final Distribution (Month 12-15) - File Form DE-295 (Petition for Final Distribution) requesting court approval to distribute remaining assets to beneficiaries. Include a proposed distribution plan showing exactly what each beneficiary receives. The court schedules a hearing 4-6 weeks after filing.
  12. Final Hearing and Distribution Order (Month 13-16) - Attend the final distribution hearing. If the accounting and distribution plan are approved, the judge issues an Order for Final Distribution. Wait for the order to become final (usually 30 days) before making distributions. Transfer assets according to the court order.
  13. Distribute Assets and Obtain Receipts (Month 14-18) - Transfer property to beneficiaries according to the court order. Obtain signed receipts (Form DE-305) from each beneficiary acknowledging receipt. File receipts with the court showing distribution is complete.
  14. Close Estate and Discharge (Month 15-18) - File Form DE-295 requesting discharge as executor. Submit all beneficiary receipts proving complete distribution. The court issues an order discharging you from duties and closing the estate. Your executor responsibilities are complete.

Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA)

The Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) is a critical tool that allows executors to take many actions without prior court approval. Under Probate Code Â§Â§ 10400-10592, IAEA authority significantly streamlines probate by eliminating the need for court petitions and hearings for routine transactions.

With IAEA authority, you can:

  • Sell real estate without court confirmation (subject to 15-day notice to beneficiaries)
  • Sell personal property without approval
  • Invest estate funds
  • Borrow money secured by estate property
  • Lease property for more than one year
  • Make repairs and improvements
  • Compromise claims and litigation

IAEA is typically requested in the original petition for probate and granted at the first hearing unless an interested party objects. Using IAEA can reduce probate duration by 3-6 months and save thousands in attorney fees and court costs. However, beneficiaries can file objections to proposed actions by submitting a written objection within 15 days of notice.

Timeline Expectations: How Long Does Probate Take?

In Los Angeles County, probate typically takes 9-18 months from filing to closing. Simple estates with IAEA authority, no disputes, and straightforward assets can close in 9-12 months. Complex estates with real estate sales, tax issues, creditor disputes, or will contests may take 18-24 months or longer.

Factors affecting probate duration: Estate size and complexity, whether you have IAEA authority, real estate sales requirements, creditor claims volume, tax return complexity, beneficiary cooperation, court calendar availability, and whether an attorney is representing the estate. Hiring experienced probate counsel typically accelerates the process by avoiding procedural errors and court delays.

Los Angeles County Specific Procedures

Los Angeles County has specific local rules and procedures that differ from other California counties:

  • Filing location: All probate cases are filed at Stanley Mosk Courthouse, 111 N. Hill Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012
  • Publication requirements: Must use Los Angeles Daily Journal or Metropolitan News for all legal notices
  • Court calendar: Initial hearings typically scheduled 6-8 weeks after filing; final hearings 4-6 weeks after petition
  • Local rules: LA County has specific formatting requirements for accountings and distribution petitions
  • Referee panel: LA County maintains a panel of probate referees for asset appraisals
  • Court fees: $435-$465 filing fees, $25 per certified copy of letters, additional fees for various petitions

Died Without a Will in California: Intestate Succession Laws

Understanding who inherits when there is no will under California's intestate succession statutes

When someone dies without a valid will in California, their estate passes according to intestate succession laws found in Probate Code Â§Â§ 6400-6455. These statutes establish a strict hierarchy determining who inherits based on family relationships. Understanding intestacy rules is essential for families navigating probate without a will.

Who Inherits When There Is No Will in California?

California intestate succession follows this priority order:

1. Surviving Spouse or Domestic Partner

The spouse's share depends on what other relatives survive:

  • 100% of community property always goes to the surviving spouse
  • 100% of separate property if no children, parents, siblings, or their descendants survive
  • 1/2 of separate property if one child or descendants of one deceased child survive
  • 1/3 of separate property if more than one child survives, or one child plus descendants of deceased children
  • 1/2 of separate property if no children but parents or siblings survive

2. Children and Descendants

If no spouse survives, the entire estate goes to children equally. If a child predeceased the decedent, that child's share goes to their children (the decedent's grandchildren) by right of representation. California recognizes:

  • Biological children (legitimate and illegitimate)
  • Legally adopted children (same rights as biological)
  • Stepchildren and foster children (generally no inheritance rights unless legally adopted)
  • Children conceived before death but born afterward

3. Parents

If no spouse, children, or descendants survive, the estate goes to the decedent's parents equally. If one parent is deceased, the surviving parent inherits the entire estate.

4. Siblings and Their Descendants

If no parents survive, the estate goes to siblings equally. If a sibling is deceased, their share goes to their children (the decedent's nieces and nephews). Half-siblings inherit the same as full siblings.

5. More Distant Relatives

If none of the above survive, the estate continues to more distant relatives in this order: grandparents, aunts/uncles, cousins, and other next of kin. If absolutely no heirs can be found, the estate escheats to the State of California.

Administrator vs. Executor: What's the Difference?

When someone dies with a will, the person named to handle the estate is called an executor. When someone dies without a will, the court appoints an administrator to perform the same duties. Both roles have identical responsibilities and powers under California law.

Priority for appointment as administrator (Probate Code Â§ 8461):

  1. Surviving spouse or domestic partner
  2. Children
  3. Grandchildren
  4. Other issue (descendants)
  5. Parents
  6. Siblings
  7. Issue of siblings (nieces and nephews)
  8. Other next of kin
  9. Public administrator (if no family member petitions)
  10. Creditors
  11. Any other person (with court approval)

When Does Intestacy Apply?

Intestate succession applies when:

  • The decedent never created a will
  • The will is invalid (improper execution, lack of capacity, undue influence, fraud)
  • The will was revoked and not replaced
  • All beneficiaries named in the will predeceased the testator
  • The will only disposes of part of the estate (partial intestacy for remaining assets)
  • Assets weren't titled properly in a trust or didn't have beneficiary designations

Problems with Dying Intestate

Dying without a will creates several significant problems:

  • No control over distribution: State law decides who gets what, which may not match your wishes
  • Unmarried partners receive nothing: Boyfriends, girlfriends, and partners without legal marriage/domestic partnership have zero inheritance rights
  • No guardian nomination: Court decides who raises minor children without your input
  • Estranged family may inherit: Relatives you're not close to may receive assets while close friends receive nothing
  • Extended probate: Intestate probate typically takes longer and costs more
  • Bond requirement: Administrator must usually purchase costly surety bond
  • Family disputes: Intestacy often creates conflict among relatives over administration and distribution
  • No executor choice: Court appoints administrator based on priority list, not your preference
  • Tax inefficiency: Miss opportunities for tax planning and estate minimization strategies
  • Charitable giving impossible: State law doesn't allow inheritance to charities or non-relatives

Creating a will or living trust ensures your wishes are honored and dramatically simplifies the estate administration process for your family. Even a simple will is far better than no estate plan at all.

Small Estate Procedures: Avoid Full Probate in California

Fast-track estate administration for estates under $184,500 using simplified California procedures

California offers several simplified procedures to avoid formal probate for small estates. These alternatives save substantial time and money, typically resolving estates in 30-90 days instead of 9-18 months. If you qualify, small estate procedures can eliminate court supervision entirely and reduce administration costs by thousands of dollars.

California Small Estate Limit 2024

$184,500

Maximum estate value for small estate procedures in 2024

Under Probate Code Â§ 13100, estates valued at $184,500 or less (excluding certain property like vehicles, salary/wages, and joint tenancy assets) qualify for simplified small estate procedures. This threshold adjusts every three years for inflation. The $184,500 limit applies to the gross value of property subject to probate, not the net value after debts.

Affidavit for Collection of Personal Property (Form DE-5200)

The Small Estate Affidavit (Probate Code Â§Â§ 13100-13116) is the simplest method to collect estate assets without formal probate. This procedure works for personal property only (not real estate) and requires no court filing or hearing.

How the Small Estate Affidavit Works:

  1. Wait 40 days: You must wait at least 40 days after the person's death before using this procedure
  2. Complete Form DE-5200: Fill out the Affidavit re Real Property of Small Value, providing details about the decedent, estate value, and your right to inherit
  3. Attach certified death certificate: Obtain official death certificate from LA County Registrar-Recorder
  4. Present to asset holders: Give the affidavit to banks, brokerage firms, DMV, or other entities holding estate assets
  5. Collect assets: Asset holders must release property to you after reviewing the affidavit - no court order needed
  6. Pay debts and distribute: You're responsible for paying debts and distributing to proper beneficiaries

What qualifies for the affidavit procedure:

  • Bank accounts and CDs
  • Stocks, bonds, and brokerage accounts
  • Personal belongings and household items
  • Vehicles (use separate DMV form)
  • Wages and salary (up to $16,625 - use Form DE-5207)
  • Business interests
  • Safe deposit box contents

Important: The person signing the affidavit must be entitled to the property under the will or intestate succession. You must also state that no probate proceeding is pending, the estate value is under the threshold, and 40 days have passed since death. False statements can result in perjury charges.

Spousal Property Petition California (Form DE-221)

The Spousal/Domestic Partner Property Petition (Probate Code Â§Â§ 13650-13660) allows surviving spouses to transfer community property and certain separate property without full probate. This powerful tool works even for estates exceeding the small estate limit.

What Property Qualifies:

  • All community property - no value limit
  • All quasi-community property (property acquired while living in another state that would have been community property in California)
  • Separate property passing to spouse under will or intestacy
  • Property held in joint tenancy with the spouse

Spousal Property Petition Procedure:

  1. Complete Form DE-221 (Spousal or Domestic Partner Property Petition)
  2. Complete Form DE-111 (Petition for Probate) - spousal version
  3. File with Los Angeles County Superior Court ($435 filing fee)
  4. Notice to Director of Health Care Services if Medi-Cal benefits received
  5. Court hearing scheduled 4-6 weeks after filing
  6. Judge issues order confirming property to surviving spouse
  7. Record certified copy with LA County Recorder for real property

Timeline: Spousal Property Petitions typically complete in 30-60 days, dramatically faster than full probate. The simplified procedure saves tens of thousands in statutory fees because executor and attorney fees are calculated only on separate property passing to others (if any), not on community property confirmed to the spouse.

Set Aside Estate Petition

Under Probate Code Â§ 6609, if the deceased's estate consists only of property passing to a surviving spouse and has a gross value under $184,500, the court can "set aside" the entire estate to the spouse through a simplified petition. This procedure combines features of the Spousal Property Petition with small estate benefits.

The Set Aside procedure is particularly useful when the estate slightly exceeds the small estate limit but all assets pass to the surviving spouse anyway. No executor is appointed, no Letters Testamentary are issued, and no ongoing estate administration is required - the court simply orders all property transferred directly to the spouse.

Do You Qualify for Small Estate Procedures?

You likely qualify if:

  • Total estate value is under $184,500 (excluding vehicles, salary, joint tenancy)
  • No real estate or only homestead property under $184,500
  • Assets are mostly personal property (bank accounts, investments, personal items)
  • At least 40 days have passed since death
  • No formal probate proceeding is pending or has been conducted
  • You are entitled to the property under the will or intestate succession

Probate Disputes & Litigation in California

Understanding will contests, executor removal, and beneficiary rights in California probate court

Probate disputes can arise when family members question the validity of a will, challenge executor decisions, or dispute asset distribution. California law provides specific grounds for contesting wills and removing executors, along with strict deadlines and procedures. Understanding your rights and the litigation process is essential if you're involved in a probate dispute.

Valid Grounds for Contesting a Will in California

Under Probate Code Â§Â§ 8250-8272, you can contest a will only on specific legal grounds. Personal disappointment with your inheritance or family disagreements aren't sufficient. Valid grounds include:

1. Lack of Testamentary Capacity

The testator must have been of "sound mind" when signing the will (Probate Code Â§ 6100.5). To have capacity, the testator must understand: (1) the nature of making a will, (2) the extent of their property, and (3) their relationship to beneficiaries. Medical records, doctor testimony, and witness observations can establish lack of capacity due to dementia, Alzheimer's, mental illness, or medication effects.

2. Undue Influence

Someone used excessive persuasion, coercion, or manipulation to override the testator's free will. Undue influence often involves a confidential relationship (caregiver, attorney-in-fact, advisor) who benefited from the will while isolating the testator from family. California has a presumption of undue influence when someone in a fiduciary relationship unduly benefits (Probate Code Â§ 21380). Evidence includes: sudden will changes, isolation from family, unequal distribution, and the influencer controlling access to the testator.

3. Fraud or Forgery

Someone deceived the testator about material facts that induced them to sign the will, or the testator's signature was forged. Fraud includes lying about family members' conduct, forging the testator's signature, or substituting pages in the will document. Handwriting experts and document examiners can provide forensic evidence in forgery cases.

4. Improper Execution

The will doesn't meet California's formal execution requirements. Under Probate Code Â§ 6110, a valid will must be: (1) in writing, (2) signed by the testator (or by someone at testator's direction), and (3) witnessed by at least two competent witnesses who were present together and understood they were witnessing a will. Holographic wills (handwritten, no witnesses) have different requirements under Â§ 6111.

5. Revocation

The will was properly revoked by the testator before death. Revocation occurs by: (1) physically destroying the will with intent to revoke, (2) executing a later will that revokes prior wills, or (3) written declaration of revocation (Probate Code Â§ 6120). A presumption of revocation exists when a will last in the testator's possession cannot be found after death.

Critical Deadline: 120 Days to Contest a Will

IMPORTANT: You have only 120 days after the will is admitted to probate to file a will contest in California (Probate Code Â§ 8226). This deadline is strictly enforced.

The 120-day period begins when the court admits the will to probate and issues Letters Testamentary. If you miss this deadline, you generally cannot contest the will even if you have valid grounds. If you weren't properly notified of the probate proceeding, different deadlines may apply under Â§ 8227, but you must act quickly once you learn of the probate.

Executor Misconduct and Removal

Executors owe fiduciary duties to beneficiaries and must act with utmost good faith, loyalty, and honesty. Common types of executor misconduct include:

  • Self-dealing: Using estate assets for personal benefit, borrowing from estate, selling assets to themselves
  • Embezzlement: Taking estate funds, hiding assets, transferring property to themselves or relatives
  • Negligent management: Failing to maintain property, allowing insurance to lapse, letting assets deteriorate
  • Improper investments: Making risky investments, commingling estate funds with personal accounts
  • Failure to account: Not providing required financial reports, hiding transactions from beneficiaries
  • Delays and inaction: Unreasonably prolonging administration, failing to file required documents
  • Hostility toward beneficiaries: Refusing to communicate, withholding information, treating estate as personal property
  • Conflict of interest: Favoring certain beneficiaries, making distributions contrary to will terms

How to remove an executor in California: File a petition for removal (Form DE-200) under Probate Code Â§ 8502. The petition must state specific grounds and provide evidence of misconduct. The court will schedule a hearing where both sides present evidence. If removal is granted, the court appoints a successor executor or administrator. The removed executor may be ordered to reimburse the estate for losses caused by misconduct and may forfeit compensation.

Beneficiary Rights in California Probate

As a beneficiary of a California probate estate, you have important legal rights:

  • Right to notice: Formal notification of probate proceedings, hearings, and accountings
  • Right to information: Access to estate inventory, financial records, and accounting reports
  • Right to object: File objections to accountings, proposed distributions, or executor actions
  • Right to petition court: Request orders compelling executor to act, remove executor, or compel accounting
  • Right to contest will: Challenge will validity within 120 days on proper grounds
  • Right to timely distribution: Receive inheritance within reasonable time per statute
  • Right to legal representation: Hire attorney to protect interests (fees may be payable from estate)
  • Right to court supervision: Request court intervention if executor isn't properly administering estate

Costs of Probate Litigation

Probate litigation can be expensive. Will contests and executor removal proceedings typically cost:

  • Simple contests: $15,000-$50,000 in attorney fees and costs
  • Complex litigation: $50,000-$150,000+ for trials with expert witnesses
  • Trial preparation: Depositions, discovery, expert witnesses (forensic accountants, handwriting experts, medical experts)
  • Court costs: Filing fees, deposition transcripts, court reporter fees

Who pays for probate litigation? Generally, each party pays their own attorney fees unless the court orders otherwise. Under Probate Code Â§ 11003, the court may award attorney fees from the estate to beneficiaries who successfully challenge executor misconduct. If you lose a frivolous contest, you may be ordered to pay the estate's legal costs.

Many probate disputes settle through mediation before trial. Settlement negotiations can resolve conflicts while avoiding the time and expense of litigation. Experienced probate litigation attorneys often achieve favorable settlements that address beneficiaries' concerns without the uncertainty and cost of trial.

Asset-Specific Probate Guidance in California

How to handle different types of assets through California probate

Different types of assets require specific procedures during probate. Understanding how to properly handle real estate, vehicles, bank accounts, and other property ensures compliance with California law and efficient estate administration. Here's your comprehensive guide to managing each asset type through probate.

Probate Real Estate in California

Real property is often the most valuable probate asset and requires careful handling under California law.

Selling a House in Probate California

To sell real estate during probate:

  1. Obtain Letters Testamentary: You need court authority before listing property
  2. Get property appraised: Probate referee provides date-of-death value for inventory
  3. Hire real estate agent: List property at market value (appraisal + market conditions)
  4. Accept purchase offer: Negotiate terms with buyer
  5. File petition for sale: Form DE-260 requesting court approval (unless you have IAEA authority)
  6. Provide notice: Send 15-day notice to beneficiaries of proposed sale
  7. Court confirmation hearing: Judge reviews sale terms and approves if in estate's best interest
  8. Overbid process: At hearing, others can overbid by 10% (rare but possible)
  9. Close escrow: Complete sale after court order becomes final (typically 10 days)
  10. Deposit proceeds: Place funds in estate bank account

IAEA Advantage: With Independent Administration authority, you can sell real property after giving 15-day notice to beneficiaries without court confirmation. This saves 4-6 weeks and eliminates the overbid risk.

Transfer House After Death California

To transfer real property to beneficiaries without selling:

  1. Include property in petition for final distribution
  2. Obtain court order approving distribution to specific beneficiary
  3. Prepare Executor's Deed (transfer deed from estate to beneficiary)
  4. Record deed with Los Angeles County Recorder
  5. File Preliminary Change of Ownership Report with Assessor
  6. Beneficiary assumes responsibility for property taxes, insurance, maintenance

Property tax considerations: Under Proposition 19 (effective Feb 2021), transfers to children no longer receive automatic property tax reassessment exclusion except for primary residences (with value limits) and family farms. Beneficiaries should consult with tax advisors about potential reassessment.

Transfer Car Title After Death California

California has simplified procedures for transferring vehicle titles after death:

For Small Estates (Under $184,500):

Use DMV Form REG 5 (Affidavit for Transfer Without Probate). Requirements:

  • Wait 40 days after death
  • Complete Form REG 5
  • Provide certified death certificate
  • Show you're entitled to vehicle under will or intestacy
  • Submit to DMV with title transfer fee ($15)

For Probate Estates:

  • Submit certified copy of Letters Testamentary
  • Complete standard title transfer paperwork
  • Pay transfer fees and applicable taxes
  • Executor signs as "Executor of Estate of [Name]"

Bank Accounts for Deceased Person California

Bank accounts require different procedures depending on ownership type:

  • Joint accounts with right of survivorship: Pass automatically to surviving account holder - no probate needed. Present death certificate to bank.
  • POD/TOD accounts: Pass to named beneficiary outside probate. Beneficiary presents death certificate and ID to claim funds.
  • Individual accounts (probate estate): Frozen at death. Executor presents Letters Testamentary to access and transfer to estate account.
  • Trust accounts: If properly funded, pass per trust terms without probate. Successor trustee manages distribution.

Important: Banks will freeze individual accounts immediately upon notification of death. Family members cannot access funds even with bills to pay until the executor provides Letters Testamentary. This is why proper estate planning with joint accounts or trusts is crucial.

Personal Property in Probate

Personal property (furniture, jewelry, collectibles, household items) requires:

  • Inventory: List all items with estimated values on Form DE-160
  • Appraisal: Probate referee appraises valuable items (jewelry, art, collectibles over $1,000)
  • Security: Store valuables safely, maintain insurance
  • Distribution: Distribute per will or intestacy after court approval
  • Sale: Sell items if necessary to pay debts (estate sales, auction houses)

Digital Assets in Probate

Under California's Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (Probate Code Â§Â§ 870-884):

  • Email accounts: Request access from providers (Google, Yahoo, etc.) with Letters Testamentary
  • Social media: Request account deletion or memorialization per platform policies
  • Cryptocurrency: Access digital wallets, transfer to estate account, report on inventory
  • Online storage: Access iCloud, Dropbox, Google Drive to retrieve important documents
  • Digital photos/files: Download and preserve for beneficiaries
  • Online accounts: Close subscriptions, recurring charges, memberships

Business Interests in Probate

Business ownership requires special handling:

  • Sole proprietorship: Business assets included in probate estate, executor may continue operations temporarily
  • Partnership: Review partnership agreement for buy-sell provisions and succession terms
  • LLC/Corporation: Transfer membership interests or stock per operating agreement and will
  • Valuation: Hire business appraiser for date-of-death valuation
  • Ongoing operations: May need court approval to continue business during probate

How to File Probate in Los Angeles County

Follow this step-by-step process to file probate at Stanley Mosk Courthouse:

  1. Prepare required documents:
    • Original will (if any)
    • Certified death certificate (original)
    • Form DE-111 (Petition for Probate)
    • Form DE-121 (Notice of Petition to Administer Estate)
    • Form DE-140 (Order for Probate)
    • Proof of publication (after publication complete)
    • Proof of mailing notices to heirs/beneficiaries
  2. File at probate counter: Submit documents at Room 311 (Third Floor), pay filing fees
  3. Obtain hearing date: Clerk assigns hearing date 6-8 weeks out
  4. Publish notice: File proof of publication with court before hearing
  5. Attend hearing: Appear before judge with completed proofs of notice
  6. Obtain Letters: Return to Room 311 to pick up certified Letters Testamentary (usually 1 week after hearing)

LA County Local Probate Rules

Los Angeles County has specific local rules that supplement California Probate Code:

  • Chapter 7 Local Rules: Govern probate proceedings in LA County Superior Court
  • Tentative rulings: Posted online day before hearing at lacourt.org (check before attending)
  • Accounting format: Must follow specific formatting requirements outlined in local rules
  • Publication requirements: Must use LA Daily Journal or Metropolitan News (no other publications accepted)
  • Continuances: Requests must be filed at least 5 court days before hearing with written stipulation or good cause showing
  • Ex parte applications: Must be filed and noticed per local rule requirements
  • Document lodging: Proposed orders must be lodged with clerk after hearing approval

Pro Tip: Always check tentative rulings online the day before your hearing. If the tentative ruling is favorable and no party objects, you may not need to appear in court. However, if you want to argue against a tentative ruling, you must appear and notify the court by 4:00 PM the day before.

Probate Referee Panel

  • What they appraise: Real estate, business interests, stocks, collectibles, vehicles (over certain value)
  • What they don't appraise: Cash, publicly traded securities with known values, personal effects under $500
