Types of Conservatorship in California

California law provides several types of conservatorship to address different situations and needs. As your Glendale conservatorship attorney, I help families determine which type is appropriate for their loved one and guide them through the specific requirements for each.

General Conservatorship

For adults who cannot care for themselves or manage their finances due to age, illness, or injury. Can be a conservatorship of the person (personal care), estate (finances), or both. Most common type for elderly adults with dementia or Alzheimer's disease.

Limited Conservatorship

Designed for adults with developmental disabilities who need some assistance but can still make some decisions independently. Provides only the specific powers needed while preserving the conservatee's independence and rights to the greatest extent possible.

LPS Conservatorship

Named after the Lanterman-Petris-Short Act, this conservatorship is for individuals with serious mental illness who require involuntary mental health treatment. Typically initiated by county mental health departments and requires specific findings about the person's mental condition.

Temporary Conservatorship

Emergency conservatorship established quickly when there's an immediate threat to the proposed conservatee's health, safety, or finances. Provides temporary authority until a permanent conservatorship can be established through the regular court process.

The Conservatorship Process in Glendale

Establishing a conservatorship involves multiple steps and typically takes 2-3 months from filing to appointment. Understanding the process helps families prepare for what to expect and ensures the smoothest possible experience during a difficult time.

Initial Consultation and Case Evaluation

We meet to discuss your loved one's situation, evaluate whether conservatorship is appropriate, and determine which type best fits your needs. We'll explain the process, timeline, and costs so you can make an informed decision.

Petition Preparation and Filing

We prepare the conservatorship petition and all supporting documents, including a capacity declaration from a physician or psychologist. The petition is filed with the Los Angeles Superior Court along with required filing fees (approximately $465).

Notice to Relatives and Interested Parties

California law requires notice to the proposed conservatee, their spouse, close relatives, and certain government agencies. Proper notice must be given at least 15 days before the hearing.

Court Investigation

The court appoints an investigator to visit the proposed conservatee, interview relevant parties, and prepare a report with recommendations for the judge. This investigation helps protect against unnecessary or inappropriate conservatorships.

Court Hearing and Appointment

At the hearing, the judge reviews all evidence, considers any objections, and determines whether to grant the conservatorship. If approved, the conservator takes an oath and receives Letters of Conservatorship authorizing their powers.

Post-Appointment Requirements

After appointment, the conservator must file an inventory of the conservatee's assets, obtain a bond if required, and begin fulfilling their duties. Annual status reports and accountings are required throughout the conservatorship.

Timeline and Costs

A standard conservatorship typically takes 2-3 months from filing to appointment. Emergency or temporary conservatorships can be established more quickly when immediate protection is needed. Costs include court filing fees (approximately $465), attorney fees, court investigator fees, and bond premiums if required. Total costs typically range from $3,000 to $8,000 for uncontested matters, with contested conservatorships costing significantly more.

Conservatorship vs Guardianship

Many people confuse conservatorship and guardianship, but in California, these are distinct legal arrangements for different situations:

Conservatorship (Adults)

Conservatorship applies to adults (age 18+) who cannot care for themselves or manage their finances due to mental illness, developmental disability, age-related conditions, or other incapacity. A conservator is appointed by the court to make decisions for the adult.

Guardianship (Minors)

Guardianship applies to minor children (under 18) whose parents cannot care for them due to death, incapacity, abandonment, or other circumstances. A guardian is appointed to provide care and make decisions for the child until they reach adulthood.

Both conservatorship and guardianship require court approval, ongoing supervision, and regular reporting. The procedures and requirements are similar but governed by different sections of California law. Avoiding Conservatorship Through Advance Planning

While conservatorship is sometimes necessary, proper estate planning can help your family avoid the need for court intervention. A living trust allows a successor trustee to manage assets if you become incapacitated, while a durable power of attorney designates someone to handle financial matters. An advance healthcare directive ensures your medical wishes are followed. We recommend families create these documents before a crisis occurs to protect their loved ones and avoid the expense and complexity of conservatorship proceedings.

Conservatorship FAQs

How much does a conservatorship cost in Glendale?

A conservatorship in Glendale typically costs between $3,000 and $8,000 in attorney fees for uncontested matters, plus court filing fees of approximately $465. Additional costs may include court investigator fees, bond premiums if required by the court, and ongoing accounting fees. Complex or contested conservatorships involving disputes among family members can cost significantly more. During your free consultation, we'll provide a realistic cost estimate based on your specific situation and explain all potential expenses upfront.

How long does it take to get a conservatorship in California?

A standard conservatorship in California typically takes 2-3 months from filing to appointment. The process includes filing the petition, serving notice to relatives (at least 15 days before the hearing), a court investigation by a court-appointed investigator, and a hearing before a Los Angeles Superior Court judge. Emergency or temporary conservatorships can be obtained more quickly, sometimes within days, when there's an immediate risk to the proposed conservatee's health, safety, or finances.

What's the difference between conservatorship and guardianship in California?

In California, conservatorship is for adults (age 18 and older) who cannot care for themselves or manage their finances due to incapacity, while guardianship is for minor children whose parents cannot care for them. Both require court approval and ongoing supervision. A conservator manages an adult's personal care and/or finances, while a guardian has similar responsibilities for a minor child. The legal procedures are similar but governed by different code sections. Our firm handles both conservatorship and guardianship matters throughout Glendale and Los Angeles County.

Can I be appointed conservator if I live out of state?

Yes, California law allows out-of-state residents to serve as conservators, though you may need to appoint a local agent for service of process and demonstrate your ability to fulfill your duties despite the distance. The court will consider factors like your relationship to the conservatee, your ability to make decisions in their best interest, and your plan for managing their care from afar.

What powers does a conservator have in California?

A conservator of the person can make decisions about the conservatee's living arrangements, medical care, food, clothing, and personal care. A conservator of the estate manages finances, pays bills, invests assets, and handles property. The specific powers granted depend on the type of conservatorship and what the court orders. All actions must be in the conservatee's best interest.

Can a conservatorship be terminated or modified?

Yes, conservatorships can be terminated if the conservatee regains capacity, or modified if circumstances change. The conservatee, conservator, or interested parties can petition the court for termination or modification. The court will review evidence and may order an evaluation before making changes. Our firm assists with conservatorship modifications and terminations.
