One of the first questions families ask when a loved one passes away is: "How long will probate take?" As a Glendale probate attorney who has guided hundreds of families through the California probate process, I understand why this matters. Your family needs access to funds, wants to sell real estate, and deserves closure during an already difficult time.

The honest answer? California probate typically takes 9 to 18 months for straightforward cases, but can stretch to 2 years or longer for complex estates. The timeline depends on numerous factors—some within your control, others dictated by court schedules and mandatory waiting periods.

In this comprehensive guide, I'll walk you through the entire California probate timeline, explain what affects the duration, identify unavoidable delays, and share strategies to expedite the process whenever possible.

## The Quick Answer: Typical California Probate Timeline

**Minimum Timeline:** 9-12 months for the simplest estates
**Average Timeline:** 12-18 months for most estates
**Complex Cases:** 18-36+ months

**Why the minimum is so long:** California law mandates certain waiting periods that cannot be shortened, even in the simplest cases. The creditor claim period alone requires 4 months from the date Letters are issued.

## Understanding California's Mandatory Waiting Periods

Before diving into the detailed timeline, you need to understand that California Probate Code imposes mandatory waiting periods that cannot be waived or shortened, regardless of how efficient your attorney is or how cooperative your family members are.

### The 4-Month Creditor Claim Period

**California Probate Code Section 9100**

Once the executor is appointed and publishes notice to creditors, the court requires a minimum 4-month waiting period before the estate can be closed. This allows creditors to file claims against the estate.

**The clock starts:** When Letters Testamentary are issued
**Creditors have:** The later of either 4 months after Letters are issued OR 60 days after receiving actual notice
**Cannot be shortened:** Even if you know there are no creditors

**Real-world impact:** This single requirement means no California probate can close in less than 4-5 months, even if everything else moves quickly.

### The 120-Day Will Contest Period

**California Probate Code Section 8200**

After the will is admitted to probate, interested parties have 120 days to contest its validity.

**The clock starts:** When the will is admitted to probate
**Contest period:** 120 days
**Cannot be waived:** Even if all beneficiaries agree

**Practical note:** Most will contests are filed early in this period if they're going to be filed at all. If you reach day 100 without a contest, you can usually breathe easier.

### Notice and Hearing Requirements

California requires specific notice periods before various probate hearings:

**Initial Petition for Probate:** 15 days' notice to all heirs and beneficiaries
**Petition to Approve Sale of Real Property:** 15 days' notice
**Final Petition for Distribution:** 15 days' notice

These notice requirements add weeks to the timeline at multiple stages.

## Month-by-Month California Probate Timeline

Let me walk you through a typical probate case from start to finish, based on an estate with a will, a house to sell, and no disputes.

### Month 1: Initial Filing and Preparation

**Week 1-2: Gather Documents and Information**
- Obtain certified death certificate (10 copies recommended)
- Locate the original will
- Identify all assets and their values
- Compile list of heirs and beneficiaries
- Gather decedent's financial records

**Week 3-4: Prepare and File Petition**
- Attorney prepares Petition for Probate (Form DE-111)
- Prepare proposed order (Form DE-131)
- File petition with probate court
- Pay filing fees ($435-$500 in Los Angeles County)
- Schedule initial hearing date (usually 6-8 weeks out)

**Time elapsed:** 1 month

### Month 2-3: Notice and First Hearing

**Immediately After Filing:**
- Mail notice of petition to all heirs and beneficiaries (15-day requirement)
- Publish notice in local newspaper (three consecutive weeks)
- File proof of service and publication with court

**6-8 Weeks After Filing: First Hearing**
- Attend hearing on Petition for Probate
- Court admits will to probate (if valid)
- Court appoints executor
- Executor takes oath
- Court issues Letters Testamentary

**Critical milestone:** Once you have Letters Testamentary, you can start accessing assets, paying bills, and managing the estate.

**Time elapsed:** 2-3 months

### Month 3-7: Estate Administration

**Within 60 Days of Appointment:**
- Mail Notice of Administration to all known creditors (Probate Code §9050)
- File copy of published notice with court
- Open estate bank account
- Notify financial institutions
- Transfer stocks, bonds, and securities to estate

**Within 4 Months of Appointment:**
- Complete Inventory and Appraisal (Form DE-160)
- Hire probate referee for formal appraisals
- File Inventory and Appraisal with court

**Ongoing During This Period:**
- Pay ongoing expenses (mortgage, insurance, utilities)
- Maintain property
- Respond to creditor claims
- File decedent's final income tax returns
- Manage estate investments

**If Selling Real Estate:**
- Hire real estate agent
- List property for sale
- Accept offer
- Prepare Petition for Sale of Real Property
- Obtain court confirmation (adds 4-6 weeks)

**Time elapsed:** 7 months

### Month 7-9: Creditor Claim Deadline Passes

**After 4-Month Creditor Period:**
- Allow an additional 30 days for late claims
- Respond to any creditor claims filed
- File objections to improper claims if necessary
- Pay valid creditor claims from estate funds

**120-Day Will Contest Deadline:**
- Monitor for will contests (unlikely after 90 days)
- Resolve any contests filed (adds 6-18 months if contested)

**Ongoing:**
- Continue managing estate assets
- Complete real estate sale (if applicable)
- Liquidate assets as needed

**Time elapsed:** 9 months

### Month 9-12: Prepare for Final Distribution

**Estate Accounting:**
- Prepare final accounting of all receipts and disbursements
- Account for every dollar in and out of estate
- Calculate statutory executor and attorney fees
- Obtain receipts from beneficiaries for any advances

**Tax Returns:**
- File estate tax return (Form 706) if required (estates over $13.61M in 2024)
- File estate income tax return (Form 1041)
- File decedent's final personal income tax returns (federal and state)
- Obtain tax clearances

**Prepare Final Petition:**
- Draft Petition for Final Distribution (Form DE-140)
- Attach final accounting
- Calculate and request statutory fees
- Propose distribution plan

**Time elapsed:** 12 months

### Month 12-15: Final Hearing and Distribution

**File Final Petition:**
- File Petition for Final Distribution with court
- Schedule final hearing (6-8 weeks out)
- Mail 15-day notice to all beneficiaries
- Publish notice if required

**Final Hearing:**
- Attend hearing on Final Petition
- Court reviews and approves accounting
- Court approves statutory fees
- Court orders final distribution
- Court discharges executor

**After Final Order:**
- Distribute assets to beneficiaries
- Obtain signed receipts
- Close estate bank account
- File final papers with court

**Time elapsed:** 15-18 months

**Case Closed**

## What Affects How Long Probate Takes?

While the mandatory waiting periods set a minimum timeline, numerous factors can extend probate significantly.

### Factors That Speed Up Probate

**1. Well-Organized Estate Documents**
When the deceased kept excellent records, we can locate assets, identify creditors, and prepare necessary documents much faster.

**2. Independent Administration Authority**
California Probate Code §10400 allows executors to act without court approval for most actions. This eliminates months of waiting for court hearings.

**3. Liquid Assets**
Estates with bank accounts and brokerage accounts are much easier to administer than estates requiring real estate sales or business valuations.

**4. No Real Estate Sales**
Selling property through probate requires court confirmation and adds 2-4 months to the timeline.

**5. Cooperative Beneficiaries**
When family members agree on the distribution plan and trust the executor, the process moves smoothly.

**6. Experienced Probate Attorney**
An attorney who knows the local court's procedures and requirements prevents delays caused by incorrect paperwork or missed deadlines.

**7. Efficient Local Court**
Los Angeles County's probate courts (particularly Stanley Mosk Courthouse) have backlogs that affect hearing dates. Smaller counties often schedule hearings more quickly.

### Factors That Slow Down Probate

**1. Will Contests**
Challenging the validity of the will can add 6-24 months to probate. Litigation over undue influence, lack of capacity, or fraud requires extensive discovery, motions, and potentially a trial.

**2. Family Disputes**
Even without formal will contests, disagreements between beneficiaries create delays. Disputes over:
- Executor's actions
- Asset valuations
- Distribution plans
- Executor compensation
- Attorney fees

**3. Difficult-to-Value Assets**
Business interests, intellectual property, art collections, and unique real estate require professional appraisals that take time to complete.

**4. Real Estate Sales**
Selling property through probate adds significant time:
- Listing and marketing: 1-3 months
- Court approval process: 4-6 weeks
- Escrow closing: 30-45 days
- Total added time: 3-5 months

**5. Creditor Issues**
Disputed creditor claims require court hearings and can delay final distribution by 3-6 months.

**6. Tax Complications**
Estates requiring federal estate tax returns (Form 706) face IRS review that can take 6-12 months. State tax issues add additional delays.

**7. Missing Heirs or Beneficiaries**
When we can't locate beneficiaries named in the will, we must conduct extensive searches and potentially post bonds for missing heirs.

**8. Out-of-State Property**
Real estate located outside California requires "ancillary probate" in that state, essentially running two probate cases simultaneously.

**9. Insufficient Estate Funds**
When the estate lacks liquid assets to pay debts and expenses, we must sell assets, which takes additional time.

**10. Court Backlogs**
During the pandemic, Los Angeles County probate courts faced massive backlogs. While improving, hearing dates are still scheduled 6-10 weeks out.

Los Angeles County has local rules that can affect timing:

**Rule 4.116: Required Information**
LA County requires more detailed information in probate petitions than some counties, which can delay filing if information is incomplete.

**Rule 4.117: Appraisals**
LA County is strict about appraisal requirements and may reject inventory appraisals that don't comply fully.

## How to Speed Up California Probate

While you cannot eliminate mandatory waiting periods, you can minimize delays with these strategies:

### Strategy #1: Request Independent Administration

**California Probate Code §§10400-10592**

Request "Independent Administration of Estates Act" (IAEA) authority in your initial petition. This allows the executor to:
- Sell real property without court confirmation (with exceptions)
- Distribute assets without court approval
- Settle claims without court hearing

**Time saved:** 2-4 months by eliminating multiple court hearings

**Requirement:** Will must not prohibit it, and beneficiaries must consent or not object.

### Strategy #2: Prepare Documents in Advance

Don't wait for the first hearing to start gathering information. Begin immediately:

- Order 10 certified death certificates
- Locate all financial account statements
- Identify all real property
- Create list of potential creditors
- Compile contact information for all heirs

**Time saved:** 2-4 weeks on the front end

### Strategy #3: Hire an Experienced Local Probate Attorney

An attorney who regularly practices in Los Angeles County probate courts knows:
- Exactly what information each judge requires
- How to prepare bulletproof petitions that are approved on first submission
- Which court clerks to work with for expedited processing
- How to avoid common pitfalls that create delays

**Time saved:** 1-3 months by avoiding rejected filings and continuances

### Strategy #4: Communicate Proactively with Beneficiaries

Keep beneficiaries informed throughout the process. Most disputes arise from lack of communication. Send regular updates:
- Monthly status reports
- Explanation of delays
- Timeline expectations
- Copies of court orders

**Time saved:** Eliminates objections that delay hearings

### Strategy #5: Get Professional Appraisals Quickly

Don't wait for the court-appointed probate referee. Hire appraisers immediately for:
- Real property
- Business interests
- Collectibles and personal property
- Unusual assets

Have appraisals ready when the referee needs them.

**Time saved:** 2-4 weeks in completing Inventory and Appraisal

### Strategy #6: Start Tax Returns Early

Don't wait until the end of probate to think about taxes. File required returns as soon as information is available:
- Decedent's final personal income tax returns (due April 15)
- Estate income tax returns (Form 1041)
- Estate tax return if required (Form 706, due 9 months after death)

**Time saved:** 2-3 months at the end of probate

### Strategy #7: Maintain Estate Property

Keep estate property in excellent condition:
- Pay mortgages on time
- Maintain insurance coverage
- Complete necessary repairs
- Keep utilities active

Property that falls into disrepair creates additional work and delays, especially if real estate sales are needed.

**Time saved:** Avoids complications that add weeks or months

## When Probate Takes Longer Than Expected

If your probate case is exceeding typical timelines, here are common reasons and solutions:

### Issue: Court Keeps Continuing Hearings

**Causes:**
- Incomplete petition
- Missing required documents
- Insufficient notice to beneficiaries
- Judge needs additional information

**Solution:** Work with your attorney to ensure every petition is complete and includes all required attachments. Review local rules before filing.

### Issue: Probate Referee Delays

**Causes:**
- Overwhelming caseload
- Difficulty accessing property
- Waiting for professional appraisals

**Solution:** Communicate proactively with the referee. Provide appraisals and access immediately when requested.

### Issue: Beneficiaries Won't Cooperate

**Causes:**
- Disputes over distribution
- Distrust of executor
- Lack of communication

**Solution:** Consider mediation. Often a neutral third party can resolve disputes in weeks rather than months of litigation.

### Issue: Creditor Won't Accept Payment Offer

**Causes:**
- Creditor disputes amount owed
- Creditor claims secured interest
- Estate claims invalid debt

**Solution:** File objection to creditor claim and request court hearing. Get resolution through court order.

## Probate vs. Trust Administration Timeline Comparison

To understand why many California families use living trusts to avoid probate, compare these timelines:

**Probate Administration:**
- Minimum: 9-12 months

**Trust Administration:**
- Minimum: 4-6 weeks
- Typical: 2-4 months

The difference is dramatic. Properly funded living trusts allow your family to receive their inheritance in weeks instead of over a year.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Probate Timeline

### Can we speed up probate by paying extra fees?

No. California's mandatory waiting periods cannot be shortened regardless of how much money you're willing to pay. However, hiring an experienced attorney and being highly organized can eliminate unnecessary delays.

### What if we need money from the estate before probate is finished?

The executor can request court approval to make preliminary distributions to beneficiaries if the estate has sufficient funds to cover all debts and expenses. This requires a petition and court hearing, adding 6-8 weeks to get approval.

### Does having a will make probate faster?

Having a will doesn't speed up probate, but it does make the process smoother because your wishes are clear. Estates without wills (intestate) face the same timeline but with additional complications determining who inherits.

### Can the executor be removed if probate is taking too long?

Yes, but only if the executor is failing in their duties. Normal delays caused by court backlogs or mandatory waiting periods are not grounds for removal. However, if an executor is actively delaying the process, neglecting duties, or mismanaging assets, beneficiaries can petition for removal.

### What happens if the executor dies during probate?

If the executor dies during probate, the court must appoint a successor. This adds 2-3 months to the timeline for the appointment process. This is why naming successor executors in your will is important.

## The Bottom Line: Plan Ahead to Avoid the Wait

The most effective way to speed up probate is to avoid it entirely through proper estate planning. A properly funded revocable living trust allows your family to receive their inheritance in weeks instead of waiting 12-18 months for probate to conclude.

Even if you're currently in probate, understanding the timeline helps you set realistic expectations and identify areas where you can minimize delays.
